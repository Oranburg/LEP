# What Nehemiah Knew: A Jewish Lawyer Reads the Pope's Encyclical on Artificial Intelligence

*Seth Oranburg*

*Draft 1 — May 27, 2026. Tikvah / Mosaic / Sapir register. Not for circulation.*

---

## I. The Nehemiah Asymmetry

When Pope Leo XIV chose Nehemiah as the governing image for his encyclical on artificial intelligence, he made a move that the Jewish reader notices before the Catholic one does. We have been inside the book of Nehemiah for centuries, and the Talmud quotes it freely for historical data and halakhic propositions; but we have never, as a tradition, developed Nehemiah 2-6 as a governance parable the way we have developed the Tower of Babel. The Pope has done something we have not: he has read our texts with a purpose. That is not a criticism. It is an invitation, and this essay is an attempt to accept it from the inside out.

*Magnifica Humanitas* (Leo XIV, 2026) opens by setting two Hebrew Bible images against each other as the organizing contrast of the entire document. Babel (Gen 11:1-9) is the city built without God: one language, one technology, one direction, pride substituted for blessing, uniformity where there should be communion. Jerusalem under Nehemiah (Neh 2-6) is the city rebuilt from ruin by distributed labor: the wall-section assigned to each family, leadership exercised through convocation, shared responsibility rather than command from above. "The primary choice is not between a 'yes' or 'no' to technology," Leo XIV writes at ¶ 9, "but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem." Every major analytical move in the encyclical follows from that premise.

The Jewish reader arrives at this framing with something the Catholic tradition does not carry on the inside. On Babel we are loaded: Rashi, Ramban, Bereshit Rabbah 38, Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 24, Yalkut Shimoni, generations of midrash treating the tower as a moral-theological parable about idolatry, hubris, and the corruption of language. On Nehemiah the classical Jewish interior is comparatively thin. The commentators on Nehemiah 2-6 work the text conscientiously: Rashi glosses individual phrases, Malbim offers socially attentive readings, Metzudat David and Metzudat Zion cover the plain sense throughout. But they are doing philological and historical work, not developing the rebuilding of Jerusalem's walls as a political theology or a governance parable. The Talmud's interest in Nehemiah is canonical (Bava Batra 15a: who wrote the book, and in what relation to Ezra?) and chronological (Rosh Hashanah 3b: what year of Artaxerxes does Nehemiah's mission correspond to?). No Talmudic passage treats the wall-building as a model of communal leadership or distributed governance under exile. No midrash was located, across Sefaria's index, developing the debt-relief decree of Nehemiah 5, the shared-labor architecture of Nehemiah 3, or the night survey of Nehemiah 2 as templates for institutional design.[^1]

The asymmetry has a practical consequence for this essay. Leo XIV is doing something the Jewish tradition itself does not quite do with Nehemiah. He reads the book as a governance text; the classical exegetes read it as a historical text. The essay that takes the papal invitation seriously is not receiving a pre-built interior so much as receiving an invitation to construct one. That construction is the intellectual work of the next several thousand words.

One classical exception deserves a blockquote. The Malbim on Nehemiah 5:1 comes the closest to social-institutional commentary in the entire classical corpus on this section of the book:

> זעקת דלת העם היתה רבה על העשירים ומפרש שבין הזועקים היו שתי כתות
>
> "The cry of the common people against the wealthy was great — and he explains that among the protesters there were two classes."

The Malbim here tracks the distinction between poor workers who have pledged their fields and children against debt and wealthy creditors who hold those pledges. Nehemiah's response is to convene the nobles and rulers, demand the release, and formalize it through an oath (Neh 5:12-13). The Malbim's exegesis stays at the level of sociological observation in service of plain-sense meaning; it does not develop into a theory of how leaders should handle economic exploitation during collective projects. But it shows that the materials for a governance reading were present in the text all along. What Jewish exegesis preserved as historical fact, Leo XIV deploys as institutional prescription.

The road Leo XIV has paved in institutional brick has been walked, partially, by a small set of modern interpreters. Yuval Levin's "Nehemiah Option," articulated in a 2024 American Enterprise Institute op-ed and a Tikvah Ideas lecture, reads Nehemiah 4:17 (the builders who held tools in one hand and weapons in the other) as a civic-renewal paradigm for contemporary American institutions, the most pointed recent use of the text for governance purposes.[^2] Dov Zakheim's *Nehemiah: Statesman and Sage* (Maggid, 2016) offers the most sustained Jewish scholarly governance reading of the full book, treating Nehemiah's leadership under foreign rule as a model for political-administrative competence in service of communal restoration. These are notable modern voices that build toward what classical exegesis did not develop. They are not the only ones, and others may emerge as the field engages this question more seriously.

## II. When Power Has No Sovereign: *Dina D'Malkhuta* and the AI Platform Problem

The encyclical's most original structural observation is not about technology; it is about sovereignty. At ¶ 5, Leo XIV names the problem with precision: "Today, however, the main drivers of development are private, often transnational, parties that are endowed with resources and the capacity to intervene that surpass those of many Governments. Technological power thus takes on an unprecedented, predominantly 'private' aspect, which makes it even more challenging to discern, govern and direct such power toward the common good."

This is not a regulation gap. States can regulate many things they cannot match in technical capacity. The structural problem Leo XIV is identifying is more fundamental: the entity with the power is not accountable to any *demos*, cannot be replaced by election, does not face the loss of legitimacy that constrains a sovereign, and operates across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously so that no single legal system can reach it. The word Leo XIV is circling around is sovereignty. He does not use it; the encyclical's grammar is subsidiarity. But the diagnosis is of a sovereignty failure.

The halakhic tradition's most sophisticated resource here is not a prohibition but a premise. דינא דמלכותא דינא (*dina d'malkhuta dina*), the law of the kingdom is law, is stated without elaboration at Bava Kamma 113a-b and developed further at Bava Batra 54b-55a. Maimonides codifies the doctrine and extends it: the law of the sovereign is binding because the sovereign has a recognized authority within a defined jurisdiction, and subjects derive practical benefit from that authority (*Mishneh Torah, Hil. Gezelah va-Aveidah* 5:11-18). The doctrine has real teeth: it can override rabbinic enactments in commercial matters, validate acquisitions under secular law that would be invalid under Torah law, and bind Jews in their dealings with non-Jewish legal systems.

But notice what the doctrine presupposes. There must be a *malkhuta*: a governing authority with jurisdiction over a defined territory and population, capable of enforcing its enactments and accountable (at minimum) to the reality that its subjects could flee or revolt if the burden became intolerable. A private company headquartered in one jurisdiction, operating globally through servers distributed across many others, with no citizenship, no territory, no elections, and no exit mechanism for those who depend on its infrastructure is not a *malkhuta* in any functional sense the halakhic doctrine contemplates. The doctrine's premise fails before you reach its rule.

This matters for AI governance because the natural move, in halakhic reasoning and in American administrative law alike, is to ask which authority's rule governs this transaction. When the answer is "no authority with the relevant capacity," you have exposed a gap that neither legal system was designed to address. Leo XIV's ¶ 71 is precise on this point: "The principle of subsidiarity requires that such processes not be imposed from above in an opaque and unilateral manner, but instead be directed toward the common good with transparency, accountability and meaningful forms of participation." The encyclical's subsidiarity response is the positive prescription the *dina d'malkhuta* doctrine leaves open when its premise fails.

The *kehilla* governance architecture suggests what subsidiarity looks like in practice when the state cannot reach. The classical Jewish community in the Diaspora allocated distinct competencies to distinct institutions: the *bet din* for adjudication, the *chevra kadisha* for burial, the *gemach* for interest-free loans, the communal board for taxation and representation before the sovereign authority. Each institution operated within its sphere; none could absorb the others' functions without disrupting the whole. The king's institutional limits in Deut 17:14-20 supply the Torah's top-down constraint: the king may not accumulate horses, wives, or gold beyond measure, and he must keep a copy of the Torah with him and read it all the days of his life, so that "his heart will not be exalted above his brothers." The constraint is on concentration, which is both a moral and a structural point. Power that concentrates without limit becomes unable to hear the wall-builders' cry.

Leo XIV's ¶ 108 puts the same point from the bottom up: "Communities and intermediary organizations must not be reduced to passive recipients of decisions made elsewhere; they must be able to contribute to discernment and oversight. Moreover, ownership of data cannot be left solely in private hands but must be appropriately regulated. Data is the product of many contributors and should not be treated as something to be sold off or entrusted to a select few." The *kehilla* model is not merely an analogy here; it is a proof of concept. Distributed, accountable, layered governance of shared resources is achievable. The Jewish community built it under conditions far more hostile than anything today's AI governance debates present.

Marian Tupy, writing in the *Wall Street Journal* the day this essay was drafted, offers the clean libertarian rejoinder.[^3] If Jeff Bezos's $275 billion fortune implies, under Nordhaus's 2% innovator-capture rate, roughly $13.8 trillion in consumer surplus and 22 hours saved per Amazon customer per year, then by the arithmetic that matters most — time, which is "our most precious resource" — the case for Bezos's wealth is unassailable. The arithmetic is not wrong. The four minutes per day saved is probably a conservative estimate for most active Amazon users. The essay concedes this without hesitation. What the ledger cannot count is whether those 22 hours were freed into genuine human flourishing or simply recaptured by the same platform through attention-capture, addiction design, and algorithmic dependency. The encyclical asks a question Tupy's arithmetic was not designed to answer: were the hours saved placed at the worker's disposal, or at Amazon's? *Rerum Novarum* (Leo XIII, 1891) asked the same question about the industrial mill. The answer then was: the hours saved by the machine were not returned to the worker as freedom; they were returned to the owner as margin. Leo XIV's encyclical is *Rerum Novarum* for the platform age, and the *dina d'malkhuta* problem is what you get when the mill has no sovereign capable of requiring otherwise.

## III. Babel as Diagnosis: Transhumanism and the Brick-Over-Person Error

The encyclical names the ideological adversary with unusual specificity. At ¶¶ 115-117, Leo XIV addresses transhumanism and posthumanism as "the ideological background present in some centers of technological power," characterized by a "futuristic vision of an 'enhanced human being' or 'human-machine hybrid.'" The posthumanist project, in its more radical form, "challenges anthropocentrism and envisions a hybridization of human beings, machines and the environment, even anticipating a threshold where humanity surpasses itself in a new evolutionary stage." At ¶ 174, the encyclical returns to condemn "all forms of slavery, trafficking and the commodification of persons," naming these as the practical consequences when the transhumanist logic reaches supply chains and labor markets. The connection between the utopian aspiration (the enhanced human) and the dystopian reality (the enslaved worker producing the devices) is the encyclical's most powerful analytical move.

The midrash saw it first. Rabbi Pinhas, in Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer, gives the following account of the Tower of Babel's construction:

> רַבִּי פִּנְחָס אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיוּ שָׁם אֲבָנִים לִבְנוֹת אֶת הָעִיר וְאֶת הַמִּגְדָּל. מָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין? הָיוּ מְלַבְּנִים לְבֵנִים וְשׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתָן כְּיוֹצֵר חֶרֶשׂ... וְאִם נָפַל אָדָם וָמֵת, לֹא שָׂמוּ אֶת לִבָּם עָלָיו. וְאִם נָפְלָה לְבֵנָה אַחַת, הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין וּבוֹכִין וְאוֹמְרִין: ״אוֹי לָנוּ! אֵימָתַי תַּעֲלֶה אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ?״
>
> Rabbi Pinhas says: There were no stones there with which to build the city and the tower. What did they do? They molded bricks and fired them like a potter... If a man fell and died, they paid him no heed. But if one brick fell, they would sit and weep, saying: "Woe to us! When will another one come up in its place?"

*(Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 24:6)*

Rabbi Pinhas states what Leo XIV is diagnosing: the Babel builders had inverted the moral hierarchy. The brick was mourned; the person was not. The project had swallowed its participants. The technology had become the end; the human had become the input. This is precisely what the encyclical calls the "Babel syndrome" (¶ 10): "the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance."

The transhumanism critique and the midrash are the same observation across millennia, which is itself an argument for the natural-law reading the encyclical proposes: some moral insights are available to human reason without revelation, confirmed by traditions that arrived at them independently.

The Jewish law category most precisely responsive to this critique is איסורי הנאה (*issurei hana'ah*), the class of things from which benefit may not be derived (Pesachim 21a-25b; *Mishneh Torah, Hil. Ma'akhalot Asurot* 8). These are goods that the Torah removes from the commodity form entirely, not because they have no value but because their value exceeds the market form. But the human person is the paradigm of a different category: Lev 25:39-43 forbids the permanent reduction of an Israelite to fungible labor. "You shall not rule over him with rigor." The prohibition is absolute and non-waivable; it does not yield to consumer-surplus arithmetic. The human capacity for self-determination is exactly the kind of value that *issurei hana'ah* thinking holds outside the market form, not as a market failure to be corrected, but as a categorical exclusion that the market form is structurally incapable of honoring.

Aquinas reaches the same place from a different direction. At *ST* II-II Q.77 a.4, he asks whether goods can be sold at higher prices to those who need them urgently. The answer is no: the just price is determined by the good itself, not by the buyer's vulnerability. The person's dignity is not a variable input to the price of labor; it is a floor below which no transaction can go without moral failure. Michael Sandel has named this problem correctly in *The Case Against Perfection* (2007): there are capacities and goods that are corrupted, not merely priced, when subjected to market logic.[^4] What neither Sandel nor the transhumanists can supply is the tradition-grounded account of why this is so. The *issurei hana'ah* category and the *justum pretium* doctrine together provide it: some goods are constitutively outside commerce because their value is incommensurable with commercial exchange, not merely very high within it.

Leo XIV's "new forms of slavery" (¶ 174) embedded in AI supply chains are the twenty-first-century instance of the brick-over-person inversion. The cobalt miner in the Democratic Republic of Congo who extracts materials for the battery powering a device optimized for human augmentation is the person who fell while carrying bricks. The enhancement project does not weep for him. The market for the device does not price his dignity. The encyclical calls this a failure of "ethical discernment" (¶ 174); the midrash calls it the Babel syndrome; the halakhic tradition calls it עבדות (*avdut*), servitude that the Torah's categorical exemption from permanent reduction was designed to prevent. The three descriptions converge on the same violation.

## IV. Subsidiarity, the *Kehilla*, and the Layered Wall

"No one can single-handedly bear the weight of the challenges the world is facing, just as no one is so weak that they cannot play their part, for 'power is made perfect in weakness'" (¶ 13). This is from the encyclical's introduction, and it is subsidiarity stated as a building-site principle: every person has a section of the wall, and no one person or institution can arrogate the whole. The force of the observation is not egalitarian but architectural. A wall that depends on one builder fails when that builder falls. A wall divided among many families, each accountable for its own section, produces both resilience and participation.

The principle of subsidiarity, as Leo XIV traces its pedigree, was formally articulated by Pius XI in *Quadragesimo Anno* (1931) at ¶¶ 79-80: "It is an injustice and at the same time a grave evil and disturbance of right order to assign to a greater and higher association what lesser and subordinate organizations can do." The formula is negative: it tells you what higher levels may not absorb. The positive content is the *kehilla*'s lived architecture. The *kehilla* organized Jewish communal life in the Diaspora by distributing distinct competencies to distinct institutions: the *bet din* adjudicated, the *chevra kadisha* buried the dead, the *gemach* lent without interest, the synagogue provided for worship. Each institution had its sphere; none could claim competence in another's domain without disrupting the ecology of communal trust. The *kehilla* was subsidiarity in practice before Pius XI had a name for it.

The relevance to AI governance is direct. Leo XIV's ¶ 71 identifies the problem: in the digital economy, "the highest level is not the State, but rather major economic and technological actors that exercise *de facto* power over the conditions of everyday life." The subsidiarity principle, applied to this structure, requires that communities and intermediary organizations not be "reduced to passive recipients of decisions made elsewhere" (¶ 108). That is the wall-section logic again: each community must rebuild its own portion. A global AI governance structure that assigns all decisions to platform companies, with communities left only to consume, is Babel with better public relations.

The king's limits at Deut 17:14-20 supply the Torah's structural constraint on concentrated power from the top. The king "shall not multiply horses to himself," "shall not multiply wives," "shall not greatly multiply silver and gold." Maimonides reads these prohibitions as constituting the monarchy's proper limits: the king may govern, but he may not accumulate without limit (*Mishneh Torah, Hil. Melakhim* 3:1-4). The Talmud's *dina d'malkhuta* doctrine then supplies the bottom-up constraint: the sovereign's law binds because there is a sovereign who has taken on the obligation of governance in exchange for the community's submission. The exchange is bilateral. The governance structure that takes the submission without accepting the obligation of governance is not a *malkhuta* at all.

Leo XIV at ¶ 109 names what subsidiarity affirmatively demands of AI governance: "To speak of subsidiarity calls for protecting the ability of communities to make choices and corrections, rather than confining their role to mere oversight after the standards have been set elsewhere." The *kehilla* did not exercise merely nominal oversight over communal tax rates, burial procedures, or adjudication of commercial disputes; it exercised real governance of those domains, with the *bet din* as a fully functional court and the communal enactment (*cherem*) as a binding instrument. The model is not representation; it is competence.

Connection to Seth's LotF (Law of the Firm): the firm as institution, the assignment of discrete competencies within a governance structure, and the subsidiarity principle map onto corporate governance exactly. The Nehemiah model is teaching material for why every section of the wall must have an owner: when everyone owns the whole wall, no one has responsibility for any section, and the wall is not rebuilt. Coase asked why firms exist; the *kehilla* model asks what conditions make the firm a carrier of human flourishing rather than merely an efficient allocation of resources.[^5] Leo XIV's encyclical suggests that the answer is the same one the *kehilla* practiced: defined competencies, distributed responsibility, accountability upward but not micromanagement downward, and a recognition that the institution serves persons who cannot be reduced to inputs.

The connection to Seth's AotV (*Architects of the Void*) is equally direct. Leo XIV calls for a "wise architect" who shapes the digital environment toward the good [VERIFY: encyclical paragraph number]. The entrepreneurial lawyer as sub-creator, in AotV's framing, is precisely this figure: the person who designs the institutional forms within which commerce and governance occur, answerable to a tradition that judges those forms by whether they carry or distort human flourishing.

## V. The Dignity of Work Before the Transaction Is Complete

Leo XIV's ¶ 156 is the most legally specific prescription in the encyclical: "At this time of transition, it is not enough to react only when jobs disappear; we must oversee the transformation in advance. One viable path is, first of all, to establish social criteria for innovation. Here, every introduction of automation and AI should be accompanied by verifiable measures to protect the employment, retraining and participation of workers."

The שכיר (*sachir*) law anticipates this by structure. Lev 19:13 commands: "the wages of a hired worker shall not remain with you overnight until the morning." Deut 24:15 extends the command: "you shall give him his wages on the same day before the sun sets." The rule is ex ante, not remedial. The employer's obligation to the worker attaches before the worker demands payment, and before any regulatory authority intervenes. The Talmud develops the doctrine across both sides of the transaction: the worker who quits in the middle of the day forfeits part of the wage (Bava Metzia 76a-77a), but the employer who withholds the wage at day's end has violated a Biblical prohibition, not merely a civil remedy. Maimonides codifies the worker's right as actionable immediately upon completion of the work (*Mishneh Torah, Hil. Sechirut* 11:1-3). The protection is structural.

That structure is exactly what Leo XIV wants from AI governance at ¶ 156: protection built into the design of the transition, not patched on afterward. The *sachir* law understood that by the time the wage dispute reaches adjudication, the worker has already gone hungry. The law therefore places the obligation at the moment the work is completed, in the ordinary course of business, without requiring the worker to assert a claim. The parallel to "verifiable measures to protect employment" built into the introduction of automation rather than appended to it as a mitigation is not coincidental. Both are responses to the structural power imbalance between the party who controls the resource and the party who depends on it.

Shabbat 31a adds the dimension that is often lost in instrumentalist accounts of labor law. The first question asked of a person at the heavenly tribunal, per the Talmud, is, in Aramaic: נשאת ונתת באמונה (*nasata v'natata b'emunah*) — did you deal faithfully in business? Commercial dealing is the primary arena of covenant obligation, the site where faithfulness is tested under conditions of self-interest and information asymmetry. אמונה (*emunah*) is not merely accuracy; it is trustworthiness in the full moral sense. The worker who shows up is trusting that the wage will be paid. The employer who defers that payment has not merely violated a rule; they have breached a covenant.

*Rerum Novarum* (Leo XIII, 1891) identified the same structural vulnerability in 1891: workers face capital with no protection except their collective power to withhold labor, and the law of wages must recognize that the person behind the labor has a dignity that the market price of labor cannot fully capture. Leo XIV at ¶ 30 identifies the two *Rerum Novarum* insights that "remain highly relevant today": "the primacy of human labor over any mindset focused solely on finance or productivity" and "the inseparable link between proclaiming the Gospel and pursuing a more just social order." *Laborem Exercens* (John Paul II, 1981) at ¶¶ 6-10 extended this into a theology of labor as participation in creation. Leo XIV extends it further into a law of AI transition: the worker has a right to protection *before* the displacement occurs, not only a claim to remediation afterward.

The integralist critics are right that liberalism's institutional framework has failed to sustain the conditions under which the dignity of work can be honored.[^6] They are right about the diagnosis. Where the encyclical parts ways with the integralist response is in the prescription: subsidiarity-plus-multilateralism is Leo XIV's answer (¶ 71: "higher-level institutions must recognize, protect and promote the freedom and creativity of lower-level entities"), not the reconstruction of confessional political authority. For Jewish engagement with natural-law arguments about labor dignity, the distinction matters enormously. The *kehilla* model of communal governance survived for centuries under political systems profoundly hostile to Jewish life; it did not depend on the sovereign sharing its confessional commitments. The subsidiarity framework generalizes in a way that confessional politics cannot.

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks's account of covenantal labor makes the same point from the Jewish side: the commercial covenant is not a private transaction between two parties optimizing their interests, but a relationship of mutual obligation embedded in a community that bears witness to its terms.[^7] The worker's dignity is not a preference to be weighed against the employer's efficiency gains; it is a claim that the community is obligated to enforce. That is why the *sachir* rule is placed in Lev 19:13, the same passage that commands "you shall love your neighbor as yourself" (Lev 19:18). The wage rule and the love command share a verse structure because they share a moral premise.

## VI. Natural Law as Shared Grammar, Not Common Creed

The *Magnificat* says that God has filled the hungry with good things and sent the rich away empty. Leo XIV's encyclical reads that promise as institutional architecture: the hungry are filled not by redistribution alone but by governance structures that protect their dignity before the transaction is complete, that place accountability at every level of the wall, and that refuse to treat any human being as a brick to be placed or discarded by a project larger than itself. Jewish law arrived at the same architecture by a different route, through the *sachir*'s right to his wage at day's end, through the *kehilla*'s layered competencies, through the *issurei hana'ah* that holds certain things outside the market not because they have no value but because their value exceeds the market form. What the two traditions share at this level is not theology. It is grammar: a shared vocabulary for what law must do when power concentrates and persons are forgotten. That grammar is what Jewish lawyers are positioned to bring to the public square, and this encyclical is as good an occasion as any to begin.

The convergences documented across sections I through V are real and they are not coincidental. The Babel-syndrome diagnosis, the subsidiarity prescription, the limits-on-commodification doctrine, and the dignity-of-labor floor are not sectarian propositions imported from one tradition into another. They are publicly deployable natural-law grammar: conclusions available to human reason, confirmed by the convergence of traditions that arrived at them through independent routes. The Jewish reader of *Magnifica Humanitas* is not being asked to accept the encyclical's christological premises. The natural-law layer of the document — everything from the Nehemiah reading through the subsidiarity analysis through the transhumanism critique — is accessible to and confirmable by the Jewish legal tradition without any concession on the theological questions that divide the traditions.

Leo XIV's encyclical closes with three questions that the convergences documented above cannot yet answer from within either tradition alone. They constitute the agenda for the next round of legal scholarship.

First: what governance structures does subsidiarity actually prescribe for AI platforms under current positive law? The encyclical's ¶ 71 analysis requires transparency, accountability, independent checks, algorithmic disclosure, equitable data access, and avenues for recourse. American administrative law can deliver some of these through existing agency authority; others require statutory action; still others may require structural remedies that neither administrative nor antitrust law is currently designed to provide. The halakhic *bet din* model suggests that what is needed is not merely more regulation but a different kind of institution: one with adjudicatory competence, a defined jurisdiction, and authority derived from the community's participation rather than from above.

Second: whether the *dina d'malkhuta dina* doctrine's functional premise can be extended, by analogy or by קל וחומר (*kal va-homer*), to private actors who exercise quasi-sovereign power. The doctrine has always been about functional sovereignty, not formal title. A private company that controls infrastructure on which millions of people depend for economic participation, healthcare access, and civic information is exercising something that looks functionally like sovereign authority over those people's lives. If *dina d'malkhuta dina* follows the function rather than the form, the doctrine may supply not only an obligation to follow the platform's rules but also a framework for demanding that those rules satisfy the reciprocal obligations of governance.

Third: what the *issurei hana'ah* model contributes to the commodification-of-data question that *Sovereign Charities* and *Open Secrets* have both circled. Data about a person's health, preferences, relationships, and locations is not merely a commodity with a high market value; it may be, in the *issurei hana'ah* sense, a category of good that the market form is structurally incapable of honoring without corrupting. If so, the right policy response is not better pricing of personal data but categorical exclusion from commercial exchange, which is a more radical proposal than current data-protection law contemplates.

Michael Walzer in *Spheres of Justice* (1983) argued that different social goods are governed by different principles of distribution, and that the pathology of modern societies is the colonization of one sphere by the logic of another.[^8] Money should buy goods in the market; it should not buy votes, sacred offices, or love. The encyclical's argument is in this family: the human person, the natural environment, and the structures of communal life belong to spheres whose logic is not the logic of market exchange, and AI governance is the current test of whether those sphere boundaries can be maintained. Jewish law has been running this argument for three millennia, through the land-tenure laws of Lev 25, the limits on usury, the structure of *issurei hana'ah*, and the *kehilla*'s insistence that certain communal functions cannot be privatized without destroying their value. Leo XIV has produced the most sophisticated Catholic Social Doctrine statement of the same argument to date.

What Jewish lawyers are positioned to do with this encyclical is neither to endorse its theological premises nor to stand apart from its natural-law analysis. The position is more interesting: to read the encyclical as a fellow practitioner of an overlapping grammar, to identify where the Jewish legal tradition supplies interior resources that the Catholic tradition deploys from outside, and to bring both to bear on the legal questions that neither tradition was designed to answer alone. The Nehemiah asymmetry with which this essay opened is the template. Leo XIV read a Jewish governance text with a purpose that Jewish exegesis had not fully developed. The appropriate response is not to cite the asymmetry as a deficiency in the tradition's self-understanding but to accept the invitation and build.

The wall has sections. This one belongs to us.
