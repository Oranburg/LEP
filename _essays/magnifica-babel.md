# Magnifica Humanitas and the Babel Question

## Opening

Pope Leo XIV's *Magnifica Humanitas: On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*, given at Rome on 15 May 2026, takes Babel and Nehemia as the governing biblical images for the AI age. The opposition is not decorative. It is the most precise doctrinal frame the Church has yet produced for the question of who shares in the upside of an AI venture. The encyclical reads the present industrial moment between two opposed Old Testament structures, names the configuration each represents in commercial life, and applies the older *societas* / *mutuum* distinction to the new technology. The argument that follows works the opposition through in three moves: Babel as the platform structure, Nehemia as the partnership structure, and what Leo XIV is doing when he sets the two against each other. Three contemporary cases test the frame. Two short closing sections name the encyclical's demand on business law and the convergence with the pedagogical question treated in [AI as Havruta](/LEP/essays/ai-as-havruta/).

The claim to be argued is simple. The Babel arrangement and the Nehemian arrangement are not metaphors for AI commerce. They are the two structures AI commerce can actually take. The first one is taken by default. The second one has to be drafted.

## I. Babel as the platform structure

Genesis 11:1 to 9. The plain features of the text are the features of the modern platform. The people are gathered. The speech is unified. The bricks are pooled. The project rises by coordinated labor under coordinated direction. No single laborer owns the tower. The tower's purpose, written into the text, is to make a name for the builders, which is to say to concentrate reputational and reach-based capital at the top of the structure. The wages are paid in pride and in the fear of dispersal. When the dispersal comes, the workers carry no portable claim on what they built. They lose the bricks they fired and the labor they gave.

The structure is older than capitalism and structurally familiar within it. Capital is gathered at one node. Labor is given by many nodes. The product accrues to the gathering node. The dispersal, when it comes, falls on the laboring nodes. The modern platform reproduces the form with technical precision. Capital is gathered into the training run and the data center. Labor is given by authors, annotators, content moderators, and end users whose attention and queries train the next iteration. Data is harvested. Attention is monetized. Returns flow upward to a tower the contributors do not own and cannot reach.

The tower is not built by anyone in particular. It is built by everyone in general, which is to say it is built by no one with standing to claim a share in what it becomes. The structure produces value, and the value is real. The question Leo XIV puts to the structure is the question Genesis already put: when the scattering comes, what does the builder carry away?

The Babel arrangement answers: nothing portable, nothing owned, nothing that survives the dispersal. The arrangement is a *mutuum* in built form. The contributor extends labor and data on terms that promise nothing back except continued participation. The platform retains the residual. The platform bears the structural risk only in the trivial sense that the project might fail; the laborer bears the real risk that the project will succeed without him.

## II. Nehemia as the partnership structure

Nehemiah chapters 3 and 4. The text is unusual in scripture because it reads, on its surface, like a construction schedule. Section by section, the wall of Jerusalem is allocated. The priests rebuild near the Sheep Gate. The goldsmiths and perfumers rebuild near their quarter. The men of Tekoa rebuild the section opposite their houses. The list is long and specific. Its specificity is the doctrinal content.

The allocation is the form. Each builder rebuilds the section adjacent to what that builder owns. The work is divided so that the part of the wall a family raises is the part of the wall that protects the family. The wall rises as a whole because each part rises under a stake. The structure is partnership at shared risk in its built expression: the upside, a defended city, is shared because the downside, the breach of one's own section, is shared. The builder who cuts corners on his section is the builder whose house falls first when the section fails.

This is the form Leo XIV holds up against Babel. It is not utopian. It is operational. The Nehemian wall got built and stood up. The biblical record names the form that made the standing possible, which is not gathered labor under unified speech but distributed labor under distributed stake. The doctrine of partnership at shared risk, traced through the [doctrinal lineage]( /LEP/lineage/ ) from Roman commercial law through Aquinas through Salamanca through the social encyclicals, finds in Nehemiah its earliest scriptural anchor for built work specifically. The Roman *societas* is the legal form. Nehemiah is the construction site.

## III. What Leo XIV is doing with the opposition

*Magnifica Humanitas* §§ 7 and following. The encyclical does not reject artificial intelligence as a technology, and a careful reader should not mistake the Babel passage for that rejection. The encyclical rejects a particular configuration of AI deployment. The configuration it rejects is the one in which the human contributor is rendered as input rather than partner. The configuration it endorses is the one in which the human contributor retains a stake in what the contribution produces.

The doctrinal move is identical to the *societas* / *mutuum* distinction Aquinas worked out at *ST* II-II q. 78 a. 2 ad 5 and the Salamanca theologians elaborated for seven decades after him. A venture is morally appropriate when the contributors share the risk and share the return. A venture is morally inappropriate when one party extracts a fixed return while the other parties bear the residual exposure. Leo XIV applies the older doctrine to the newer instrument. The instrument changes; the test does not. The test reads the configuration and asks where the residual sits. Where the residual sits with the party that supplied the capital and not with the parties that supplied the labor or the data, the configuration is the form the doctrine names as defective.

The encyclical's § 7 sets the frame. § 67 brings the new forms of property, the algorithm, the platform, and the dataset, under the principle of the universal destination of goods. § 163 names the insufficiency of the invisible hand for the AI age, restating the *Centesimus Annus* claim that market coordination is not a moral substitute for moral reasoning. § 173 names the hidden labor chain, the annotators and content moderators and mineral extractors whose work the apparent automation depends on. § 178 names data colonialism, the extraction of value from populations whose data is collected without informed participation in the upside. Read together, the five paragraphs operationalize the Babel and Nehemia framing across the structural points where an AI venture's documents do their work.

## IV. The Babel question applied

Three contemporary configurations illustrate what the test produces in practice.

**Compute and the labor of authorship.** A large language model is trained on the labor of millions of authors and the compute capacity of a small number of providers. The training corpus is the gathered brick. The compute is the gathered capital. The authors are not paid in equity, in royalty, or in any continuing stake. They are paid, on the standard arrangement, in nothing, and the model that results captures the residual. The Nehemian alternative, considered seriously, is some configuration in which the training corpus contributors carry a continuing claim on the model's commercial output. The legal forms for this are not yet settled; a series of lawsuits and a smaller series of contractual experiments are testing the question now. The doctrinal point is prior to the legal one. The configuration that pays the contributor nothing and retains the residual at the provider is the Babel configuration. The configuration that ties the contributor's stake to the model's success is the Nehemian one. The lawyer's question is which configuration the documents she is drafting actually constitute, regardless of which label the parties attach to it.

**Data and the labor of annotation.** A platform whose value depends on user-generated content, click data, or annotated training data has gathered the labor of many contributors. The standard contractual form is the terms-of-service grant, which assigns the platform a perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free license to the user's contribution. The user, in exchange, receives continued access. The grant is presented as consideration, but it is structurally a fee for entry rather than a share in the venture the entry constitutes. § 178 of *Magnifica Humanitas* names this configuration. The Nehemian alternative is a structure in which annotation work or data contribution carries a continuing stake in the platform's value. The cooperative platform, the data dividend, and the data-trust models being explored at the regulatory margins are early sketches of what that structure might be. The point for the present essay is that the test is doctrinal, not political. The structure that concentrates the residual at the platform fails the test regardless of whether the legal label is "terms of service," "consent," or "license."

**AI-assisted ventures and founder labor.** A founder using AI tools to build a venture is, structurally, in a partnership with the AI vendor. The vendor supplies the instrument. The founder supplies the application of the instrument to a particular commercial purpose. The vendor's terms of service determine whether the founder's labor produces value the founder owns or value the vendor captures. Standard terms preserve the founder's ownership of the output. Some terms reserve to the vendor a continuing right to train on the founder's inputs, which is to say the founder pays a hidden fee in the form of contributing to the vendor's residual. The Nehemian frame requires reading these terms with the *societas* / *mutuum* test. Where the founder's contribution produces value the founder owns, the arrangement is partnership-adjacent. Where the founder's contribution produces value the vendor captures, the arrangement is the Babel configuration in miniature, and the founder's lawyer has work to do.

## V. What the encyclical asks of business law

Leo XIV's § 67 brings algorithms, platforms, and data under the universal destination of goods. The principle is old; its application to these forms is new. The application is that ownership of these new forms is not a matter of indifference. A platform that concentrates the residual at the top while consuming the labor of contributors at the bottom is a form of property that fails the doctrine, and the documents that constitute it are documents that constitute a moral configuration as well as a commercial one.

§ 163 names the insufficiency of the invisible hand. The implication for transactional practice is direct. The lawyer who treats market coordination as a substitute for moral reasoning produces deals that work in the short term and fail in the long term. The deals fail not because they violate a rule of law but because they constitute arrangements the underlying anatomy cannot sustain. The terms of service that capture all of the residual; the data-collection consent flow that returns no share of the value generated; the worker-classification structure that strips portable claims from the contributors who built the platform; these are clauses the encyclical reads under the *societas* / *mutuum* test and names accordingly.

The implication for the lawyer is concrete. How a venture's documents treat data ownership, algorithm licensing, platform-access provisions, training-data rights, and contributor compensation matters morally and not only commercially. The lawyer who drafts these clauses without the doctrinal vocabulary is not drafting in a neutral register. She is drafting Babel by default, because the default contractual furniture supplied by the standard AI-platform stack is Babel furniture. The Nehemian alternative requires deliberate drafting against the default. It requires the lawyer to know that the default exists, to know what it constitutes, and to know what the alternatives look like in built form.

## VI. The havruta question, briefly

The same encyclical that frames the capital question (Babel against Nehemia) also frames the pedagogical question (oracular AI use against adversarial pairing). [AI as Havruta](/LEP/essays/ai-as-havruta/) treats the pedagogical side in its own register. The two questions converge at a single point. An AI deployment that strips judgment from the user is also typically a capital structure that strips equity from the user. The user who accepts the oracle's output without challenge is the user who has accepted the platform's terms without negotiation. The two atrophies share a doctrinal source. The pedagogical posture and the capital posture both turn on whether the user remains a partner in what the deployment produces or is reduced to an input for what the platform retains.

The point is not that the two questions are identical. The point is that they fail together when they fail. A pedagogy that forms students into oracle-consumers produces founders who do not know how to read a terms-of-service grant against the *societas* test. A drafting culture that accepts the standard platform furniture produces deals that train the next generation of founders to expect Babel as the natural shape of AI commerce. The encyclical addresses both atrophies because they are atrophies of the same capacity, the capacity for judgment held under one's own stake.

## Closing

Babel scattered the workers because the project was not theirs. Nehemia held the city because each section was. Leo XIV applies the older doctrine to the newest instrument, and the application reads cleanly across compute, data, training corpora, and the founder's own arrangement with the platform the founder builds on. The lawyer's job is to read the instrument through the doctrine and to draft so that the doctrine holds.

The doctrine holds when the residual sits with the parties that bore the risk. It fails when the residual sits with the party that gathered the capital while the labor and the data were given by others. The test is older than the technology. It is older than the corporation. It is older than the joint-stock form. It is older than usury law. It is as old as the wall of Jerusalem and the tower at Shinar, and the encyclical's contribution is to insist that the age of the question does not exempt the present generation of lawyers from answering it.

The instruments are new. The doctrine is not. The drafting is the work.
