# The Deskilling Loop: How Modern Professions Engineer Out Professional Judgment

## The Diagnosis

Something systematic has happened to professional work over the past half-century. It is not that professionals became less intelligent or less trained. By most measures they became more credentialed. The average physician today has more years of formal education than her 1970 counterpart. The average securities lawyer has sat through more mandatory CLE hours than any generation before. Aviation engineers pass more documented certification checkpoints. Financial compliance officers hold more professional designations.

Yet the work product that defines each profession, the clinical judgment, the legal analysis, the engineering sign-off, the risk assessment, has been steadily stripped out of the professional's hands and relocated. Sometimes it migrates to checklists. Sometimes to software. Sometimes to a supervisory layer whose job is to document that judgment was exercised somewhere in the process. The professional remains in the room. The judgment moves elsewhere.

This paper names that process the deskilling loop. The loop has a structure: compliance pressure produces documentation requirements; documentation requirements consume time; time pressure accelerates the adoption of standardized decision protocols; those protocols progressively substitute for the professional's own judgment; reduced exercise of judgment accelerates the atrophying of judgment; atrophied judgment generates more errors or liability exposure; more errors produce more compliance pressure. The loop is closed. Each revolution tightens it.

Each revolution tightens the loop." caption="**The deskilling loop.** Four stages, one cycle, tightening with each revolution." %}

The deskilling loop is not a lament about technological change. Some standardization is a genuine improvement: surgical checklists reduce infection rates; rule-based disclosure requirements protect investors who could not otherwise evaluate a prospectus. The claim is narrower and harder to refute. Modern professional systems have gone past the point where standardization supplements professional judgment and arrived at a point where it displaces it, not occasionally, not in marginal cases, but as the operational norm. The statistics from four professions make the case.

## Medicine: The Documentation Machine

A physician in a modern American hospital spends approximately 49 percent of her working time on electronic health record entry and administrative desk work. That is the finding of Christine Sinsky and colleagues in a 2016 study of 57 physicians across four specialties and multiple practice settings.[^fn-sinsky] For every two hours a physician spends with a patient, she spends nearly two additional hours documenting what happened in the encounter. The documentation requirement did not emerge from nowhere. It serves legitimate functions: insurance billing, malpractice defense, care coordination across providers, regulatory compliance. But the aggregate effect on the formation and exercise of clinical judgment is severe.

[^fn-sinsky]: Christine A. Sinsky et al., *Allocation of Physician Time in Ambulatory Practice: A Time and Motion Study in 4 Specialties*, 165 Annals Internal Med. 753, 753–60 (2016) (doi: 10.7326/M16-0961).

The physician who can spend 49 percent of her time on EHR entry and paperwork is spending less than half her available time on the activity (clinical reasoning with a patient) that her training prepared her to do. More critically, the EHR interface itself is not neutral. It is built around billing categories, insurance codes, and regulatory checkpoints. A clinician working through an EHR intake screen encounters a structured environment that guides her toward certain diagnoses, certain treatment algorithms, and certain documentation pathways. The system is not preventing good medicine; it is shaping what counts as documentable medicine, and those two things are not identical.

The result is a feedback structure. Time pressure drives clinicians toward the structured pathways the EHR presents. Structured pathways produce adequate documentation for billing and compliance purposes. Adequate documentation relieves the immediate time pressure and reinforces the choice to use the pathway next time. The physician who exercises clinical judgment outside the structured pathway produces documentation that is harder to bill and easier to second-guess. The incentive structure punishes the deviation.

This is the deskilling loop in medicine. It does not prevent experienced clinicians from exercising judgment. It makes the exercise of judgment more costly, less legible to the institutional record, and harder to sustain as a habit. Over time, over a career, over a generation of training, the habits atrophy.

## Law: The Compliance Archipelago

American law created a new professional class in the last fifty years. In 1970, the compliance officer was not a recognized occupational category in any meaningful sense. By the 2020s the Bureau of Labor Statistics tracked the role systematically under its own occupational code, with employment in the hundreds of thousands.[^fn-compliance-count] The growth represents not just a cost center but a reallocation of professional judgment.

[^fn-compliance-count]: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, SOC 13-1041 (Compliance Officers), available at bls.gov/oes/current/oes131041.htm.

The growth of compliance as a profession reflects a specific theory: that legal risk is best managed by specialists whose job is to monitor compliance with specific rules, rather than by the judgment of the lawyers, bankers, and businesspeople whose conduct those rules govern. The theory has logic. Regulatory regimes have grown complex enough that a practicing attorney cannot carry the full regulatory map in her head. Dodd-Frank alone generated 398 rulemaking requirements after 2008.[^fn-dodd-frank] The Securities and Exchange Commission, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, and multiple other agencies each added layer upon layer of rulemaking. The compliance specialist emerged as the person whose job is to track the layers.

[^fn-dodd-frank]: Davis Polk & Wardwell LLP, Dodd-Frank Progress Report (Dec. 2014) (tracking 398 total rulemaking requirements under the Dodd-Frank Act).

The problem is structural. When legal judgment is disaggregated into a compliance function, the judgment question changes. The lawyer exercising judgment asks: what does my client need to accomplish, what legal constraints govern that activity, and how do I serve both the client's interest and the regulatory framework? The compliance officer asks: has the required checklist been completed? These are not the same question. The first requires synthetic judgment across a complex situation. The second requires documentation that a defined process was followed.

Over time, the proliferation of compliance requirements changes what junior lawyers learn to do. The first-year associate at a large financial institution in 2026 spends a large fraction of her professional time on compliance reviews, KYC documentation, and regulatory filing. The client counseling, the deal structure analysis, the exercise of legal imagination about what a transaction can accomplish: those skills develop later, if they develop at all, because the early professional years are occupied by process work. The loop closes: complex regulation generates compliance-heavy practice; compliance-heavy practice crowds out judgment formation; lawyers with underdeveloped judgment produce work that requires more compliance oversight; more compliance oversight generates more process requirements.

## Finance: Judgment Replaced Twice

Finance offers the deskilling loop in its purest form, because finance is an industry that believed it had already solved the judgment problem. The quantitative revolution in finance from the 1970s forward rested on a premise: that risk could be modeled, and that modeled risk could substitute for the exercise of judgment about particular transactions. The Value-at-Risk metric, the credit-default swap pricing model, the mortgage-backed security tranche structure: each was presented as a technology that made professional judgment more precise by reducing it to calculation.

The premise turned out to be wrong in ways that are now well understood. The 2008 financial crisis was not caused by a failure of the judgment-substituting models in the sense of a calculation error. It was caused by systematic misplacement of trust in those models as substitutes for the kind of judgment that asks: does this transaction make sense, and for whom? The question that the quant model could not ask was the judgment question.

The regulatory response, the 398 Dodd-Frank rulemaking requirements, created a second loop on top of the first. The original deskilling loop (judgment replaced by model) generated a catastrophic failure. The regulatory response generated a compliance loop (model failure corrected by rule). The financial professional now operates inside both loops simultaneously: she runs the quantitative model, and she runs the compliance checklist, and the time and cognitive space available for the exercise of judgment about the actual transaction has been compressed by both.

What the compliance and modeling frameworks share is a theory of professional competence that is hostile to the exercise of judgment under uncertainty. Both frameworks treat the professional's task as the correct execution of a defined procedure. The procedure is the answer; the professional is the procedure's execution engine. On that theory, professional error is a failure to execute the procedure correctly, a compliance failure, rather than a failure of judgment about whether the procedure fit the situation.

## Aviation: The Delegation That Consumed the Judgment

The 737 MAX certification disaster offers the deskilling loop's most concentrated specimen. Approximately 87 percent of the safety certification work for the 737 MAX program was performed not by Federal Aviation Administration engineers but by Boeing employees operating under the Organization Designation Authorization program, through which the FAA delegated its certification authority to the manufacturer.[^fn-faa-boeing] The delegation was legal, institutional, and systematic. It was also a structural transfer of the judgment function.

[^fn-faa-boeing]: U.S. Dep't of Transportation, Office of Inspector General, Weaknesses in FAA's Certification and Delegation Processes Hindered Its Oversight of the 737 MAX Certification (Feb. 23, 2021), available at oig.dot.gov. The House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee separately addressed the same delegation in its final report (Comm. Print, 116th Cong., Sept. 2020); the 87% figure originates in the OIG report.

The FAA engineers who retained nominal oversight were overseeing a process they had not directly conducted. The Boeing engineers who performed the certification work were certifying their own employer's product. The result was an accountability structure in which neither party exercised the kind of independent professional judgment that the certification system was designed to produce. The FAA engineer could not independently evaluate what he had not independently investigated. The Boeing engineer was embedded in an institutional context in which the judgment that the aircraft was safe was not merely a technical conclusion but an organizational interest.

This is the deskilling loop at the institutional level. The FAA's capacity to exercise independent engineering judgment atrophied as delegation grew. Engineers who do not themselves perform certification analyses over the course of a career do not retain the judgment capacities that certification analysis develops. The delegation that was efficient in the short run was destructive of the institutional capacity in the long run. When the judgment was needed: when MCAS failed and the question was whether anyone in the institutional structure could recognize that the certification analysis had been inadequate, the capacity to exercise that judgment had been systematically contracted out.

The 346 people who died in the Lion Air and Ethiopian Airlines crashes were not killed by a technological failure. They were killed by an institutional failure that resulted from a systematic decision to relocate professional judgment outside the professional structure whose job was to exercise it.

## The Common Mechanism

Across medicine, law, finance, and aviation, the same structure produces the deskilling loop. The mechanism has four stages.

The first stage is the substitution offer. A technology, a procedure, a regulatory framework, or an institutional delegation offers to take on a portion of the professional's judgment task. The offer is presented as supplementation: the EHR helps the physician track patient history; the compliance department helps the lawyer track regulatory requirements; the quantitative model helps the banker assess risk; the ODA program helps the FAA manage its certification workload. The supplement is genuine. The physician does have better patient records. The lawyer does know more rules. The banker does have a more precise risk estimate. The FAA does process more certifications.

The second stage is the time reallocation. Because the supplement is doing some of the judgment work, the professional's time is freed for other things. In practice, the freed time does not flow back into the judgment task; it flows into more documentation, more compliance review, more model validation, more oversight of the supplement. The supplement generates its own administrative overhead. The physician spends her freed clinical time on EHR documentation of the clinical encounter the EHR was supposed to support.

The third stage is the atrophy. The judgment capacities that are not exercised do not remain at their prior level. Clinical reasoning, like all forms of practical wisdom, is a skill that develops through practice and degrades through disuse. The physician who exercises clinical judgment under time pressure, with ambiguous evidence, in complex cases, develops better clinical judgment. The physician who relies on structured diagnostic pathways reliably produces defensible documentation. These are not the same activity, and they do not produce the same professional. The atrophy is not a moral failing; it is a predictable response to changed incentive structures.

The fourth stage is the accountability displacement. When professional errors occur (the misdiagnosis, the compliance failure, the risk model breakdown, the certification inadequacy), the question of accountability is referred back to whether the defined procedure was followed correctly. If it was, the procedure itself may be improved, but the professional is not held responsible for the exercise of judgment. If it was not, the professional is responsible for a procedural failure, not a judgment failure. In either case, the remedy is more procedure, more documentation, more checklist. The loop tightens.

There is a secondary consequence that matters for formation. When the procedure is the only locus of accountability, the profession loses the internal standard by which it could identify what a good practitioner looks like. Excellence becomes compliance. The question "did she exercise sound judgment?" is replaced by "did she follow the protocol?" A profession that cannot distinguish the excellent practitioner from the compliant one cannot transmit excellent practice to the next generation, because excellence is no longer visible in the institutional record.

Michael Polanyi described this class of problem in his account of tacit knowledge: the skills that professionals carry cannot be fully articulated, precisely because they are the skills of judgment: the ability to assess which explicit procedure fits the present situation, to recognize when no existing procedure fits, to generate a response to a situation that the procedure-designers did not anticipate.[^fn-polanyi] The deskilling loop systematically attacks the tacit-knowledge layer while leaving the explicit-knowledge layer intact. The result is a professional who can document anything and judge nothing.

[^fn-polanyi]: Michael Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (1966). The argument that explicit knowledge depends on a tacit substrate ("we know more than we can tell") applies directly to professional judgment: the explicit procedure depends on the tacit capacity to recognize when and how to apply it.

Friedrich Hayek's point about knowledge is directly relevant here, though Hayek made it in a different context. The knowledge that matters for complex decisions is local, particular, and embedded in specific circumstances, exactly the knowledge that standardized procedures cannot capture and that centralized systems cannot transmit.[^fn-hayek] The compliance framework treats professional judgment as a form of centrally codifiable knowledge: write enough rules and all the relevant judgment can be captured. The deskilling loop is what happens when that theory is run to its conclusion.

[^fn-hayek]: F.A. Hayek, *The Use of Knowledge in Society*, 35 Am. Econ. Rev. 519, 519–30 (1945). Hayek's argument concerned economic planning, but the epistemological point generalizes: dispersed, tacit, context-dependent knowledge cannot be centralized without loss.

## What the Loop Forecloses

The deskilling loop forecloses three things that cannot be recovered by adding more rules or more technology.

The first is the capacity to recognize the novel situation. Professional judgment is most critical precisely when the standard procedure does not apply. The physician who encounters a presentation that does not fit any diagnostic algorithm needs the clinical judgment to recognize the gap and reason from first principles. The lawyer who encounters a transaction structure that no existing regulatory framework anticipates needs the legal judgment to reason from the purpose of the law, not its letter. The engineer who confronts a failure mode that no prior certification analysis identified needs the engineering judgment to understand what is actually happening. These are the situations where professional judgment is not supplemented by procedure but must substitute for it entirely. The deskilling loop contracts the population of professionals who retain that capacity.

The second is the capacity for professional accountability. Accountability requires an agent who can be meaningfully held responsible for a decision. When the judgment is distributed across a compliance department, a model, an institutional delegation, and a documentation system, the agent disappears. The 2008 financial crisis and the 737 MAX certification failure share this feature: in the aftermath of both disasters, it was genuinely difficult to identify which human beings had exercised the judgment that produced the catastrophe, because the judgment had been so thoroughly distributed and proceduralized that no individual could be located as its author. A profession whose members cannot be held accountable for the exercise of their professional judgment is not, in any meaningful sense, a profession.

The third is the formation of the next generation. Professions transmit their judgment capacities through practice, not through credentialing. The medical student who works beside a clinician exercising clinical judgment in ambiguous situations acquires clinical judgment. The law student who watches a senior lawyer work through a complex transaction acquires transactional judgment. The engineering student who participates in a genuine certification analysis acquires the judgment that certification requires. When the senior professional's time is consumed by documentation and process, the transmission mechanism breaks down. The junior professional is trained in process compliance and receives inadequate exposure to the judgment exercise that process compliance was supposed to support.

Michael Oakeshott's distinction between technical knowledge and practical knowledge is exact here.[^fn-oakeshott] Technical knowledge can be written down in rules and procedures and transmitted through formal instruction. Practical knowledge, the knowledge of how to do something well formed through habituation and practice, cannot be transmitted that way; it can only be acquired by doing the thing alongside someone who already knows how to do it. The deskilling loop degrades the transmission of practical knowledge by replacing the doing with documentation of the doing.

[^fn-oakeshott]: Michael Oakeshott, *Rationalism in Politics* (1962). Oakeshott's argument that the Rationalist treats all knowledge as technical and thereby misunderstands the nature of practical skill is directly applicable to the professional-regulation context.

## The Loop Is Not Inevitable

The deskilling loop has a structure, and a structure has intervention points. The diagnosis is the work of this paper. The prescription, what kind of professional formation defends against the loop and what the transactional lawyer specifically must be trained to do, is the work of the companion essay, [Jurisgenesis in Transactional Drafting]({{ '/essays/jurisgenesis-drafting/' | relative_url }}). The institutional question, what conditions a program must satisfy to form judgment at all, is the work of [The Three States of a Living Tradition]({{ '/essays/three-states/' | relative_url }}). The three essays read together.

The point this essay closes on is narrower. A profession whose members can execute procedures but cannot exercise judgment when procedures run out is not a profession in any sense its founders would recognize. The physician who can diagnose the ordinary case reliably but cannot recognize when she is facing an extraordinary one is not a better physician for having better checklists. She is a more dangerous one. The documentation requirements and compliance frameworks that drive the loop serve real functions and will not disappear. The question is whether anything else remains.
