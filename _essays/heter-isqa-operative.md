# Heter Isqa as Operative Form

## I.

The *heter isqa* is not a historical artifact. It is the operative commercial instrument by which observant Jews and Jewish institutions structure capital deployment without violating the biblical prohibition on *ribbit*. Major Israeli banks offer *heter isqa* versions of commercial loans and certain consumer products. Jewish community lenders and *gemach* funds in the United States, the United Kingdom, and elsewhere use *heter isqa* deeds as a matter of routine. The deed is drafted, signed, witnessed, and filed. It functions in present commercial life the way any other commercial contract functions: it allocates capital, allocates risk, and allocates return.

That is the claim of this essay. The lineage page treats the *heter isqa* as the halakhic mechanism that completes Maimonides on *tzedakah*.[^fn-lineage] This essay shows that the mechanism is operative. The doctrinal commitment the Jewish tradition holds, that capital must be deployed in partnership at shared risk rather than as a fixed-interest loan to a fellow Jew, is not preserved as memory. It is practiced.

The Catholic twin to this argument is set out in [Jurisgenesis in Transactional Drafting](/LEP/essays/jurisgenesis-drafting/). There the *societas* is shown as a live drafting practice. Here the *heter isqa* is shown as its halakhic counterpart, operative in the same hour and on the same balance sheets.

## II. The problem the *heter isqa* solves

The Torah prohibits lending at interest to a fellow Jew. Leviticus directs the Israelite to uphold his brother and to take no *neshekh* or *tarbit* from him.[^fn-lev] Deuteronomy repeats the prohibition and contrasts the foreigner, from whom interest may be taken, with the brother, from whom it may not.[^fn-deut] The prohibition is biblical, not rabbinic. It binds the lender and the borrower alike; later authorities will hold that a Jew who pays interest to another Jew transgresses no less than the one who collects it.

The prohibition rules out the *mutuum* form: a loan of fungible capital that the borrower must repay with a fixed premium regardless of what the capital earned. The borrower bears all the risk. The lender keeps the capital safe and adds a return on top. That is what the Torah forbids and what the Talmudic literature elaborates.

The Talmud responds with an alternative structure. Bava Metzia 5:4 and the *sugya* at 68a to 70b set out the *isqa*: a deal in which the capital provider hands funds to a recipient who will trade or invest with them, structured so that the provider's return depends on the venture's outcome. The provider does not hold a debt; he holds a position in a deal.[^fn-bm] The Talmudic discussion is detailed: what fraction is loan and what fraction is deposit, what wage the manager is owed for his labor, how oath obligations attach to losses claimed, how the deal must be witnessed to satisfy the prohibition rather than merely paper over it. The *isqa* is not a euphemism for a loan. It is a different instrument.

Maimonides places the *isqa* structure in its doctrinal context. In Hilkhot Mattenot Aniyim 10:7 he ranks partnership with the one who has fallen as the highest rung of *tzedakah*, above every form of gift.[^fn-rambam-mat] In Hilkhot Sheluhin v'Shutafin he sets out the operational law of the *isqa* and the partnership-manager.[^fn-rambam-shel] The two halakhot belong together. The highest rung of charity is *shutafut*, partnership. The operational form of *shutafut* is the *isqa*. The doctrinal commitment and the commercial instrument are one body of law.

## III. From *isqa* to *heter isqa*

The medieval period generated the *shtar isqa*, the formal deed by which two parties enter the Talmudic structure. By the early modern period the Ashkenazi authorities had settled on a standardized deed, the *heter isqa*, literally a "permission of partnership," that could be appended to or recited over what would otherwise look like an interest-bearing loan and convert it into a halakhically permitted partnership-at-shared-risk.

The Rema, Rabbi Moshe Isserles of sixteenth-century Krakow, plays the formative role for the Ashkenazi tradition. His glosses on the Shulchan Arukh treat the *heter isqa* as the standard remedy in Yoreh De'ah 167, the section that codifies the laws of *ribbit*.[^fn-ya] The deed is not a loophole. It is a redrafting of the deal as the Talmud has authorized it to be drafted. The two parties have not avoided the prohibition; they have written a different contract from the one the prohibition forbids.

The point is worth pausing on. A loophole presupposes that the underlying transaction is the prohibited one and that the form is a way of dressing it up. The *heter isqa* tradition does not see itself this way. The underlying transaction, when properly executed, *is* a partnership at shared risk. The deed is the document that makes that fact legible to the parties, to the rabbinic court, and to any later party who needs to know which kind of deal this was. The Rema and the authorities who follow him insist on the substantive requirements: the provider's capital must in fact be at risk in the venture; the deal must be witnessed; the manager-borrower owes the truth about losses; the wage component for the manager's labor must be real.

## IV. How the *heter isqa* works structurally

The mechanism can be stated cleanly. The capital provider gives funds to the recipient under a deed that designates the capital in two parts. Half is a loan: the recipient bears the risk on that half and owes it back regardless of outcome. Half is a deposit-at-risk, sometimes called a *piqadon*: the provider bears the risk on that half, and the recipient owes it back only to the extent the venture preserved it. The recipient takes the combined sum and invests it in a real venture, whether trade, lending, real estate, or another commercial use the deed describes.

If the venture succeeds, the parties share the profit in the proportions the deed sets. The provider's share of profit is attributable to his deposit-at-risk portion, not to the loan portion. If the venture fails, the parties share the loss in the structural proportions: the recipient absorbs the loss on the loan half, and the provider absorbs the loss on the deposit half. The provider's "return," to the extent he receives one, is profit on a position genuinely exposed to loss, not interest on a debt insulated from loss.

The deed handles several practical problems the Talmud already anticipated. The manager-borrower is owed a small wage for his labor in running the venture; without it, the provider would be receiving free management of his capital, which would itself raise *ribbit* concerns. The deed sets the wage explicitly, often nominally. The deed also addresses how losses are to be proved: the manager swears an oath, or, in many modern versions, the parties stipulate that losses may be proved only by the testimony of named witnesses or a designated rabbinic court, which has the practical effect of letting the parties settle for the agreed return in ordinary cases while preserving the substantive risk-sharing the halakha requires.

The modern banking deed adds an overlay. A bank that issues a commercial loan under a *heter isqa* will treat the stated rate as the expected profit share on the deposit half of the structure. The borrower-recipient signs the *heter isqa* together with the loan documents. If the venture fails in a way the deed recognizes, the bank's recovery is limited to what the structure permits. If the venture succeeds, the bank receives the stated rate as its share of profit. The deed is a real allocation, not a fiction, even where the parties expect in the ordinary case that the stated rate will be paid.

## V. The *heter isqa* in modern banking

The *heter isqa* is used today. The major Israeli banks, including Mizrahi-Tefahot, Bank Leumi, Bank Hapoalim, and Discount Bank, offer commercial loans and certain consumer products structured under a *heter isqa* deed. Customers who wish to transact under halakhic terms request the *heter isqa* version; the bank treats the request as ordinary business. Bank of Israel and the rabbinic authorities have issued guidance on what a compliant *heter isqa* requires for institutional use, and the Chief Rabbinate's *heter isqa kelali*, a general communal deed, supplies a baseline form that many institutions adopt.

Outside Israel, the institutional footprint is smaller but real. In the United States, Jewish community lenders, *gemach* operations, and certain private credit funds use *heter isqa* deeds as a matter of standard practice for loans between Jewish parties. Some American banks with a substantial observant Jewish customer base will execute a *heter isqa* on request. The *gemach* networks in Brooklyn, Lakewood, Baltimore, Cleveland, and elsewhere extend interest-free loans by default, and where a structured return is appropriate, the *heter isqa* is the form.

The point is not the size of the market. The point is that the deed is alive. A doctrinal commitment that the Catholic tradition has had to reconstruct, after several centuries during which the explicit usury doctrine retreated from operative banking practice, the Jewish tradition has kept in continuous use. The instrument was never put down. The drafting tradition was never lost. The halakhic authorities never stopped issuing new versions to handle new instruments: revolving credit, syndicated loans, credit cards, structured products. Each new commercial form has generated a corresponding *heter isqa* literature.

## VI. What the *heter isqa* shows

Three claims follow.

First, a doctrinal commitment can persist as live commercial practice across three thousand years if the community that holds the doctrine builds the instruments to carry it. The *heter isqa* is the proof of concept. The Torah's prohibition on *ribbit*, the Talmud's elaboration of the *isqa*, Maimonides's doctrinal placement, the Rema's standardization, and the modern bank's commercial product are one continuous tradition of drafting. The lawyer in Krakow in 1570 and the bank attorney in Tel Aviv in 2026 are working on versions of the same document.

Second, the *societas* / *shutafut* convergence the lineage page traces is not symbolic. It has a real operational analogue in contemporary banking. The Catholic and Jewish traditions converge on partnership at shared risk as the just form of capital deployment; the Jewish tradition has been practicing the form continuously while the Catholic tradition retreated from explicit usury doctrine after the early modern period. The recovery of the Catholic form, in venture-capital drafting, in profit-participation loans, in the modern *societas* literature, is in part a recovery of what the Jewish tradition never let lapse. The two traditions can teach each other on this point because one of them has the operative experience the other has temporarily lost.

Third, the alternative to fixed-interest debt is not absence of finance. It is a different structure of finance. Critics of the historical usury doctrine often treat it as incompatible with developed commercial life, on the assumption that productive credit requires the *mutuum* form. The *heter isqa* is a counterexample of the most direct sort: a real banking sector, with real commercial loans and real consumer credit, operating under a documentary structure that the doctrine permits. The form is more complex than a simple loan. The form requires the parties to do the doctrinal work that the deed sets out. The form is not impossible. It is in fact what observant Jewish commerce has done.

## VII. Implications for present transactional drafting

The *heter isqa* is not a parochial instrument. It is a worked example of the doctrinal rule the Program names [Partnership at Shared Risk](/LEP/partnership-at-shared-risk/). The lawyer trained to draft a *societas*, in the Roman and Thomistic lineage the lineage page traces, and the lawyer trained to draft a *heter isqa*, in the Talmudic and post-Talmudic lineage the same page traces in its sixth section, are working with the same doctrinal commitment in two vocabularies. They are draftsmen of partnership where a draftsman of a fixed-interest loan would be a draftsman of *mutuum*.

The Program for Law and Entrepreneurship treats both as substantive doctrine and trains practitioners to read and draft in both vocabularies. The Catholic lawyer who learns the *heter isqa* learns what a continuous drafting tradition looks like when the doctrine has not been allowed to lapse. The Jewish lawyer who learns the *societas* literature learns what the Catholic moral theology of commerce has reconstructed and how the reconstruction is now showing up in venture, ESOP, and tokenized-venture drafting.[^fn-jurisgen] The two streams are not in competition. They are mutually instructive because they are answering the same doctrinal question with a shared answer.

The work for the practitioner is the same work the lineage page describes for the modern instrument generally: read the deal through the anatomy and draft the deal so the anatomy holds. The *heter isqa* shows what that work looks like when the tradition has done it without interruption. The remaining task is to do it everywhere else.
