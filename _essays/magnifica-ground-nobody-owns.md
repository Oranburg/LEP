# The Ground Nobody Owns: Torah, Roman Law, and the Age of AI

*Seth C. Oranburg*

When Justinian's lawyers organized the Roman law of property, they made one fundamental cut before any other. Book Two of the Institutes opens not with who owns what but with what kind of thing is at issue. Things common to all by natural law (*res communes omnium*) — the air, running water, the sea, the seashore — are outside private appropriation entirely. Things belonging to no one (*res nullius*) — wild animals, fish, abandoned property — belong to the first person who takes them. The first category is the lawyer's instruction not to try. The second is the lawyer's invitation to capture.

The law of first possession, from Justinian's *occupatio* through Locke's labor-mixing theory through the American law of capture, is built on the second category. John Locke gave it its most influential modern form: a person who removes something from the state of nature and mixes labor with it makes it property, so long as enough and as good is left for others. Richard Epstein refined the analytical foundations of the doctrine in 1979; Carol Rose answered him in 1985 by recasting first possession as a communicative act performed in front of an audience. Both accounts operate inside the law of things. Neither asks whether the thing in question belongs in the law of things at all.

This gap matters more than it once did. The AI industry has built its data model on an implicit application of the *res nullius* rule: personal information, attention, behavioral patterns, and user-generated content are treated as available for capture by the first party who acquires and processes them. Pope Leo XIV's encyclical *Magnifica Humanitas* pushes back against this framing at ¶ 178, arguing that "personal lives" cannot be reduced to "exploitable information" and that "shared knowledge" must become "a true common good rather than an instrument of dominance." The encyclical reaches for *res communes omnium* as its counter-image, the category that limits capture.

But Roman law's two categories do not quite cover the case, and I noticed, reading the week's Torah portion one evening while I had the encyclical open on the same desk, that Hebrew supplies a third one.

The Torah portion was *Bamidbar*, the opening of the Book of Numbers. The Israelites are in the מדבר (*midbar*), the wilderness, and God tells Moses to count the people by name. The rabbis asked why the Torah was given in the desert and not in a city or a cultivated field that belonged to somebody. The answer in *Bamidbar Rabbah* 1:7 is a single legal term: the desert is הפקר (*hefker*), ownerless. It belongs to no tribe and no king and no company, and the tradition holds that anyone willing to make himself ownerless like the wilderness can receive what was given there.

*Hefker* is a live category in Jewish law. Property can be rendered *hefker* by deliberate abandonment, at which point it returns to a kind of legal wilderness and is available for acquisition. But the wilderness of Sinai was *hefker* in a different sense. It was not abandoned; it had never been appropriated in the first place. And it was not *hefker* in the *res nullius* sense, the first-taker's invitation. The Torah was not given there because anyone who showed up could capture it. It was given there because the giving required ground that no one had already enclosed. The wilderness is the category of thing that must remain ownerless as a condition of what can happen on it.

Roman law has no name for this. *Res communes omnium* is the category that cannot be privatized because the good is too broadly shared: the air, the sea. *Res nullius* is the category that can be privatized, waiting for the first taker. Neither is the category of the thing that must remain outside all forms of ownership because the giving requires it. *Hefker* in the wilderness sense is a third category.

The practical stakes are concrete. The personal data that AI platforms acquire is not air or running water; it does not fit comfortably in *res communes omnium*. But it is also not a wild animal or a piece of abandoned property. It is information that is constitutively connected to the person who generated it. The *res nullius* framing treats the connection as irrelevant once the data has been detached from its source. The *hefker* framework suggests a different question: is this a kind of thing whose value depends on its remaining outside the structures of ownership? If the answer is yes, then the appropriate response is not to build a better property regime for personal data. It is to recognize that some personal information participates in personhood so thoroughly that no property regime can honor its value without corrupting it.

Leo XIV's encyclical names this as a question of "shared knowledge" becoming a "true common good," but the Hebrew category is more precise. It is not shared in the way that air is shared, available to all without diminishment. It is more like the wilderness: the ground on which something can be received that cannot be received anywhere else, and that remains receivable only as long as the ground stays open.

The question this raises for the lawyer drafting in the AI age is not principally what property rules should govern personal data. It is the prior question: is this the kind of thing that the law of property is equipped to govern at all? The איסורי הנאה (*issurei hana'ah*) tradition in Jewish law holds that certain goods sit outside commerce not because they have no value but because their value exceeds the market form. Putting something in commerce does not price it correctly; it corrupts it. The wilderness of Sinai was not available for capture, even by the people standing in it. It was available only for receiving, and only to those who had first made themselves available.

The census in Numbers begins on the same ground: before the people build anything, before they are assigned to any tribe's position in the camp, they are counted by name, one at a time. The counting precedes any allocation or assignment. The person is recognized before the project begins.

That is a different starting point than Justinian's. The first question is not what kind of thing this is and who may take it. The first question is whether the person in front of you has been recognized as a person before any transaction begins. The clause that treats a user's behavioral data as an input to be captured and processed for training a downstream model has answered the second question without stopping to ask the first. The encyclical asks the lawyer to stop first.
