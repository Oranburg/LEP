# The Cup-Bearer's Position

*Seth C. Oranburg*

The bird was banging on my window again. He comes in the evenings, when I work late. I have named him Bangy Bird. He bangs for a while and leaves when it is fully dark.

I was not paying attention to him that night. On the screen was the new encyclical from Pope Leo XIV: *Magnifica Humanitas*, released on a Monday in May, addressed to the world. On the desk beside the laptop was my Chumash, open to *Bamidbar*, the Book of Numbers. I had been with it since before Shabbat, working my way slowly through the parsha the way a person works slowly through a foreign city with a pocket map. I noticed. That is the honest verb.

What I was doing that night, mostly, was stalling. The screen had a half dozen unanswered emails, a platform-services agreement that needed three more rounds before it was right, a data-governance memo I had been arguing with for two days, and the encyclical, which I had downloaded that morning and promised myself I would read before bed. And the Chumash was on the desk because it had been there all week, the way a book gets there when you mean to return to it and keep finding reasons not to.

Leo opens the encyclical with a question about what we are building. His frame is two buildings, two cities: Babel against Jerusalem. "The primary choice," he writes in the ninth paragraph, "is not between a 'yes' or 'no' to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem." Babel is the city built for its own name, where one language shuts out every other language and a tower is raised to reach heaven by force of engineering. Jerusalem under Nehemiah is the city rebuilt by ordinary people, each family given its own section of the wall, no section more important than another. Leo is a careful writer and he knows what he is doing when he sets these two cities side by side, because the contrast is not only between pride and humility, or between bad technology and good technology. It is between two ways of organizing people: one that flattens everyone into uniform labor, and one that sees each person as irreplaceable and gives each person a piece of the wall to hold.

I read that passage twice and then I put the encyclical down and picked up the Chumash, not because I thought they were about the same thing but because I thought they were about the opposite things, and I wanted to sit with the contrast. The encyclical is about cities. The Chumash I had open was about a wilderness.

The Book of Numbers begins in the desert. God tells Moses to count the people, every person by name, every family, every tribe. שאו את ראש (*Se'u et rosh*), lift up the head: that is the census command, literally "raise the head," and the rabbis make something of this, that you count a person by lifting his head, not by writing down a number. The desert has no buildings. The desert belongs to no one. The rabbis noticed this and said the Torah was given in the wilderness precisely because the wilderness is הפקר (*hefker*), ownerless, so that anyone willing to receive it could receive it. The Torah could not be given in a city because a city belongs to someone, and the Torah belongs to everyone.

I had been circling that contrast all week without quite seeing where it led. Two buildings against a wilderness. The wilderness as the place where names are raised, one at a time, before anything is built.

Then I found Nehemiah, and everything shifted.

I found him the way you find things in a Chumash at midnight, which is to say I went looking for something else and Nehemiah was standing there when I arrived. Leo had mentioned him and I wanted to go back to the source text. Nehemiah is not in the Torah; he is in one of the later books, part of what we call the Writings, and he is one of the stranger figures in the tradition because he is a Jew in high office at a non-Jewish court. He is cupbearer to Artaxerxes, the Persian king. Not a minor position: the cupbearer stands at the king's elbow, pours his wine, tastes it for poison. You do not get that job without trust, and you do not keep it without discretion.

Nehemiah hears that Jerusalem's walls have been broken and its gates burned. He sits and weeps for days, fasting and praying. Then he goes back to his post at the king's side and he waits. He does not resign. He does not announce that he cannot in good conscience serve a Persian king while his people's city lies in ruins. He carries the king's cup and waits for the right moment, and when the moment comes he asks for leave to go and rebuild.

The text records Artaxerxes's question and Nehemiah's answer so quickly that you can miss them. The king asks: "What are you requesting?" And Nehemiah writes: "I prayed to the God of Heaven, and then I answered the king." The prayer happens in the space between the question and the answer. A breath between what power asks and what the faithful say.

He gets the leave. He gets letters of safe passage. He gets timber from the king's forest to build the gates. The position inside the Persian court is not the shameful part of the story. It is the equipment.

I set the Chumash down and looked at the screen for a long time without reading.

I am joining a Catholic university in August. The Catholic University of America, in Washington, in the Archdiocese of Washington, founded by the American bishops and chartered by Pope Leo XIII. I will be a professor in the law school there. I am Jewish. I have been Jewish in non-Jewish institutions before, because that is most of what American Jewish professional life is, but this one is going to be different, because the institution's religious identity is legible and official in a way that most institutions' is not. There will be a chapel. There will be a university Mass. There will be crosses in the hallways and a theology faculty and a president who reports, in some formal sense, to Rome.

I have been working through this for months. There is nothing wrong with a Catholic university or the people in it. The discomfort is that accepting the position can feel like an admission: that I am willing to work at the margin of my own tradition, that I have chosen a professional home whose faith is not mine, that there is something embarrassing in the choice.

Nehemiah did not seem embarrassed.

He seemed clear. He had a position inside an institution whose faith was not his, and the position gave him access to the resources that let him rebuild something his people had lost. That is not a compromise of the story. That is the story. The cupbearer's job is not a prelude to Nehemiah's real life; the cupbearer's job is why Nehemiah could get timber from the king's forest and safe passage through the provinces. When he arrives in Jerusalem and surveys the broken walls at night, alone, he says nothing to the nobles and priests and officials, not yet. He waits until he has seen enough to know what the work requires. Then he calls them together and says: "You see the trouble we are in. Come, let us rebuild the wall of Jerusalem, that we may no longer suffer derision." *And they said: let us rise up and build. So they strengthened their hands for the good work.*

The verb the Hebrew uses for the strengthening is הֶחֱזִיק (*heḥezik*), from the root that means to be strong, to seize hold of. It runs through the third chapter of the book like a refrain: "next to him, so-and-so *heḥezik*; next to him, such-and-such family *heḥezik*." Each person gripping their section of the wall.

Each person taking charge of their piece. Nehemiah does not build the wall. He organizes the building. He is still, in some sense, a court official, a man with access to a Persian king's letters, standing in the ruins of his people's city with a surveyor's eye and a diplomat's patience, watching his neighbors grip their section of the wall.

Leo reads this and sees subsidiarity: governance at the lowest competent level, each person accountable for their own piece, nobody assigned more than they can hold. I read it and see something else first, or something adjacent. I see a figure who does not have to choose between the Persian court and the Jewish people. He brings the Persian court's resources home to the Jewish people and stands in the ruined city and organizes the rebuilding. The position in the non-Jewish institution is the instrument. What he does with it is the point.

The night I made this connection I went looking in the Hebrew for things I might have missed. I looked up the modern Hebrew for artificial intelligence, because the encyclical is about artificial intelligence and I had been reading it all evening and I was curious whether the Hebrew had anything to say that Leo's Italian didn't. It is בינה מלאכותית (*bina melakhutit*). The first word is understanding, the kind the tradition says God gave Solomon when Solomon asked not for riches but for a heart that could judge his people wisely. The second word is the adjective form of מלאכה (*melakha*), which means work or craft.

The root of *melakha*, the consonants underneath the word, are the same consonants as the root of מלאך (*malach*), the Hebrew word for messenger, which the Christian tradition translates as angel. The intelligence of craft and the messenger share their letters, because both are sent. Both have a job. Neither chooses.

The tradition holds that an angel has no inclination to fall and, for the same reason, no inclination to rise. It cannot disobey, cannot repent, cannot love, and cannot enter a covenant, because entering a covenant requires the freedom to break it. An angel performs the mission of whoever sent it, exactly, and the only question that has ever mattered about a messenger is who did the sending.

Leo says this about AI at paragraph 99, in a list of negations: the machine does not undergo experiences, does not possess a body, does not feel joy or pain, does not have a moral conscience. A thing that executes a mission without conscience is a thing the tradition already had a name for.

I drafted the platform-services agreement the next morning. *Shall* is a different country from *may*. The clause governs the machine, and the machine does not pause to ask whether the clause is wise. The lawyer is writing the angel's instructions, setting down what the messenger is permitted to carry and what it is not. The Hebrew had been there the whole time.

What I had been circling all week is the question of what it means to carry a tradition into a room where the tradition is not the official one. The Jewish professor at the Catholic university, the Jewish cupbearer at the Persian court, the Jewish lawyer drafting clauses to bind an algorithm with no יצר הרע (*yetzer hara*) and no יצר הטוב (*yetzer tov*). The tradition is not on the letterhead. It is the thing you carry.

Nehemiah prays before he answers the king. The prayer happens in the space of a breath, between the question and the answer. He is standing at the king's side with the cup, and he is also, in that same moment, standing inside the covenant. The two things are not at war with each other. The court position gives him the timber to build the gates. The covenant gives him the reason to go home.

I have been thinking about what I carry to the Catholic university. Not as the designated Jewish voice in a Catholic room, but as a scholar who learned to think from a tradition and carries it into the work. The tradition shows up in the questions I ask, in the sources I reach for, in the things I notice when I look. I do not need to announce it. I do not need to hide it. I need to grip my section of the wall.

Leo's Nehemiah rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones. That is his phrase, and I have been reading it for weeks now, turning it over. You do not build the wall first. You count the people first, by name, by family, raising each head. The census in the wilderness comes before the march toward any city. The Chumash was on the desk all week for a reason, and the reason was that the wilderness is the place where the work of counting people happens before any stone is laid.

The night Bangy Bird stopped banging I was still at the desk with both books open. The silence had been there a while before I noticed it. The bird leaves when it is fully dark; he does not care what I am reading. But he is the reason I notice the silence, and through the silence the dark, and through the dark the room I am actually in, which is neither the encyclical's Babel nor the encyclical's Jerusalem but something earlier: the census-ground, the wilderness, the place where a man with access to a Persian king's letters and a Jewish people's grief and a cup in his hand figures out what to build and where.

I closed the Chumash. I left the encyclical open.

Tomorrow there is a platform-services agreement with three more rounds to go, and a faculty appointment that starts in August, and a clause to revise on a model I do not fully understand yet, and a section of wall that has my name on it.

*Seth Oranburg is a professor of business law at The Catholic University of America Columbus School of Law, where he writes on law, technology, and the governance of emerging systems from a Jewish legal perspective.*
