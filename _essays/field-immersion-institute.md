# Field Immersion and Executive Institute

## The problem

Professional legal education designs its pedagogy by theorizing about judgment formation. It does not study the surviving traditions in which judgment formation actually occurs. Three such traditions exist in the West and remain in active practice: the Socratic elenchus, recovered in democratic settings from a philosophical past that modern institutions largely severed (Athens); havruta learning, practiced continuously in voluntary institutions without state infrastructure for two millennia (Jerusalem); and disputatio, transmitted through institutional continuity in university settings without interruption (Rome). These three conditions (severed-and-reclaimed, continuously-practiced-without-state, continuously-practiced-within-institution) constitute a natural typology for studying what makes a pedagogical tradition survive, transfer, and form practical knowledge in practitioners. No professional school has studied them as a set. The result is a professional education debate conducted without primary evidence about how the relevant methods work when used by practitioners who have never stopped using them.

## The intervention

Two integrated mechanisms, one for research, one for dissemination.

The first is a co-investigator field immersion structured as fieldwork on the three states of a living pedagogical tradition. The program's co-investigators travel to Athens, Jerusalem, and Rome as learners, not observers: they participate in elenchus, havruta, and disputatio under practitioners who have never stopped teaching them. The immersion generates firsthand evidence about how each method operates, what cognitive conditions it requires, and how it transfers to new institutional settings. This is the methodological commitment Oakeshott names in distinguishing practical knowledge (knowledge that can only be learned in practice) from technical knowledge that can be reduced to rules. The DC station is the deployment site: the evidence gathered abroad is brought into contact with the legal and regulatory problems the program is built to address, with structured measurement against three validated instruments (Reflective Judgment Interview, Metacognitive Awareness Inventory, Defining Issues Test-2) adapted from cognitive science.

The second is an executive institute modeled on the George Mason Law and Economics Center. A short, intensive summer institute for business executives, in-house counsel, and senior practitioners applies the program's research findings to the people who hold institutional decision-making authority. The LEC model is directly applicable: bring one discipline's tradition of rigorous analysis into contact with practitioners from another field who have never encountered it, and the encounter produces a lasting reorientation of how they reason about their decisions. The institute is a dissemination channel, a partial revenue source, and an evidence-generation site: participant reasoning before and after the institute is measurable data.

## Deliverables

1. A co-investigator field immersion to Athens, Jerusalem, and Rome, with a documented research design, confirmed host partnerships at each station, and a research report.
2. A three-states-of-tradition typology paper, suitable for submission to a peer-reviewed journal or a policy-facing publication.
3. An inaugural executive institute (three to five days, summer 2028), with curriculum, enrolled participants, pre- and post-measurement data, and published materials.
4. A recurring executive institute schedule by AY 2028-29.
5. An expertise-transfer protocol: a documented methodology any professional school faculty member (in law, medicine, business, or engineering) can follow to acquire and teach a classical pedagogical method systematically.

## Timeline

**Year 1 (AY 2026-27):** Confirm host partnerships. Finalize measurement instruments. Design executive institute curriculum and recruit inaugural faculty.

**Year 2 (AY 2027-28):** Co-investigator field immersion. Research design for deployment phase completed. Executive institute curriculum finalized.

**Year 3 (AY 2028-29):** Inaugural executive institute with pre- and post-measurement. Immersion research report and typology paper submitted. Expertise-transfer protocol documented.

## Budget

Budget developed for full Templeton proposal. The field immersion component (two co-investigators, travel, host honoraria, documentation, research assistant) falls within the $250,000 to $450,000 range identified in program planning for a single twelve-month immersion year. The executive institute carries its own revenue model; net cost to the grant is estimated to be modest after participant fees. Full budget table will accompany the formal proposal.

## Metrics

Field immersion completed at all three stations with confirmed hosts. Typology paper submitted for peer review. Executive institute enrollment, participant seniority, and pre- and post-measurement data collected. Expertise-transfer protocol documented and posted as a public research product. Annual report to the funder with outcome data table.

## Why this funder

Education funders committed to rigorous, evidence-based approaches to teaching and learning will recognize that the field immersion is not a study tour: it is primary fieldwork on the conditions under which practical knowledge forms and transmits. The three-states typology produces a research contribution that generalizes far beyond legal education to any professional field where expert judgment is at stake. The executive institute tests and disseminates the findings at the level of practitioners who already hold institutional authority. The expertise-transfer protocol makes the research product replicable at any professional school willing to do the fieldwork. This is an empirical program in the Hayekian sense: it treats tacit, local knowledge as the object of inquiry and builds a systematic method for making that knowledge transferable without stripping it of the practical dimension that makes it knowledge in the first place.
