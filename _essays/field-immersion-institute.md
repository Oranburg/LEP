# Field Immersion and Executive Institute

*The faculty research and executive education arm of the Program's flagship summer. The student cohort formation arm is described separately in [The Pilgrimage and the Bootcamp]({{ '/essays/pilgrimage-bootcamp/' | relative_url }}). The two arms share infrastructure and the three-station genealogy but address different audiences, generate different deliverables, and require different funders. The intellectual structure both draw on is set out in [The Three States of a Living Tradition]({{ '/essays/three-states/' | relative_url }}) and [Washington Taken Seriously]({{ '/essays/washington-taken-seriously/' | relative_url }}).*

## The problem

American legal education debates judgment formation without primary evidence about how the surviving traditions of judgment formation actually work. Three traditions in the West remain in active practice: the Socratic elenchus, reconstructed in democratic settings (Athens); ḥavruta learning, transmitted communally in voluntary institutions for two millennia (Jerusalem); and the disputation, transmitted through institutional continuity inside pontifical universities (Rome). These are the three states of a living pedagogical tradition. No professional school in the United States has studied them as a set of operating institutions and asked what each method requires, what compounds in students who train inside it, and what transfers when the method is moved to a new setting. The result is a pedagogy literature that argues about formation in the abstract while the methods that demonstrably form judgment continue to operate in plain sight.

## The intervention

{% include figure.html name="callout-three-stations" alt="Three nodes labeled Athens (severed and reclaimed, elenchus), Jerusalem (continuous communal, ḥavruta), Rome (continuous institutional, disputatio)." caption="The three pedagogical traditions the field immersion studies as operating institutions." %}

A two-track research and dissemination program built around the three-station typology.

**Track one: the field immersion.** Two LEP faculty co-investigators (Oranburg and Hoffarth), plus one doctoral or post-doctoral research assistant, conduct six weeks of fieldwork during the summer of 2027. At each station the team sits inside the practice as learners under named practitioners (the Athenian classical-philosophy faculty, the Jerusalem yeshiva masters, the Roman pontifical-university instructors) and documents what the method requires, what changes in the participant's reasoning, and what the structural conditions of transmission are. Pre- and post-immersion measurement uses three validated cognitive instruments adapted from the developmental-pedagogy literature: the Reflective Judgment Interview (King and Kitchener), the Metacognitive Awareness Inventory, and the Defining Issues Test-2. The immersion produces primary qualitative and quantitative data on how each tradition forms judgment and what makes the method replicable in a modern professional school.

**Track two: the Executive Institute.** A four-day residential institute for thirty senior business executives, in-house general counsel, and senior law-firm partners, modeled on the George Mason Law and Economics Center's executive program. The Institute presents the field-immersion findings, supplies the substantive doctrinal frame the Program teaches (the *societas* / *mutuum* distinction and its application in modern transactional drafting), and works through structured problem sets that put the participants' own institutions under the Program's analytic lens. Pre- and post-Institute measurement on the same cognitive instruments documents the change in participant reasoning. The Institute is self-supporting at scale through tuition; the inaugural cohort is partially subsidized to seed reputation and to broaden the participant pool beyond firms that can already pay. ## Deliverables

1. A peer-reviewed pedagogy paper documenting the three-station immersion findings, target venues *Journal of Legal Education* and *Studies in Higher Education*.
2. A protocol document specifying the field-immersion methodology for replication by other institutions.
3. An inaugural Executive Institute (August 2027) with thirty enrolled participants and pre- and post-instrumented data on participant reasoning.
4. A recurring Institute schedule by AY 2028-29 producing partial program self-sufficiency through tuition revenue.
5. A research dataset on cognitive change in senior practitioners after immersion in the Program's substantive frame, released under appropriate access conditions for replication.

## Timeline

**Year 1 (AY 2026-27):** Recruit research assistant. Confirm host practitioners at each station. Pilot the cognitive instruments with the LEP faculty. Design Institute curriculum.

**Year 2 (AY 2027-28):** Conduct six-week field immersion (June and July 2027). Run inaugural Executive Institute (August 2027) with thirty participants. Draft pedagogy paper.

**Year 3 (AY 2028-29):** Submit pedagogy paper. Second Executive Institute, scaled to forty participants. Publish methodology protocol.

## Budget

Budget developed for full proposal. Research-and-immersion costs (two faculty, one research assistant, six-week travel, host honoraria, instrument administration, data analysis) fall within the $250,000 to $400,000 range. The Executive Institute carries a tuition revenue model that should approach break-even in year two and produce net contribution in year three. Full budget table accompanies the formal proposal.

## Metrics

Pre- and post-immersion change in faculty co-investigators on the three cognitive instruments. Pedagogy paper accepted for publication. Executive Institute enrollment, participant seniority, and pre- and post-Institute change on the instruments. Replication adoption by at least one peer institution by 2030. The Program treats methodological rigor as the central metric: a fieldwork program that does not produce instrumented, replicable evidence has produced a study tour, not a research project.

## Why this funder

The field immersion is structured as primary research on questions American legal education has not asked with rigor. Classical-liberal and policy-education funders have consistently supported the development of executive education at the intersection of law, economics, and institutional design (the George Mason LEC is the precedent). The Field Immersion and Executive Institute extends that tradition to a question the LEC model has not yet treated: what survives across the long-running pedagogical traditions of the West, and how does that survival inform the formation of judgment in modern professional schools. The Executive Institute carries the findings to the practitioners whose institutional decisions shape how labor, capital, and partnership are arranged in present commercial life. Both halves serve the substantive scholarly and public-interest commitments that classical-liberal funders have built their portfolios around.
