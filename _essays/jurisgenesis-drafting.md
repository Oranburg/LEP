# Jurisgenesis in Transactional Drafting

Robert Cover described law as something a community generates when it organizes itself around a normative commitment. He called the generation of legal meaning *jurisgenesis*. He set it against *jurispathy*, the state's act of choosing one meaning over the others, killing the rest. The standard reading takes jurisgenesis to operate at the altitude of constitutions, religious traditions, and social movements. The reading offered here is narrower and more practical. Every transactional drafting act is a jurisgenerative act in miniature. The lawyer who writes the operating agreement constitutes the partnership. The venture lives under the law the lawyer made.

This essay receives the prescriptive material stripped from [The Deskilling Loop](/LEP/essays/deskilling-loop/) and from the working paper *Architects of the Void*. It names the vocation those two pieces describe in their separate registers: the transactional lawyer as a creator of private law, not an executor of law made elsewhere. The diagnosis there is that modern professional structures have stripped the generative act out of the lawyer's hands. The claim here is that the generative act is the vocation, and that its formation is the central pedagogical work of an entrepreneurial law program serious about what it trains people to do.

## I. What Cover actually said

The argument begins with Cover. *Nomos and Narrative*, the foreword to the Supreme Court's 1982 Term in the *Harvard Law Review*, sets out a picture of legal life that the academy has spent four decades half-using. Law, Cover wrote, is the bridge between the world that is and the world that ought to be. A *nomos* is a normative universe held in place by the stories a community tells about itself. Law is what crosses from story to conduct. Legal meaning is generated wherever a community of meaning organizes itself around a commitment strong enough to constitute a way of life.[^cover-1983]

[^cover-1983]: Robert M. Cover, *Foreword: Nomos and Narrative*, 97 Harv. L. Rev. 4 (1983) (the Supreme Court 1982 Term foreword). [VERIFY page pin before publication.]

That is jurisgenesis: the proliferation of normative meaning out of communities. Cover's examples are large. Insular religious communities generate law. Social movements generate law. Resistant minorities generate law. The state, faced with the abundance, performs *jurispathy*: it picks one meaning and authorizes it, and the others die or go underground. The picture inverts the conventional account. The state is not the source of law. The state is the editor.

The transactional lawyer operates upstream of the editor. Her work is one of the many sources of legal meaning whose product may or may not be ratified by a court later. When a dispute arrives at a courthouse and a judge construes the contract she drafted, the judge is performing jurispathy on the *nomos* her drafting helped create. The drafting was the law-making. The adjudication is the selection.

This shift in altitude is the point. Cover's framework is usually applied to grand normative communities. It applies just as cleanly to a closing-room. The deal lawyer at the table is a node in a community of meaning, and the document she produces is the local law of that community, valid until and unless the state's editor intervenes.

## II. The drafting moment as legal creation

Watch what actually happens when the lawyer writes a clause.

She is not transcribing a rule from a treatise. There is no treatise that contains the operating agreement she is drafting. She is selecting from a set of possible legal forms, the partnership, the limited liability company, the joint venture, the silent participation, the convertible note, the SAFE, the profits interest, the carry, the preferred return, the drag, the tag, the put, the call. She is modifying the chosen form to fit the deal's economics. She is committing the parties to a structure that did not exist in this particular configuration before she wrote it.

The resulting document is a small body of private law. Its provisions govern the parties' conduct in the venture. The default rules of the relevant code, the Delaware LLC Act, Article 9, the Securities Act, fill the gaps the document does not address, but the document itself supplies the operative law for the matters it addresses. The parties' future conduct is governed by it. Courts will enforce it. The state will treat it as binding even though the state did not write it.

This is jurisgenesis in the technical Coverian sense. A community of meaning, here a venture and its investors, has organized itself around a normative commitment, here the deal, and the lawyer has reduced the commitment to legal form. The form is law for that community. It will remain law unless and until a court is asked to read it and chooses to read it some other way.

The implication is that drafting is not clerical. Drafting is the act through which a layer of American private law comes into being. The country's commercial life is governed in large part by the documents transactional lawyers write, not by the cases courts decide. The cases are a thin and reactive overlay. The documents are the substrate.

## III. The doctrinal substance the drafter creates

A clause is not a string of words. A clause is a position on a doctrinal question. The drafter who does not know which question her clause is answering is making private law by accident.

The clearest illustration is the partnership-versus-loan question that runs from Roman commercial law to the present. Aquinas worked it out in the *Summa* as the distinction between *societas* and *mutuum*: capital deployed into a partnership at shared risk earns a return justly, capital extended as a loan at fixed interest is usurious. The Jewish tradition reached the same conclusion through independent reasoning, arriving at *shutafut* as the highest rung of *tzedakah* in Maimonides's ordering, and at the *isqa* and *heter isqa* as the practical-law mechanism that gives *shutafut* contractual form. The convergence is set out in detail in [Partnership at Shared Risk](/LEP/partnership-at-shared-risk/) and in the [Lineage](/LEP/lineage/) page.

The transactional lawyer who drafts a financing document is taking a position on that question whether or not she knows it. A preferred return that must be paid before the equity holders see a cent, with no genuine downside exposure for the holder of the preferred, is *mutuum* in equity clothing. A profits interest that pays only out of upside and bears its share of loss is *societas*. A SAFE that converts on a priced round but pays nothing if the company fails is closer to equity than to debt; a SAFE that promises a fixed payment in any liquidity event regardless of outcome is closer to debt than to equity. Every clause carries a doctrinal commitment. The drafter who knows the substance is constituting the relationship between the parties on a chosen ground. The drafter who does not is taking the doctrinal commitment of whoever wrote the template, often without recognizing that a commitment has been made.

This is what it means to say drafting is jurisgenerative in a substantive and not merely formal sense. The lawyer is not just writing words that bind. She is choosing, on behalf of her client and on behalf of the venture's counterparties, which of several possible bodies of doctrine the parties will live under. The choice is law-making at the level of doctrine, not just at the level of contract terms.

## IV. What the deskilling loop forecloses for this lawyer specifically

[The Deskilling Loop](/LEP/essays/deskilling-loop/) sets out the diagnosis across professions. The pattern is the same wherever it appears. Credentialed knowledge expands. Judgment migrates out of the professional's hands and into checklists, templates, supervisory layers, and software. The professional remains in the room. The act that defines the profession happens somewhere else.

Apply the pattern to the transactional lawyer specifically. Her work product, the operating agreement, the purchase agreement, the credit document, the security agreement, has been steadily reformatted around standardized templates. The American Bar Association supplies model forms. Practising Law Institute publishes annotated specimen agreements. The major law firms maintain internal precedent banks indexed and tagged for fast retrieval. Automated drafting platforms now generate first drafts from intake forms. Compliance and risk-management layers review the drafts to confirm conformity with firm and client policies. The associate who once wrote the clause now selects from a dropdown, accepts the suggested language, and routes the document to the reviewer.

The output looks similar. A clause appears on the page. The parties sign. The deal closes. The work is invisible to anyone reading the document afterward. The atrophy is in the lawyer.

The jurisgenerative act has been stripped out. The lawyer who selects from a dropdown is not making law in the Coverian sense. She is implementing law made by whoever wrote the template, somewhere else, at some earlier moment, for some other deal. The doctrinal position the clause carries is a position she has not chosen and may not recognize as a position. The community of meaning around the deal is being constituted by a *nomos* whose drafters are not in the room and may not be alive.

This is not a complaint about technology. The complaint is that an entire layer of American private law is being generated by people who have been deprived of the formation needed to recognize what they are doing. The country's commercial substrate is increasingly produced by professionals who experience themselves as processors of forms. The forms are doing the law-making. The professionals are doing the typing.

The atrophy of the drafting lawyer's judgment is the atrophy of an entire layer of American private law. The diagnosis in *The Deskilling Loop* applies to every profession it surveys. The version that applies to the transactional lawyer is uniquely consequential because the transactional lawyer's work is the law itself for the venture, not merely the application of law made elsewhere.

## V. What formation defends against

The professional formation that defends the jurisgenerative act has several components. They are not theoretical. They are the capacities a transactional lawyer must actually carry into a closing-room if she is to do the work the work requires.

The first is doctrinal command. The drafter must know what the clauses carry, not only how they look on the page. She must be able to say, of any clause she writes, which doctrinal question it answers and which answer it gives. The partnership-versus-loan question is one example. The agent-versus-principal question is another. The property-versus-contract question is a third. The license-versus-assignment question is a fourth. The substance is not optional. Without it the drafter is a typist for the template's author.

The second is *phronesis*, practical wisdom: the capacity to recognize when a deal's economics require a structure that no template anticipates. Most deals fit existing forms. Some do not. The lawyer who has been trained only on templates does not see the misfit, because the template is what she has been given to think with. The lawyer who has been formed in the substance can see when the form on the desk is the wrong form for the deal in the room, and she can build the right one. This is the capacity the templated practice cannot teach because the templated practice presupposes its absence.

The third is moral seriousness. Each clause is a small act of law-making with consequences for the parties' lives. The founder who signs the operating agreement is binding herself to the structure the lawyer drafted. The investor who signs the subscription agreement is committing capital under the terms the lawyer wrote. The employee who signs the equity grant is taking on the risk the lawyer specified. The lawyer is not transcribing. She is constituting the conditions of other people's commercial existence. The work calls for the kind of attention people give to something they take seriously, not the kind of attention people give to forms.

The Program is designed to form these capacities. The transactional clinic puts students in front of real deals where the doctrinal question matters and the template will not answer it. The simulation course puts students through a sequence of drafting exercises in which the deal's economics are deliberately mismatched to the standard form, so the student learns to see the misfit and to construct a fitted alternative. The cohort mentorship pairs students with practitioners who still carry the jurisgenerative habits because they were formed before the templating regime closed in. The institutional conditions that make such formation possible are set out in [The Three States of a Living Tradition](/LEP/essays/three-states/): a tradition does not transmit itself by accident, and the conditions for its transmission must be deliberately built.

## VI. The vocation, named

The transactional lawyer at the founding moment of a venture is a *sub-creator* in the older theological vocabulary, a *jurisgenerator* in the Coverian vocabulary, an *architect of the void* in the working paper of that name. The three vocabularies pick out the same person from three different angles. The theological vocabulary names the participation in a creative act not her own. The Coverian vocabulary names the act of generating legal meaning prior to its ratification by the state. The architectural vocabulary names the constructive work of giving shape to a structure where none had stood. All three describe the lawyer who constitutes the legal forms within which a commercial life can be lived.

The work has been done by lawyers for as long as there have been commercial ventures. The Roman jurists who wrote on the *exercitor navis* were doing it. The medieval Italian notaries who drafted the first *commenda* contracts were doing it. The English conveyancers who built the trust were doing it. The American securities lawyers who built the venture-capital partnership in the 1960s were doing it. The work is continuous with a tradition older than the United States and older than the modern profession. The present moment is distinctive only in that the work is being industrialized, automated, and stripped out of the lawyer's hands, with the result that a layer of private law is being generated by mechanisms that bear no relation to the formation the work requires.

The Program's pedagogical work is the formation of the lawyer who can still do the work. Not as nostalgia. Not as a romantic protest against the templating regime. As a practical commitment to the proposition that American commercial life needs lawyers who can constitute the legal forms within which ventures live, and that such lawyers must be formed deliberately, in conditions designed to form them, by teachers who themselves carry the capacities they aim to transmit. The vocation is the *jurisgenerator*. The formation is the work. The Program is the place where the work is done.
