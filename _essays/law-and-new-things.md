# Law and New Things

*Governing Markets and Machines with Moral Courage*

In May 1891, Leo XIII opened *Rerum Novarum* with a phrase. *Of new things*. The Latin shorthand for industrial transformation, and for the legal question the transformation forced. What does law owe the worker, the family, the propertied owner, the owner of nothing, when the conditions of production have shifted under all of them? Leo XIII answered with the framework that became the spine of modern Catholic social thought: labor and capital must hold together under shared moral conditions, the state's role is real but bounded, private property is a natural right with social obligations, the worker is not a commodity, and the poor have a claim that no economic regime can dissolve.

In May 2026, Leo XIV reopened the same question under different conditions. *Magnifica Humanitas* takes artificial intelligence as the present-day analogue to industrialization in 1891: a technological transformation that rearranges who works, who owns, who decides, and who is excluded. The encyclical does not announce a new doctrine. It restates the tradition under conditions that demand restatement.

The Program takes 2026–27 as the year to engage that arc. The year theme, agreed by the co-directors, is *[Entrepreneurship: Curing Poverty]({{ '/theme/' | relative_url }})*. The scholarly convening inside the year is a two-day invitation-only symposium at CUA Columbus Law, co-convened with Robert T. Miller (Scalia Law, George Mason), publishing in the *Catholic University Law Review*'s 2026–27 symposium edition. The working title of the symposium is *Law and New Things: Governing Markets and Machines with Moral Courage*. The thesis the year endorses is that entrepreneurship cures poverty; the symposium asks what law owes when the new forms of property, contract, competition, and regulation that the AI age has produced begin to reshape the conditions under which the cure can take.

## I. The hypothesis the symposium tests

*Magnifica Humanitas* ¶ 85 names three things AI can exacerbate: inequality, control, and exclusion. The encyclical asks the prior question whether technological innovations are neutral, and answers no. The doctrinal moves that follow rest on the trichotomy.

The symposium treats the trichotomy as a hypothesis to be tested, not a conclusion to be confirmed. Each term has its own analytic shape. Inequality is distributive: the gap between what one person commands and what another commands. Control is relational: who sets the terms under which the other acts. Exclusion is threshold: who falls below the floor of meaningful participation altogether. If the three terms hold as separable harms, each maps to a different doctrinal tool, and the legal question becomes whether the tool fits the harm. If the three terms collapse, or if the morality-of-markets and economic-growth literatures supply a stronger frame, the encyclical's analytic apparatus needs to be reshaped before it can carry legal weight.

This is what serious engagement with the Magisterium looks like in a law-review register. A Catholic university has a particular obligation to take papal teaching seriously, which means engaging it without presuming it. The symposium recruits scholars on both sides of every panel question.

## II. Why this arc, this year

Two features of the present moment make the *Rerum Novarum*-to-*Magnifica Humanitas* arc the natural frame for a 2026–27 LEP year.

The first is the citation chain. *Rerum Novarum* (1891) is the founding document of the modern Catholic social tradition. *Quadragesimo Anno* (1931) restated it at the fortieth anniversary. *Centesimus Annus* (1991) restated it at the hundredth. *Laborem Exercens* (1981), *Caritas in Veritate* (2009), *Laudato Si'* (2015), *Fratelli Tutti* (2020), and now *Magnifica Humanitas* (2026) are restatements of the same intellectual project under shifted conditions. A law-review symposium that names the arc names a 135-year continuous tradition of legal-and-economic reflection on what new technological conditions require of law. The tradition is not a foreign import to American legal scholarship. It is part of the scholarly inheritance that CUA Columbus exists to carry into the academy.

The second is the operational alignment. The deployment-site of AI governance is Washington. The federal apparatus that sets the legal speed limit on AI was substantially rebuilt between October 2023 and the present. President Biden's Executive Order 14110, which established the U.S. AI Safety Institute at NIST and a coordinating apparatus across the Executive Branch, was issued November 2023 and revoked January 23, 2025. President Trump's January 2025 Executive Order directed development of the AI Action Plan, released July 2025 and now in implementation. State-level enforcement has emerged in parallel: the Colorado AI Act of 2024, the California Privacy Protection Agency's expanded AI portfolio under the CPRA, New York City Local Law 144 on automated employment decision tools, and the Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act of 2025. The architecture of American AI law as it exists in 2026 is new, contested, and still settling. A symposium at the Hill, in the year the architecture is still settling, sits where the deployment-site engagement actually happens.

## III. The five panels

Each panel anchors on a doctrinal question that takes one of the three encyclical harms and reads it through an American legal vocabulary.

*Foundations: New Things, New Harms?* tests the encyclical's three-harm trichotomy directly. Is inequality, separately from control, separately from exclusion, the right way to break out the legal questions AI raises? Or do the morality-of-markets and economic-growth literatures (Hayek, Friedman, McCloskey, Novak, Gregg, Smith read whole) supply a frame that better tracks what is actually changing? Anchored by Catholic and classical-liberal natural-law scholars holding different positions, with the broader CST jurisprudence bench behind each anchor.

*New Forms of Property: Data, Algorithms, and Intellectual Property* takes *Magnifica Humanitas* ¶ 67 at its word. Patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure, and data are the new forms of property the universal destination of goods now reaches. Two live debates run inside the question: whether personal data should be propertized given new cryptographic infrastructure, and whether intellectual property is a natural right or a utilitarian state grant. Anchored by Lockean and natural-rights IP scholars on one side and utilitarian and commons-tradition scholars on the other.

*New Forms of Contract: Automated Architecture and Voluntary Exchange* takes the *control* term. Can contract doctrine, the unconscionability and duress thresholds, the standard-form and AI-mediated assent doctrines, be re-tuned to address relational control without smuggling distributive conclusions into the threshold question? What is the correct definition of economic coercion when one party negotiates against a machine? Anchored by promissory and classical-liberal contract theorists and by the law-and-political-economy school.

*New Forms of Competition: Dynamic Markets and Static Antitrust* asks which of the three harms antitrust is built to address, and whether the consumer-welfare standard is miscalibrated for foundation-model concentration and innovation-intensive markets. The classical-liberal narrow-antitrust reading, the dynamic-markets innovation-focused reading, and the neo-Brandeisian structural reading each get an anchor.

*New Rules, New Regulators: AI Governance in DC* is the CLE-credit practitioner panel. The reach is broad by design: the Executive Office of the President; the U.S. AI Safety Institute at NIST and the Bureau of Industry and Security on chip export controls; the DOJ Antitrust Division and the FTC; sectoral federal agencies on credit, hiring, medical devices, capital markets, and banking; the Defense and intelligence apparatus; state-level enforcement in California, Colorado, New York, and Texas; in-house policy heads at the major frontier AI labs; plaintiffs' counsel from the training-data and bias dockets; the federal judiciary; Hill staff; and the policy-engagement think tanks. The panel reaches the people who actually operate the legal speed limit.

## IV. What the symposium is not

The symposium is not a critique of capitalism, not an integralist project, not a general-interest AI-policy panel, not a repeat of any prior antitrust conference, and not an exercise in confirming what the encyclical concludes. The frame is internal to a market-oriented jurisprudence. The historical record on automation and economic growth gets taken seriously, including the data showing dramatic reductions in extreme poverty alongside dramatic increases in within-country inequality across the long industrial arc. The Catholic intellectual provenance is visible in the frame and unforced in the substance.

The point is that the question Leo XIII opened in 1891 has not closed. The 2026 conditions are not the 1891 conditions, but the analytic structure of the question, what does law owe under technological transformation, is the same structure. Lawyers who learn to ask the question in its 2026 form acquire a vocabulary that will be useful well beyond any single panel debate.

## V. How this serves the spine

The symposium serves two elements of the Program's argument simultaneously.

It serves [element II]({{ '/argument/' | relative_url }}) because the *Magnifica Humanitas* restatement of partnership at shared risk under AI conditions is the present-day substantive content of the norm that the Program teaches its students to draft to. The lineage of the norm is at [The Moral Anatomy of Enterprise]({{ '/lineage/' | relative_url }}). The 2026 statement of the norm under AI conditions is *Magnifica Humanitas*. The symposium reads the encyclical at the doctrinal resolution the Program's drafting work requires.

It serves [element IV]({{ '/argument/' | relative_url }}) because the AI Governance in DC panel puts the deployment-site engagement at the center of an academic event. The lawyer trained in the recovered methods who never encounters the regulators, the special masters, the Hill staff, the agency rulemakers, and the plaintiffs' bar has been formed for a setting that no longer exists. The symposium brings that setting into the room.

The diagnosis under which the year-theme makes urgent sense is at [The Deskilling Loop]({{ '/essays/deskilling-loop/' | relative_url }}). The AI-era restatement of the substantive norm is at [Magnifica Humanitas and the Babel Question]({{ '/essays/magnifica-babel/' | relative_url }}). The Holy Language reading of the encyclical's two cities is at [The Aleph Bet of A.I.]({{ '/essays/aleph-bet-of-ai/' | relative_url }}). The symposium gathers the scholars and the practitioners who are working the same problem from inside the American legal academy and the federal regulatory apparatus at the same time.

## VI. The publication target

The symposium publishes in the *Catholic University Law Review*'s 2026–27 volume as a symposium edition. The journal's editorial identity is exactly the identity the symposium serves. The citation form will be *Law and New Things Symposium*, 76 Cath. U. L. Rev. ___ (2027). Draft papers are due October 15, 2026; the conference convenes in November or December 2026; final manuscripts are due March 31, 2027.

The hosting is the Program's first major scholarly convening. It will not be the last. The intellectual structure is built to recur on a long cadence: each year, a panel of new things, a panel of new forms of property, a panel of new forms of contract, a panel of new forms of competition, and a panel of new rules and new regulators. The substantive content shifts with the conditions. The analytic structure does not. *Of new things*, in every year there are new things, and the law has to answer for what it owes under each.
