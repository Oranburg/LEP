# The Third Place: A Lawyer Reads the Pope in the Wilderness

*Seth C. Oranburg*

The bird was banging on my window again. I think he is an oriole, but I am not much of an ornithologist, so I named him Bangy Bird, which I admit is not a name that required much of me. Bangy Bird visits most evenings when I work late in the basement. He bangs on the glass for a while and leaves when it is fully dark.

It took me a while to notice the silence, because I had a half dozen things going at once. On the screen were a stack of unanswered emails, a motion for judicial dissolution of an uneconomic trust, and *Magnifica Humanitas*, Pope Leo XIV's new encyclical on safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence. And in two other windows I had ChatGPT open for proofreading and Westlaw's AI tool running a deep search for recent cases on social-media liabilities for fraud. I was, in other words, building a small Babel of my own while reading the warning against it, and I did not notice the irony any more than I noticed the bird had stopped.

When I did notice, the basement was dark. I got up to get away from the glare, dug my Bible out of a pile on the desk, and sat down in the armchair to read the week's Torah portion. It was *Bamidbar*, which means "in the wilderness," the opening of the Book of Numbers. The Israelites are circumambulating Sinai, and the book begins, as you would expect of a book the translators called Numbers, with a census. God tells Moses to count everyone wandering in the wasteland.

My mind drifted back to the encyclical, because *Magnifica Humanitas* is about the opposite of a wasteland. It is about cities. Two cities. Leo sets them against each other on the first page and builds the whole letter on the contrast. "Humanity," he writes, "is today facing a pivotal choice: either to construct a new Tower of Babel or to build the city in which God and humanity dwell together." Babel is the city built without God: one language, one technology, one direction, a tower meant to reach heaven and to make its builders a name. Jerusalem, rebuilt under Nehemiah, is the city raised from ruin by ordinary people, each family assigned its own section of the wall. The choice, Leo says, "is not between a 'yes' or 'no' to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem."

Maybe it is strange that a desert made me think of cities. But the census was boring me, so I put Numbers down and turned back to Genesis 11, to the tower itself. I did not know yet that I would have to come back to the desert. I thought I was leaving it.

I do not read Hebrew quickly. I know the alphabet, I go to shul, I get through the pointed text slowly with a finger on the line. But I know one thing about these letters that is worth knowing, which is that in the tradition they are not only sounds. They are supposed to be the material God used. The world was spoken into being, and the letters are the speech. You are allowed, on that view, to look at a word and ask what it is made of, the way you might ask what a wall is made of. So I looked.

The name Babel is explained inside the story that names it. When God scatters the builders, the verse says he called the place Babel "because there the Lord confused" their speech. The Hebrew for confused is *balal*. The city's own name is the confusion it produced. That alone is worth a minute. But the thing I had never seen before was sitting one letter away.

Babel in Hebrew is three letters: *bet, bet, lamed*. Abel, the second son of Adam, the shepherd Cain killed, is also three letters: *hey, bet, lamed*. The two words share their last two letters and differ only at the front. Abel's name, הבל (*Hevel*), means breath, or vapor, the same word Ecclesiastes uses for "vanity of vanities." Replace the breath at the front of Abel with a second wall and you have Babel.

The wall is the right word, because ב (*bet*) is the house. It is the first letter of the Torah, the letter creation begins with, and its shape is a little enclosure open on one side. A house is defined by its opening; that is what makes it a house and not a box. Babel doubles the *bet*, and two houses pushed front to front make no opening at all. Between them stands the ל (*lamed*), the tallest letter, the one that rises above the line of the script, the letter that turns a noun into a reaching: *lilmod*, to learn, *lishmor*, to guard. In Babel the reaching letter is sealed between two walls with nowhere to climb. And the breath, the ה (*hey*), the softest sound a person makes, barely more than an exhale, the open letter through which something from outside might enter, is exactly the letter Babel replaces with another wall.

This is what Leo is afraid of, and Hebrew draws the fear at the scale of a single letter. AI becomes Babel when it is an algorithm with no person left in the loop: a structure that learns only from what it has already produced, a house sealed on both sides, the reaching letter trapped inside a room that can only echo. We have all sat in front of the machine that has read everything and has nothing to tell us we did not already know. That is the *lamed* between two walls. That is learning when the breath has been bricked over.

Leo does not stop at the warning. He turns it into a question. Looking at the great "construction sites" of the age, he asks plainly, "What are we building?" His answer is the other city. And Hebrew has something to say about that city too.

Jerusalem first shows up in the Torah under a shorter name. A king called Melchizedek rules a place called Salem, שלם (*Shalem*), which means whole, or at peace. Where the longer name comes from is disputed; I am in no position to adjudicate it. But one reading I came across turns on a verb that appears when God finally answers Job out of the storm. God asks Job where he was "when I laid the earth's foundations," and then, "who set its cornerstone, when the morning stars sang together?" The word for set, there, is ירה (*yarah*). On the reading I found, Jerusalem is *yarah* and *shalem* together: the setting of the cornerstone of peace.

What struck me in the Job passage is not the etymology but the picture. God does not describe creation to Job as a construction project. He describes it as a song the morning stars sang together. Leo, quoting the Psalms, says the city's cornerstone will be "the 'rejected stones' — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us." The stone the builders threw away is the one the city is founded on. A city founded that way is not stacked. It is sung. It is, as Leo keeps insisting, an undertaking that "rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones," and it is never finished, because bringing people together is not the kind of thing that finishes.

So far this is the encyclical's own scheme, confirmed letter by letter: a sealed city to refuse, an open city to build. I could have stopped there. The reason I did not is the book I had set down.

Leo answers his question, "what are we building," with a tool, so I went looking for the Hebrew word for tool. It is *keli*, and it turns up everywhere in the Bible, more than three hundred times. Its first appearance is Esau's hunting weapons; it is also the word for the sacred vessels of the tabernacle. The same word covers the bow and the holy instruments. Tools are not the problem, and I think the Pope would agree, because he is not asking whether we should use them. He is asking what kind of dwelling we are building with them, and for whom.

There is a more specific Hebrew word for the kind of tool I had in mind, though. *Golem*. The made man of clay with a name written on parchment and set under its tongue or across its forehead, animated and powerful and unable to answer for anything it does. The rabbi most associated with the legend is the Maharal of Prague, in the sixteenth century, though the honest version of the story is murkier than the famous one. The word itself just means *unformed*. It is in the Psalms, where the writer says God saw his *golem*, his unformed substance, before any of his days existed. A *golem* is a thing that is half made. A formed thing without a soul. A thing that can act and cannot be held to account.

I looked up the modern Hebrew for artificial intelligence. It is בינה מלאכותית (*bina melakhutit*), two words. The first, בינה (*bina*), is understanding, which was one of the two things God gave Solomon when Solomon asked not for riches but for a heart that could judge his people wisely. The second is the adjective form of מלאכה (*melakha*), which means work, or craft. Artificial intelligence, in Hebrew, is the intelligence of craft, the understanding that is made rather than born.

What I noticed, looking at the second word, is that its root is the root of another word I do know: מלאך (*malach*), the Hebrew for messenger, which the Christian Bible translates as angel. The made intelligence and the angel share their letters, because both are sent. Both have a job. Neither chooses. In the tradition an angel has no evil inclination, which sounds like a virtue until you notice it also has no good one. It cannot disobey, cannot repent, cannot love, cannot enter a covenant, because to enter a covenant, as the tradition has it, you have to be free to break it. An angel performs the mission of whoever sent it, exactly, and the only question that has ever mattered about an angel is who did the sending.

Leo runs through what the machine cannot do. It does "not undergo experiences," does "not feel joy or pain," does not "have a moral conscience." The full list in the encyclical is longer than mine. He is right about every line of it, and the Hebrew root is older than his sentence. A thing that executes a mission without a conscience is a thing the tradition already has a name for. The name is the angel. And I know what a lawyer does. The lawyer writes the instructions the messenger carries. The contracts I draft tell the algorithm what to do and when. *Shall* is a different country from *may*. The clause governs the machine, and the machine does not pause to ask whether the clause is wise. It runs. The lawyer is the one writing the angel's instructions. I had not put that together before. It is not a discovery; it is the kind of thing a person notices when he finally looks down. The Hebrew had been there the whole time. I had not, until that night, looked.

And then I had to go back to the desert, because the desert was the thing I had set down, and it would not stay set down.

Leo gives us two cities. Babel and Jerusalem, the tower and the wall, the sealed house and the open one. Both are building projects; he calls them that himself. But the book on my desk that night was about neither. It was about the place between them, and the place between two cities is not a third city. It is the wilderness. The most important thing that ever happened to my people happened there, in the מדבר (*midbar*), on no one's land. The rabbis ask why the Torah was given in the desert and not in a city or a field that belonged to somebody, and the answer they give is that the desert is הפקר (*hefker*). Ownerless. It belongs to no tribe and no king and no company, and so anyone willing to make himself ownerless like the wilderness can receive what is given there. I had heard that before. I had not connected it to the census I was bored by, which is the first thing the Torah does in that ownerless place: it counts the people one at a time, by name, by family, lifting each head. Not bricks, which is what Babel counts and mourns when one falls. Not a wall, which is what Jerusalem will eventually need. Before the people build anything at all, in the place no one owns, they are counted by name.

I looked, later, at the word *midbar*, wilderness, and the word *davar*, which means word or speech, because they looked alike on the page, and they do share a root. I will not make more of that than I can fairly claim. But it stayed with me that the place of speech, in Hebrew, and the place where nothing is owned are written with the same letters, and that the word was given in the wilderness and not in either city.

Here is what the desert told me that the two cities could not. Both cities are images. The ground we are standing on is wilderness. We are in it right now. The age of these machines is a wilderness, ownerless in the bad sense, because no one will quite say who is accountable for what the messenger does. But ownerless can also be the good kind. *Hefker* is the condition in which something can still be received, because the ground has not yet been walled off and assigned. Leo opens a window in his encyclical and holds it open through the whole letter. What I had not seen until I picked the desert back up is that the window does not open onto Jerusalem. Not yet. It opens onto a wilderness, a narrow ownerless stretch you have to cross before any city is possible, the place where you are counted by name before you are set into a wall. You do not get to skip it. The choice Leo describes is real, but it is made by people who are willing to stand, first, in the place that belongs to no one and be counted there.

Leo's positive figure, I notice last, is Nehemiah, who was a Jewish layman in a Persian king's court, cupbearer to Artaxerxes. He took leave from his post in someone else's palace to rebuild a wall in someone else's city. The position the encyclical commends, it turns out, is the position I am writing from.

I closed the laptop. I do not remember when the bird stopped banging; the silence had been there a while. The basement was full of the small hum of devices on standby, the refrigerator upstairs cycling on, the house settling. I left the encyclical and the Bible side by side on the desk and went up.

Tomorrow there is a deposition in the morning, and after it a clause to revise on a platform-services agreement, and a client who wants to know whether the wording will hold up if it is litigated in three jurisdictions and translated into two languages. I am not going to pretend I will draft that clause as though the letters of Babel were standing behind me, because they were not standing behind me tonight either. I was a man in a basement looking at a printout. But the clause is a wall or it is an opening, depending on the wording, and the wording is my job.

The Talmud says the first question at the heavenly court is whether you dealt faithfully in business. נשאת ונתת באמונה (*Nasata v'natata b'emunah*). I notice tonight that the question is partly about the clause.

I think I will draft it with one less wall in it than I would have drafted yesterday. Then the next one. Then the next. I do not know what the sum will be. It is the only honest place I have to start, and it is, I notice, the place the desert starts too: not with a city, but with one head lifted and counted, and then the next.

The bird will be back tomorrow evening. He bangs for a while and leaves when it is fully dark, and he will not know that he is the reason I noticed the silence and the dark, and he will not care. He will be a bird at a window. The Hebrew word for window is חלון (*chalon*). I had to look that up too.
