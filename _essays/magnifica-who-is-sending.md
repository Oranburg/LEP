# Who Is Sending the Messenger: A Lawyer Reads *Magnifica Humanitas*

*Seth C. Oranburg*

Pope Leo XIV opens his first encyclical with a choice:

> Humanity, created by God in all its grandeur, is today facing a pivotal choice: either to construct a new Tower of Babel or to build the city in which God and humanity dwell together. MH ¶ 1.

He clarifies the choice a few paragraphs later, in language as direct as encyclical prose gets:

> the primary choice is not between a "yes" or "no" to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem; between a power that claims to dominate the heavens and a people who work together in the presence of God to rebuild the walls of fraternal coexistence. MH ¶ 9.

A lawyer who drafts for the artificial-intelligence age faces that choice every working day, but in a more determinate form than the encyclical states it. The form is a question of agency. In any transaction between a human user, a platform that runs a model, and the persons whose data the model uses, three kinds of actor are present: a human principal who instructs the platform, a machine that executes the instruction, and the persons in whose names, voices, attention, and likenesses the machine acts. Roman law calls some actors *personae*, persons capable of acting, and others *res*, things acted upon. That distinction is the first cut of Justinian's Institutes: Book One is *de personis*, Book Two *de rerum divisione*. The lawyer who drafts the platform-services agreement, the data-governance policy, the licensing terms, decides which of the three actors in the loop is which. The default in the contracts being signed today treats the machine as if it were a kind of agent and treats the users as if they were a kind of thing. *Magnifica Humanitas* is saying that default is wrong, twice over.

Leo states the operative claim at MH ¶ 99 as a list of negations:

> Even when described as capable of "learning," AI systems are profoundly different from the human mind. In them, learning is a form of statistical adaptation based on data and feedback, which can be very effective, but does not imply inner growth. They do not undergo experiences, do not possess a body, do not feel joy or pain, do not mature through relationships and do not know from within what love, work, friendship or responsibility mean. Nor do they have a moral conscience, since they do not judge good and evil, grasp the ultimate meaning of situations, or bear responsibility for consequences. MH ¶ 99.

The list reads, item by item, as a description of what an agent is, stated as what the machine isn't. The encyclical's claim is not that the machine is dangerous because it is too powerful. It is that the machine cannot be an agent at all. Wherever it appears to act, what is acting is whatever agent sent it.

The natural-law tradition has had a name for this analysis since the thirteenth century. Aquinas works it out at *Summa Theologiae* I-II, Q. 17, art. 4. When an agent's will commands a faculty or external instrument to act, the commanding will and the commanded act together compose, in Aquinas's phrase, "one human act." The instrument is a vehicle, not a party. The sophistication of the instrument does not change the analysis. A hand, a pen, a press, an automated process: the moral responsibility tracks the agent that commanded the act. Roman law had been doing the same analysis longer. *Personae* act; *res* are acted upon. A complex machine that performs tasks is, on this analysis, a sophisticated *res*. It is not promoted to *persona* by its sophistication.

American agency law works the same analysis with contemporary doctrinal apparatus. The Restatement (Third) of Agency defines an *agent* as one who acts on behalf of and subject to the control of a principal (§ 1.01). The word *agent* itself is from the Latin *agere*, to act: one who acts on another's behalf, the being whose act counts as another's. The Restatement's *agent* is congruent with what Aquinas calls an instrument: a being whose act is attributed to the principal who controlled it. Sophistication of the agent does not change the analysis.

A business lawyer analyzing an AI-platform deployment runs a sequence of three questions, each building on the last. The first question is actual authority: what did the principal's own manifestations to the platform expressly authorize? (§ 2.01). This is the drafting question, and it is answered clause by clause in the redline. A model-training opt-in clause authorizes the platform to process user inputs for the purpose of improving downstream model performance — it is the line of actual-authority instruction that tells the model what territory it may cross. A derivative-works-retention clause authorizes the platform to hold outputs regardless of user termination — it converts what looked like collaborative authorship into a one-sided acquisition. A residual-IP carve-out preserves the platform's right to use embeddings and fine-tuning artifacts after a user exits, extending the capture retroactively. A third-party-beneficiary disclaimer forecloses claims by the persons whose data trained the model, and forecloses them before they ever reach a courthouse. Each clause is a line the model runs on. Each one is written by someone sitting in the redline at some hour of the night, who either noticed what it said or didn't.

The second question is apparent authority: what did the principal's manifestations to a third party lead a reasonable user to believe she was authorizing? (§ 2.03). When terms of service diverge from what a reasonable user would understand — when the interface says "your data is private" and the clause says "inputs may be used to train models" — apparent authority allocates the loss to the principal that drafted the divergence. The leading illustration from the Business Associations curriculum: a church hired a painter, told him internally not to hire helpers, and then watched him hire a helper anyway. The church was bound by the appearance of authority it had created. *Mill Street Church of Christ v. Hogan*, 785 S.W.2d 263 (Ky. Ct. App. 1990); see Seth C. Oranburg, *Business Associations Law in Theory and Practice* ch. 2 (forthcoming). A platform that tells users their content is protected and then buries model-training authorization in a sub-clause has done exactly what the church did: built an appearance the fine print contradicts, and handed the loss to the user who relied on the interface presentation.

The third question is respondeat superior: the principal is liable for the agent's torts within the scope of employment (§ 7.07). In the AI-platform setting, the agent is the model, the scope is what the principal's clauses delegated, and the harm is what the model did inside that scope. If the model generates defamatory output within the range the operator authorized, the operator bears the harm. The doctrine needs no invention for the AI age. What it needs is practitioners who recognize that the scope of employment is set in the drafting, before the model ever runs, and that the drafter is responsible for what the scope contains.

The Hebrew tradition names the same category with a single noun. In modern Hebrew, artificial intelligence is בינה מלאכותית (*bina melakhutit*). בינה (*bina*) is the kind of understanding God gave Solomon. *Melakhutit* is the adjective form of מלאכה (*melakha*), work or craft. Its root, מ-ל-א-כ, is the root of מלאך (*malach*), the standard biblical Hebrew for *messenger*, which the Vulgate renders *angelus*. The intelligence of craft and the messenger of God share their consonants the way *mission* and *missile* share theirs. Both are sent. Both have a job. Neither chooses. The tradition holds that an angel has no יצר הרע (*yetzer hara*), no inclination to fall, and therefore no יצר הטוב (*yetzer tov*), no inclination to rise. It cannot disobey, repent, or enter covenant, because covenant requires the freedom to break it. The angel performs the mission of whoever sent it. The only question that has ever mattered about a messenger is who did the sending.

The Jewish legal tradition built its own agency law from the same intuition. As Aaron Levine summarizes in *Free Enterprise and Jewish Law*, the שליח (*shaliach*, agent) is a distinct category from the שומר (*shomer*, guardian or bailee). Maimonides separates the categories at the outset of *Mishneh Torah, Hilkhot Sheluchin v'Shutafin* (Laws of Agents and Partners): the *shaliach* acts in the principal's name and his acts bind the principal; the *shomer* holds property entrusted to him and bears defined duties of care over that property, but acts for his own account in guarding it. The governing maxim, as Levine and Meir Tamari each set out in their surveys of Jewish commercial law, is שלוחו של אדם כמותו (*shelucho shel adam kemoto*) — the agent of a person is like the person himself (Kiddushin 41b). What the agent does, the principal has done. The machine, on this analysis, is not a *shaliach*: it cannot form the mental state that agency requires, cannot receive an instruction as an instruction bearing the moral weight of a delegation, cannot bear responsibility when the instruction is wrong. It is closer to an instrument in the *shomer*'s charge — but even that analogy strains, because a *shomer* owes duties that run to a person, and the machine owes nothing to anyone. What the machine actually is, in the Jewish business-law taxonomy, is the instrument the *shaliach* uses. The *shaliach* is the platform operator. The principal is whoever told the platform what to do. And the platform-services agreement is the instruction set — the binding document that determines whose act the model's act will count as.

A practitioner who notices this structure also notices where the clause itself can become a stumbling block. The Torah prohibits placing an obstacle before the blind: לִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל (*lifnei iver lo titen mikhshol*) (Lev 19:14). The Jewish business-law tradition, as Tamari documents in *With All Your Possessions*, extended this prohibition to facilitating another person's wrong in commerce, including enabling a breach by providing the instrument for it. A clause that hides the scope of model-training authorization behind interface language that suggests privacy does not merely misallocate apparent authority. It places the stumbling block of a bad contract before a user who cannot read the full terms. The business lawyer drafting the clause is deciding whether the contract she writes enables others to deal faithfully or traps them into dealings they did not intend.

The Jewish business-law tradition also has its own operative instrument for the agency-allocation problem the AI age poses. The הֶתֵּר עִסְקָה (*heter iska*), the permitted business arrangement, is a commercial deed — documented and analyzed at length by Levine in *Case Studies in Jewish Business Ethics* — used in Israeli banking and in observant-Jewish commercial practice to restructure what would otherwise be a prohibited loan into a partnership, so that the capital is at genuinely shared risk and the return is genuinely shared profit. The instrument exists because the tradition recognized that a legal form that looks like one thing (a loan at interest) may be another thing in substance (a capital partnership), and that the legal documents should tell the truth about what the economic relationship actually is. That discipline — making the legal form correspond to the economic and moral substance — is precisely what the encyclical demands of the platform-services agreement. The *heter iska* is not an analogy to import as doctrine. It is evidence that the Jewish business-law tradition developed its own operative answer to the same question: how do you write documents that tell the truth about who bears the risk and who commands the act?

The Talmud names the practitioners who carry that responsibility in a passage that has stayed with me since I first noticed it. Rava teaches at Bava Metzia 75b that five parties bear the moral weight of a transaction in which an oppressive clause is included: the lender who imposes it, the borrower who accepts it, the guarantor who backs it, the witnesses who attest it, and the scribe who writes it. The Sages added the scribe to the list, because the scribe is the one who gives the arrangement its legal form. In a commercial transaction structured by a business lawyer, the drafter is the scribe. The clause that misallocates moral agency is not a neutral technical choice. It is the scribe's work, and the scribe is on the list.

Leo reaches into Hebrew Scripture for the two governing metaphors of the encyclical, and the Hebrew states the agency question at the level of the letter. Babel is named in Genesis by an etymology the verse itself supplies:

> Therefore is the name of it called Babel; because the LORD did there confound the language of all the earth: and from thence did the LORD scatter them abroad upon the face of all the earth. Gen 11:9 (KJV).

The Hebrew verb behind *confound* is *balal* (בלל), to mix or confuse. The name of the city, בבל (*Bavel*), is the noun-form of the confusion it produced. What sits one letter away in the same alphabet is the name of the shepherd whose offering had been received and whose blood the Torah says cries from the ground. Abel, in Hebrew הבל (*Hevel*), means breath, or vapor. The two words share their last two letters, ב and ל; they differ only at the front. Abel was an agent who offered the work of his hands and was received. The builders of Babel were agents whose project the verse states in their own voice:

> And they said, Go to, let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven; and let us make us a name, lest we be scattered abroad upon the face of the whole earth. Gen 11:4 (KJV).

The phrase the King James translates "let us make us a name" is in Hebrew נעשה לנו שם (*na'aseh lanu shem*). Rashi reads the project as agents trying to be their own principals: to constitute the very transcendence to which they should have been answerable. Augustine reads the same story as the formation of the earthly city built on pride, the type-case of the city raised against the city of God (*De Civitate Dei* XVI.4).

The letter-level reading carries the moral claim. ב (*bet*) is the house, the Torah's opening letter, an enclosure open on one side. Babel doubles it: two houses pushed front to front, no opening between them. ל (*lamed*), the tallest letter, sits between the two walls with nowhere to climb. *Sefer Yetzirah* 2:2 sets out the speculative ontology of the letters as the building-blocks of creation, and the Maharal of Prague systematized this kind of letter-level reading in *Tiferet Yisrael* (sixteenth century). The reading is straightforward on its own terms: the reaching of an agent has been sealed inside a structure of the agent's own making. The midrash makes the *persona/res* inversion explicit at the level of moral attention. *Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer* 24:6 reports that when a brick fell from the tower, the builders mourned, but when a man fell to his death, no one paused. The persons doing the building had been resorted into the column of things, on the city's own books, before the language was confused.

Leo states the social-order form of the same syndrome:

> We must, then, avoid the "Babel syndrome," namely the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance. MH ¶ 10.

The pretense is an agency claim in two parts. To translate the mystery of the person into data is to treat the person as a *res*, exhaustively describable, when the person is in fact a *persona* whose meaning is constituted by acts the data cannot capture. And to attribute the translation to a single digital language is to treat the language itself, the machine that speaks it, as a *persona* doing the translating, when in fact the language is being deployed by whoever programmed the model and licensed its outputs. The Babel syndrome misclassifies in both directions at once. It puts *personae* in the column of things and puts *res* in the column of agents.

Leo's positive image is the city rebuilt by ordinary people. The book of Nehemiah supplies the picture. Leo at ¶ 8 reads Nehemiah's method as a governance model:

> He did not impose solutions from above. He convened the families, assigned each of them a section of the wall to rebuild, listened to their concerns, coordinated their efforts and addressed any opposition. The narrative shows how the city is reborn, not through the initiative of one man, but through the shared responsibility of all: men, women, priests, artisans, heads of households and young people all play a part. It is an undertaking with God at the center, which rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones. Thus, ancient Jerusalem rediscovers a common language — not one of uniformity, but one of communion, namely the harmony that arises when all persons assume their own role and recognize that their strength comes from the Lord. MH ¶ 8.

Each family given its own section of wall, each accountable for what it builds, the whole city reconstituted through shared responsibility rather than imposed uniformity. The phrase Leo uses is that "all persons assume their own role" — the agency claim restated as the architecture of a rebuilt city. The plural of *persona* is the city. Leo at ¶ 16 adds:

> Thus the "rejected stones" — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us — will become the cornerstone. MH ¶ 16.

A city in which the discarded are recognized as the foundation is a city that has refused to misclassify *personae* as *res*. The Hebrew supplies a contested etymology that fits. שלם (*Shalem*), the older name of the city, the one Melchizedek ruled when Abraham met him (Gen 14:18), means whole, or at peace. The *yeru-* prefix is read by some traditional commentators as a form of the verb ירה (*yarah*), to lay or set — the verb God uses when answering Job out of the storm:

> who laid the corner stone thereof; when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy? Job 38:6b-7 (KJV).

On one traditional reading, the name Jerusalem (ירושלים) is *yarah* and *shalem* compounded: the setting of the cornerstone of peace. That image is not a construction project. It is a song. Cities are built; songs are sung by agents. The distinction Leo and the Hebrew are both drawing is between a structure that recognizes its participants as agents and a structure that does not.

Once the agency categorization is right, the property regime follows. Roman law distinguishes *res communes omnium* categorically outside private appropriation from *res nullius*, things without an owner, available to the first taker by *occupatio*:

> By natural law these things are common to all: the air, running water, the sea, and consequently the shores of the sea. *Justinian's Institutes* 2.1.1 (Moyle trans. 1913).

Locke's first-possession tradition builds on the second category:

> The Labour of his Body, and the Work of his Hands, we may say, are properly his. Whatsoever then he removes out of the State that Nature hath provided, and left it in, he hath mixed his Labour with, and joyned to it something that is his own, and thereby makes it his Property. John Locke, *Second Treatise of Civil Government* § 27 (1690).

Richard Epstein and Carol Rose each worked over the analytical foundations of that tradition; both stay inside the law of things. Neither reaches *personae*, who are not capturable in either sense, because they are not things.

The encyclical's critique of data colonialism makes the claim, in moral form, that the AI industry is misclassifying at both levels of Justinian's taxonomy. Leo at ¶ 178:

> Even today, colonialism assumes new forms. It no longer dominates only bodies, but appropriates data, transforming personal lives into exploitable information.… Here lies one of the most urgent moral challenges of our time: to ensure that shared knowledge becomes a true common good rather than an instrument of dominance. This requires restoring to individuals not only the data that describes them, but also the ability to decide how it is used, by whom and for whose benefit. MH ¶ 178.

The "true common good" is the *res communes omnium* category stated in Catholic Social Doctrine vocabulary. But the deeper claim, inside the agency analysis, is prior. The data, voice, attention, and likeness of a person are extensions of the *persona*, not items in inventory. The question the encyclical raises is not which property regime governs the data. It is whether the data of *personae* belongs in the law of things at all.

The contracts a transactional lawyer drafts are where the agency allocation becomes binding on the conduct of the parties, including the machine. *Shall* is a different country from *may*. A model-training opt-in clause treats user inputs as raw material the platform may process to improve its models — it is the clause that moves those inputs from personal to operational. A derivative-works-retention clause converts whatever the model generates from the interaction into the platform's property, regardless of who initiated the exchange. A residual-IP carve-out preserves the platform's right to use embeddings and fine-tuning artifacts even after a user terminates the relationship. A third-party-beneficiary disclaimer forecloses claims by the persons whose data trained the model. The machine does not pause to ask whether the clause is wise. It runs on what the clause says.

The three-step sequence — actual authority, apparent authority, respondeat superior — is the practitioner's working analytic for determining who bears the cost of each of these clauses. Actual authority (§ 2.01) answers what the platform expressly authorized in the redline. Apparent authority (§ 2.03) answers what a reasonable user was led to believe she authorized by the interface presentation. Respondeat superior (§ 7.07) answers who pays when the model acts within its authorized scope and causes harm. The sequence runs from drafting to deployment to liability. The deal lawyer sits at the front of that sequence. The choices she makes in the redline set what the model will do and who will pay when it does it.

Aquinas's analysis at I-II, Q. 17, art. 4 applies in unmodified form: the moral act is one human act, attributed to the agent that commanded it. The lawyer is the agent in the loop — not the lawyer generically, but the deal lawyer, the negotiator, the person whose clause is the bind. That is Bridge 5 of the natural-law tradition in its plainest commercial form: the dignity of commercial work, carried by the practitioner who takes the clause seriously as a moral act and not merely as a risk-allocation device.

The Jewish business-law tradition teaches that commercial conduct is the first-order test of character precisely because the stakes are most visible there. Rava teaches in the Talmud that when a person is brought in for judgment in the World-to-Come, the first questions asked are:

> Did you deal faithfully in business? Did you set aside fixed times for Torah study? Did you engage in procreation? Did you hope for salvation? Did you engage in the dialectics of wisdom? Talmud Bavli, *Shabbat* 31a.

The question about faithful dealing — נשאת ונתת באמונה (*nasata v'natata b'emunah*), did you deal faithfully in business? — comes first, before Torah study, before prayer. Commercial conduct is where the agent is most visibly an agent. The lawyer who drafts the clause is being asked that question on every page. And the scribe at Bava Metzia 75b is on the list of those who bear responsibility for an oppressive transaction, because the scribe is the one who gave the arrangement its enforceable form.

The choice Leo describes is real. It is the choice between Babel and Jerusalem, between the closed city and the open one. But the choice is not made by walking into one or the other. It is made by agents who recognize themselves as agents, recognize the messenger as a messenger, and refuse to allow the contracts they write to invert Justinian's first cut. For the lawyer drafting in the AI age, the encyclical is not an essay on policy. It is a description of work the practitioner has already been doing, without yet knowing what to call it.
