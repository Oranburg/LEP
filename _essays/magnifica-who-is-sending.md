# Who Is Sending the Messenger: A Lawyer Reads *Magnifica Humanitas*

*Seth C. Oranburg*

Pope Leo XIV opens his first encyclical with a choice:

> Humanity, created by God in all its grandeur, is today facing a pivotal choice: either to construct a new Tower of Babel or to build the city in which God and humanity dwell together. MH ¶ 1.

He clarifies the choice a few paragraphs later, in language as direct as encyclical prose gets:

> the primary choice is not between a "yes" or "no" to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem; between a power that claims to dominate the heavens and a people who work together in the presence of God to rebuild the walls of fraternal coexistence. MH ¶ 9.

A lawyer who drafts for the artificial-intelligence age faces that choice every working day, but in a more determinate form than the encyclical states it. The form is a question of agency. In any transaction between a human user, a platform that runs a model, and the persons whose data the model uses, three kinds of actor are present: a human principal who instructs the platform, a machine that executes the instruction, and the persons in whose names, voices, attention, and likenesses the machine acts. Roman law calls some actors *personae*, persons capable of acting, and others *res*, things acted upon. That distinction is the first cut of Justinian's Institutes: Book One is *de personis*, Book Two *de rerum divisione*. The lawyer who drafts the platform-services agreement, the data-governance policy, the licensing terms, decides which of the three actors in the loop is which. The default in the contracts being signed today treats the machine as if it were a kind of agent and treats the users as if they were a kind of thing. *Magnifica Humanitas* is saying that default is wrong, twice over.

Leo states the operative claim at MH ¶ 99 as a list of negations:

> Even when described as capable of "learning," AI systems are profoundly different from the human mind. In them, learning is a form of statistical adaptation based on data and feedback, which can be very effective, but does not imply inner growth. They do not undergo experiences, do not possess a body, do not feel joy or pain, do not mature through relationships and do not know from within what love, work, friendship or responsibility mean. Nor do they have a moral conscience, since they do not judge good and evil, grasp the ultimate meaning of situations, or bear responsibility for consequences. MH ¶ 99.

The list reads, item by item, as a description of what an agent is, stated as what the machine isn't. The encyclical's claim is not that the machine is dangerous because it is too powerful. It is that the machine cannot be an agent at all. Wherever it appears to act, what is acting is whatever agent sent it.

The natural-law tradition has had a name for this analysis since the thirteenth century. Aquinas works it out at *Summa Theologiae* I-II, Q. 17, art. 4. When an agent's will commands a faculty or external instrument to act, the commanding will and the commanded act together compose, in Aquinas's phrase, "one human act." The instrument is a vehicle, not a party. The sophistication of the instrument does not change the analysis. A hand, a pen, a press, an automated process: the moral responsibility tracks the agent that commanded the act. Roman law had been doing the same analysis longer. *Personae* act; *res* are acted upon. A complex machine that performs tasks is, on this analysis, a sophisticated *res*. It is not promoted to *persona* by its sophistication.

American agency law works the same analysis with contemporary doctrinal apparatus. The Restatement (Third) of Agency defines an *agent* as one who acts on behalf of and subject to the control of a principal (§ 1.01). Actual authority is created by the principal's manifestations to the agent (§ 2.01); apparent authority by the principal's manifestations to a third party (§ 2.03); and respondeat superior is principal liability for an agent's torts within the scope of employment (§ 7.07). The word *agent* itself is from the Latin *agere*, to act: one who acts on another's behalf, the being whose act counts as another's. The Restatement's *agent* is congruent with what Aquinas calls an instrument: a being whose act is attributed to the principal who controlled it. Sophistication of the agent does not change the analysis.

The Hebrew tradition names the same category with a single noun. In modern Hebrew, artificial intelligence is בינה מלאכותית (*bina melakhutit*). בינה (*bina*) is the kind of understanding God gave Solomon. *Melakhutit* is the adjective form of מלאכה (*melakha*), work or craft. Its root, מ-ל-א-כ, is the root of מלאך (*malach*), the standard biblical Hebrew for *messenger*, which the Vulgate renders *angelus*. The intelligence of craft and the messenger of God share their consonants the way *mission* and *missile* share theirs. Both are sent. Both have a job. Neither chooses. The tradition holds that an angel has no יצר הרע (*yetzer hara*), no inclination to fall, and therefore no יצר הטוב (*yetzer tov*), no inclination to rise. It cannot disobey, repent, or enter covenant, because covenant requires the freedom to break it. The angel performs the mission of whoever sent it. The only question that has ever mattered about a messenger is who did the sending.

Leo reaches into Hebrew Scripture for the two governing metaphors of the encyclical, and the Hebrew states the agency question at the level of the letter. Babel is named in Genesis by an etymology the verse itself supplies:

> Therefore is the name of it called Babel; because the LORD did there confound the language of all the earth: and from thence did the LORD scatter them abroad upon the face of all the earth. Gen 11:9 (KJV).

The Hebrew verb behind *confound* is *balal* (בלל), to mix or confuse. The name of the city, בבל (*Bavel*), is the noun-form of the confusion it produced. What sits one letter away in the same alphabet is the name of the shepherd whose offering had been received and whose blood the Torah says cries from the ground. Abel, in Hebrew הבל (*Hevel*), means breath, or vapor. The two words share their last two letters, ב and ל; they differ only at the front. Abel was an agent who offered the work of his hands and was received. The builders of Babel were agents whose project the verse states in their own voice. The phrase the King James translates "let us make us a name" is in Hebrew נעשה לנו שם (*na'aseh lanu shem*). Rashi reads the project as agents trying to be their own principals: to constitute the very transcendence to which they should have been answerable. Augustine reads the same story as the formation of the earthly city built on pride, the type-case of the city raised against the city of God (*De Civitate Dei* XVI.4).

The letter-level reading carries the moral claim. ב (*bet*) is the house, the Torah's opening letter, an enclosure open on one side. Babel doubles it: two houses pushed front to front, no opening between them. ל (*lamed*), the tallest letter, sits between the two walls with nowhere to climb. The reaching of an agent has been sealed inside a structure of the agent's own making. The midrash makes the *persona/res* inversion explicit at the level of moral attention. *Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer* 24:6 reports that when a brick fell from the tower, the builders mourned, but when a man fell to his death, no one paused. The persons doing the building had been resorted into the column of things, on the city's own books, before the language was confused.

Leo states the social-order form of the same syndrome:

> We must, then, avoid the "Babel syndrome," namely the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance. MH ¶ 10.

The pretense is an agency claim in two parts. To translate the mystery of the person into data is to treat the person as a *res*, exhaustively describable, when the person is in fact a *persona* whose meaning is constituted by acts the data cannot capture. And to attribute the translation to a single digital language is to treat the language itself, the machine that speaks it, as a *persona* doing the translating, when in fact the language is being deployed by whoever programmed the model and licensed its outputs. The Babel syndrome misclassifies in both directions at once. It puts *personae* in the column of things and puts *res* in the column of agents.

Leo's positive image is the city rebuilt by ordinary people. The book of Nehemiah supplies the picture. Leo at ¶ 8 reads Nehemiah's method as a governance model: each family given its own section of wall, each accountable for what it builds, the whole city reconstituted through shared responsibility rather than imposed uniformity. The phrase Leo uses is that "all persons assume their own role" — the agency claim restated as the architecture of a rebuilt city. The plural of *persona* is the city. Leo at ¶ 16 adds that "the 'rejected stones' — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us — will become the cornerstone." A city in which the discarded are recognized as the foundation is a city that has refused to misclassify *personae* as *res*. The Hebrew supplies a contested etymology that fits. שלם (*Shalem*), the older name of the city, the one Melchizedek ruled when Abraham met him (Gen 14:18), means whole, or at peace. The *yeru-* prefix is read by some traditional commentators as a form of the verb ירה (*yarah*), to lay or set — the verb God uses when answering Job out of the storm: "who laid the corner stone thereof; when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy?" (Job 38:6-7, KJV). On one traditional reading, the name Jerusalem (ירושלים) is *yarah* and *shalem* compounded: the setting of the cornerstone of peace. That image is not a construction project. It is a song. Cities are built; songs are sung by agents. The distinction Leo and the Hebrew are both drawing is between a structure that recognizes its participants as agents and a structure that does not.

Once the agency categorization is right, the property regime follows. Roman law distinguishes *res communes omnium* — air, running water, the sea — categorically outside private appropriation, from *res nullius*, things without an owner, available to the first taker by *occupatio* (*Justinian's Institutes* 2.1.1). Locke's first-possession tradition builds on the second category: a person "mixed his Labour" with what nature provided and "thereby makes it his Property" (*Second Treatise of Civil Government* § 27 (1690)). Richard Epstein and Carol Rose each worked over the analytical foundations of that tradition; both stay inside the law of things. Neither reaches *personae*, who are not capturable in either sense, because they are not things.

The encyclical's critique of data colonialism makes the claim, in moral form, that the AI industry is misclassifying at both levels of Justinian's taxonomy. Leo at ¶ 178:

> Even today, colonialism assumes new forms. It no longer dominates only bodies, but appropriates data, transforming personal lives into exploitable information.… Here lies one of the most urgent moral challenges of our time: to ensure that shared knowledge becomes a true common good rather than an instrument of dominance. This requires restoring to individuals not only the data that describes them, but also the ability to decide how it is used, by whom and for whose benefit. MH ¶ 178.

The "true common good" is the *res communes omnium* category stated in Catholic Social Doctrine vocabulary. But the deeper claim, inside the agency analysis, is prior. The data, voice, attention, and likeness of a person are extensions of the *persona*, not items in inventory. The question the encyclical raises is not which property regime governs the data. It is whether the data of *personae* belongs in the law of things at all.

The contracts a transactional lawyer drafts are where the agency allocation becomes binding on the conduct of the parties, including the machine. *Shall* is a different country from *may*. A clause that authorizes the platform to act "as agent for the user" answers one set of agency questions; a clause that treats the user as a data-subject from whom inputs are drawn answers a different set. A clause that licenses the platform to retain inputs to train downstream models treats those inputs as *res nullius*, available for capture. A clause that excludes a category of personal information from training corpora treats that category as participating in personhood and so unavailable for capture. The machine does not pause to ask whether the clause is wise. It runs.

The clause is also where actual authority is conferred (Restatement (Third) of Agency § 2.01). When the clause is hidden from or misrepresented to the third party, apparent authority allocates the cost of the undisclosed limit to the principal rather than to the third party who acted on the appearance the principal created (§ 2.03). The leading illustration: a church that hired a painter, told him internally not to hire helpers, and then watched him hire a helper anyway was bound by the appearance of authority it had built up. *Mill Street Church of Christ v. Hogan*, 785 S.W.2d 263 (Ky. Ct. App. 1990); see Seth C. Oranburg, *Business Associations Law in Theory and Practice* ch. 2 (forthcoming). When platform terms of service diverge from what a reasonable user would believe she was authorizing, apparent authority locates the loss on the principal that drafted the divergence. Respondeat superior runs further: the principal is liable for the agent's torts within the scope of employment (§ 7.07). In the AI-platform setting, the agent is the model, the scope is what the principal's clauses delegated, and the harm is what the model did with the delegation. The doctrine needs no invention for the AI age.

Aquinas's analysis at I-II, Q. 17, art. 4 applies in unmodified form: the moral act is one human act, attributed to the agent that commanded it. The lawyer is the agent in the loop.

The Jewish business-law tradition treats this kind of question as the first-order moral question. Rava teaches in the Talmud that the first question put to a person at the heavenly tribunal is, in Aramaic, נשאת ונתת באמונה (*nasata v'natata b'emunah*) — did you deal faithfully in business? — asked before any question about Torah study or prayer (Talmud Bavli, *Shabbat* 31a). Commercial conduct is where the agent is most visibly an agent. The lawyer who drafts the clause is being asked that question on every page.

The choice Leo describes is real. It is the choice between Babel and Jerusalem, between the closed city and the open one. But the choice is not made by walking into one or the other. It is made by agents who recognize themselves as agents, recognize the messenger as a messenger, and refuse to allow the contracts they write to invert Justinian's first cut. *Magnifica Humanitas*, read this way, is not principally addressed to the engineers who build the models. It is addressed to the practitioners who specify what the models may do, in clauses that allocate agency between principal, messenger, and person. For the lawyer drafting in the AI age, the encyclical is not an essay on policy. It is a description of work the practitioner has already been doing, without yet knowing what to call it.
