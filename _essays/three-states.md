# The Three States of a Living Tradition

## Notes on Pedagogical Continuity in the West

*Seth Oranburg*

---

## Tradition as Institutional Problem

A tradition does not maintain itself. Every method of forming human judgment that has survived across centuries survived because some set of institutional arrangements protected it, transmitted it, and reproduced the conditions under which it could be practiced. The question of which methods survived is therefore not separable from the question of which institutions held.

This is easy to miss. We tend to speak of intellectual traditions the way we speak of old buildings: as things that endure through their own solidity, persisting while circumstances change around them. But methods of forming judgment are not stone. They live only when practiced; they are practiced only when someone has arranged the conditions for practice; and those conditions require, at minimum, people with enough common purpose, enough time, and enough mutual accountability to submit to a method together.

Three pedagogical traditions have, across enormous spans of time and under radically different conditions, survived the disruptions that eliminated most of their competitors. The Socratic elenchus originated in fifth-century Athens, was effectively lost for many centuries in the West as a living practice, and has been reclaimed in modern democratic and liberal institutions, incompletely, selectively, and always in tension with the administrative pressures of mass education. The Jewish havruta tradition has been practiced continuously for approximately two millennia without, for most of that time, the benefit of state patronage, territorial sovereignty, or institutional permanence of the kind that supports most long-lived intellectual traditions. The Roman and medieval Catholic disputatio has been practiced, with varying intensity and fidelity, inside a continuous institutional structure (pontifical universities, the magisterium, the network of religious orders) from at least the eleventh century to the present.

These three facts about survival are not equally well understood. This essay argues that each fact encodes a different answer to the same underlying question: what must a society preserve in order to preserve a method of forming judgment? And that the three answers (institutional continuity, communal substitution, and periodic reclamation) describe not only historical configurations but the actual options available to any program or society that wants to recover or protect such methods now.

## Athens: The Severed and the Reclaimed

Socrates did not found a school in the institutional sense. He wandered. He questioned. He made enemies. The method he practiced, the elenchus, moves interlocutors from confident ignorance toward acknowledged confusion, and sometimes beyond confusion toward genuine inquiry. It required proximity, time, and a willingness to be unsettled. It was not, in its original form, a classroom technique. It was a civic practice, embedded in the agora, the gymnasium, the symposium. When the agora lost its civic vitality and the institutions that supported free philosophical conversation were suppressed or transformed, the practice did not survive in its original form. The text survived. Plato wrote. But the text is a record of conversations, not a substitute for having them.

What the West recovered from Athens was, for many centuries, primarily the text. The Dialogues were preserved, copied, and eventually transmitted back to Europe through Arabic intermediaries who had preserved them when the Byzantine East failed to pass them westward. Medieval scholastic philosophers read Plato and knew Socrates; they did not practice the elenchus. They practiced the disputatio, which is a different and in some ways antithetical method, as I will discuss below. The Socratic method as a living pedagogical technique, as a way of actually teaching through systematic cross-examination, was reconstructed, and reconstructed explicitly as reconstruction, in the nineteenth century. Christopher Columbus Langdell introduced the case method to Harvard Law School in 1870 as a form of Socratic questioning adapted to the classroom. His version was already a translation and a reduction: the original elenchus was a two-person conversation with no predetermined outcome; the Langdellian case method became a professor's tool for guiding large groups of students toward conclusions the professor had already reached.

The Langdellian elenchus was a reconstruction from text rather than a transmission from practice. This matters because it introduced a structural instability that the unbroken traditions do not share. A method reconstructed from text is vulnerable to a specific kind of drift: it can be further reconstructed, in a different direction, by the next generation that reads the same texts more conveniently or more efficiently. The Langdellian case method has been eroded and simplified over the past fifty years not because anyone decided to abandon Socratic pedagogy but because no institutional force maintained the transmission. Each generation of law professors trained in large research universities with research incentives that reward scholarship over teaching, received an increasingly attenuated version of the original method and passed on an even more attenuated version to their students.

This is what a severed tradition looks like when it is nominally maintained but not truly transmitted. The text remains. The label remains. The practice hollows out. By the time a thoughtful observer (an Edwards, writing in 1992, predicting that professional education would complete its deskilling loop) [^void-edwards] notices the drift, the gap between the label and the practice has become too wide for incremental repair. Recovery requires not incremental adjustment but a deliberate act of reconstruction: going back to the source, studying what the method actually required, and rebuilding institutional conditions that can support those requirements.

The severed-and-reclaimed condition is not a failed condition. It is, in fact, the condition in which most of the West now operates with respect to classical pedagogy. And it has distinctive virtues. The act of reclamation demands conscious articulation of what one is trying to recover. It requires the would-be practitioner to study the tradition from outside, to develop an explicit account of its requirements, and to construct the conditions for practice deliberately rather than inheriting them. This is intellectually expensive and institutionally precarious, but it produces a kind of reflective clarity about the method that unbroken traditions do not always maintain. Athens, in the reclaimed state, is hyperconscious of what it lost and why.

## Jerusalem: Continuity Without State Infrastructure

The havruta is an extraordinary institutional fact. For two thousand years, in conditions ranging from the Babylonian academies of the early Talmudic period to the great yeshivot of medieval Ashkenaz to the shtetlekh of Eastern Europe to the dispersed Jewish communities of the modern West, a distinctive method of textual study through paired dialogue has been practiced without interruption. The method requires two learners, a text, and a willingness to challenge one another until understanding sharpens. It does not require a campus. It does not require a credential. It does not require state recognition. It requires only the two learners, the text, and the tradition that teaches them how to use both.

The havruta's survival is inseparable from the broader survival of Jewish communal institutions under conditions of statelessness and, frequently, hostility. The same communal structures that maintained the law, the calendar, the language, and the memory also maintained the pedagogical method. The synagogue, the beit midrash, the kehillah: none of these is a state institution in any modern sense. They are voluntary associations held together by shared obligation, shared text, and shared practice. They are exactly the kind of intermediate institutions that Tocqueville, observing nineteenth-century American associational life, identified as the structural condition for free self-governance: small enough to create genuine accountability, large enough to persist through individual losses, and animated by a purpose that transcends the interests of any member.

Hayek's vocabulary is useful here even though he did not apply it to this phenomenon. [^hayek-tacit] The havruta tradition is a repository of what Hayek called tacit and local knowledge, knowledge that cannot be fully articulated in a text or transmitted by instruction alone, but that is carried in the practices of a community and can be acquired only by participation in those practices. The text of the Talmud is available to anyone. The practice of reading it as havruta partners read it (the rhythm of challenge and response, the tolerance for productive confusion, the refusal to accept a glib resolution of a difficult passage) is not in the text. It is in the practitioners. And practitioners are reproduced by other practitioners, not by texts.

This is why the havruta tradition survived circumstances that would have destroyed any tradition dependent on formal institutional infrastructure. The destruction of a yeshiva dispersed its students; the students carried the practice and reconstituted the institution elsewhere. The expulsion of a Jewish community from one country scattered it to others; the scattered communities maintained the method in each new location. The method was not housed in the building. It was housed in the people.

This observation has a sharp edge. A tradition housed in people requires people who have been formed in the tradition. It requires intergenerational transmission of a very specific kind: not the transmission of information (the texts were available to outsiders who never acquired the practice) but the transmission of a formed capacity: the ability to read in a certain way, to argue in a certain spirit, to tolerate a certain kind of productive uncertainty. When that transmission is interrupted, when a generation is lost to persecution, assimilation, or simple demographic collapse, the tradition does not survive automatically by virtue of the texts remaining. It must be reconstructed. The Haskalah, the Jewish Enlightenment, generated pressure in exactly this direction: it proposed that the content of the tradition (the texts, the law, the history) could be separated from the practice of the tradition (the havruta, the rhythm of daily study, the formed habits of communal argument). This proposal, wherever it succeeded, produced exactly the severed condition: the label and the text without the living practice.

The havruta tradition's survival across twenty centuries is therefore not a fact about Judaism per se but a fact about the institutional arrangements that sustained a particular kind of community. Those arrangements (voluntary, obligatory to their members, constituted around shared practice) are replicable in principle. They are not easy to replicate. They require a level of mutual commitment that modern professional and educational institutions rarely generate. But the structural logic is available.

## Rome: Continuity Within Institutional Infrastructure

The medieval disputatio was a formal procedure. A master posed a question; a student defended a position against objections; opponents raised counterarguments; the master presided and adjudicated. The procedure had rules, a schedule, a record. It was embedded in the university, which was itself embedded in a network of ecclesiastical institutions that had formal relationships with the papacy, with the religious orders, with the cathedral schools from which the universities grew.

This institutional density is the most obvious difference between Rome and Jerusalem. The disputatio was not housed in the practitioners alone; it was housed in the institution and in the network of institutions to which the institution belonged. When a particular university declined, its disputatio tradition could be sustained elsewhere in the network. When a religious order faced suppression, another order might carry on the practice. When a particular master's line died out, the institutional form survived to be filled by another generation.

The institutional structure carried costs as well as benefits. A tradition housed in an institution can be transformed by institutional pressures in ways that a tradition housed in a community of practice cannot. The disputatio, as practiced in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries at Paris and Oxford, was a genuinely open procedure in which the question was not settled in advance. By the later medieval period, and certainly by the early modern period, the disputatio had in many contexts become a formal exercise in confirming conclusions already reached: a demonstration of dialectical skill, no longer an inquiry into contested questions. The institutional infrastructure that preserved the form sometimes did so at the cost of the substance. [^void-disputatio-decline]

This tension between form and substance is the characteristic risk of institutionally-maintained traditions. The form persists because the institution persists; the substance requires constant renewal by practitioners who are genuinely committed to the method and not merely performing it. The great periods of renewed disputatio, in the twelfth-century renaissance, in the Thomistic synthesis of the thirteenth century, in the neo-scholastic revival of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, were periods in which the institutional form was inhabited by practitioners who cared about the substance. The fallow periods were periods in which the institution maintained the schedule but the practitioners had lost the conviction that the method was actually producing anything.

The Catholic university today, including those operating under pontifical charter, is the direct institutional heir to the disputatio tradition in a way that no secular university is. This is not primarily a theological claim. It is an institutional claim. The chain of institutional relationships (from the contemporary pontifical university back through the Jesuit colleges of the early modern period, the mendicant schools of the later Middle Ages, the cathedral schools of the Carolingian reform) is continuous in a sense that the chain from a modern American secular university back to Athens is not. The secular university recovered Socratic method through text; the pontifical university inherited disputatio through institutional succession.

This inheritance is also a burden. An institution that has maintained a form across many centuries has accumulated many layers of practice, some of which express the substance of the tradition and some of which merely occupy its place. Distinguishing the essential from the accidental requires exactly the kind of judgment that the disputatio, at its best, was designed to form. The tradition must be used to assess itself. Whether any particular institution is up to that task is an empirical question, not a logical one.

## Three States, Three Different Problems

The typology is now clear enough to state precisely.

Athens, in its reclaimed state, faces the problem of reconstruction. The text exists; the practice must be rebuilt from the outside in. This requires explicit knowledge of what the method demands, deliberate design of the institutional conditions that can support it, and sustained commitment to the method from practitioners who have chosen it rather than inherited it. The structural weakness is instability: without institutional infrastructure and without communal transmission, the reconstructed practice depends on the energy and commitment of individuals, which means it is vulnerable to exactly the kind of attrition that hollowed out the Langdellian case method over fifty years.

Jerusalem, in its continuous communal state, faces the problem of transmission. The practice is alive; the question is whether it can be passed on. Communal transmission works as long as the community maintains sufficient density, mutual obligation, and intergenerational continuity. It is resilient against the loss of any particular building or institution; it is vulnerable to the gradual erosion of communal bonds, to assimilation, and to the proposition that the content of the tradition can be separated from its practice. The structural weakness is diffusion: as the community disperses or diversifies, the transmission mechanism weakens even if no one decides to abandon the tradition.

Rome, in its continuous institutional state, faces the problem of substance. The form persists; the question is whether practitioners inhabit it or merely perform it. Institutional infrastructure preserves the form against most external threats; it cannot preserve the inner conviction that makes the form meaningful. The structural weakness is hollowing: a tradition housed in an institution can survive as a formal shell long after its substance has been evacuated, which means the institution can maintain the appearance of transmitting the tradition while actually transmitting only its procedures.

These three problems are not equally tractable, and no single solution addresses all three. This is why programs that want to recover judgment formation cannot simply pick one classical method and deploy it: they must understand which condition they operate under and design accordingly.

## The Modern Professional Education Situation

American professional education currently operates under something closer to the Athens condition than to either of the others. The great law schools, medical schools, and business schools of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries transmitted pedagogical methods that had real substance: the Socratic case method at its Langdellian best, the bedside teaching of Osler and the early clinical educators, the apprenticeship traditions of the older professions. Those methods were never housed in communal institutions capable of transmitting them without formal infrastructure, and they were never embedded in a continuous institutional network of the Rome type. They were housed primarily in the energy and commitment of individual teachers, in the culture of specific institutions, and in the informal transmission that happens when students who have been genuinely formed by a method go on to teach.

As the research university model displaced the teaching orientation of the older professional schools, as faculty incentives shifted toward scholarship and away from the craft of teaching, as the professions themselves became more heavily regulated and more dependent on technical compliance rather than practical wisdom, the informal transmission broke down. The deskilling Edwards predicted [^void-edwards] came to pass not because anyone chose it but because the institutional arrangements that sustained the formation methods were gradually replaced by arrangements that valued something else.

This is the deskilling loop in its institutional form: not a conspiracy against wisdom but a series of individually rational choices by individuals and institutions that, in aggregate, produced an environment in which wisdom cannot be formed because the conditions for forming it no longer exist. The rule replaces the judgment because the rule is auditable and the judgment is not. The compliance officer multiplies because compliance can be documented and practical wisdom cannot. The checklist proliferates because the checklist transfers liability and judgment does not. None of these choices is irrational in isolation. Together they describe a civilization that has progressively engineered out the capacity it most needs.

The response cannot be to add a course on practical wisdom to the existing curriculum. A course on practical wisdom taught in a context that systematically rewards the elimination of judgment from practice will be defeated by its context. The response must address the institutional conditions that make formation impossible, and that requires clarity about what institutional conditions make formation possible.

## What Programs Can Actually Do

Each of the three states of a living tradition suggests a different kind of institutional response.

The severed-and-reclaimed condition calls for deliberate reconstruction. This is harder and more conscious work than transmission, because it cannot rely on habit. It requires going back to the source: studying the original method in its original context, understanding not just what the practitioners did but why those practices produced the effects they did, and then asking which elements of the original context can be reproduced in the modern setting and which must be translated or substituted. The answer is not always obvious. The elenchus required an interlocutor with enough standing to be worth interrogating; a modern professor questioning a first-year student does not reproduce that condition, and ignoring the difference is how the Langdellian method became a tool for intimidation rather than inquiry. Translation is required. But translation demands prior understanding of what is being translated.

The communal transmission condition calls for institutional design that reproduces the functional equivalent of voluntary communal obligation. This is a different kind of institutional work: not reconstruction from text but deliberate cultivation of the social conditions that make informal transmission possible. The havruta was not invented by any reformer; it emerged from a set of social and religious obligations that structured how Jews spent their time, who they spent it with, and what counted as a serious engagement with a serious question. Modern institutions that want to reproduce something like havruta must ask how they can create genuine communities of inquiry within their formal structures: communities with enough mutual accountability to sustain the productive friction that the method requires.

The continuous institutional condition calls for vigilance against hollowing. The formal structure persists; the question is whether the practitioners inhabit it or perform it. This requires ongoing attention to the difference, which is itself a kind of practical judgment: the judgment that distinguishes genuine inquiry from its formal simulation. The institution cannot generate this vigilance automatically; it requires practitioners who have been formed in the tradition well enough to recognize when the form has lost its substance, and who have the standing within the institution to say so.

No real program operates in only one of these conditions. A program at a pontifical university in the United States, for example, operates under both the Roman condition and the American condition simultaneously. It inherits the continuous institutional structure of the disputatio tradition through its pontifical charter and its connection to the broader network of Catholic higher education. It operates inside an American professional education system that has severed its formation traditions and must reclaim them. These conditions create different demands simultaneously: from the Roman inheritance, the need for vigilance against hollowing; from the American condition, the need for deliberate reconstruction.

A program that recognizes this dual position is not confused about its identity. It occupies a distinctive location. The institutional inheritance provides resources (the form, the vocabulary, the claim on a continuous tradition) that purely reconstructed programs do not have. The American context creates an audience and an urgency, the profession's need for recovered judgment formation, that purely self-referential institutional maintenance does not generate. The combination is unusual and, if handled well, genuinely powerful.

## Why This Typology Is Itself a Research Question

The three-states typology is not a settled historical claim. It is a framework for inquiry that generates empirical questions.

How did havruta survive across twenty centuries without institutional infrastructure? The obvious answer is that it survived because Jewish communal life survived, and Jewish communal life survived because of the particular structure of Jewish obligation and the particular role of textual study in that structure. But this answer is too quick. Not all aspects of pre-modern Jewish communal life survived. Many practices, texts, and methods did not survive. Havruta did survive. Why havruta and not other methods? What features of the method made it compatible with the kinds of communities that Jewish life could sustain under adversity? These are empirical questions about institutional resilience that scholars of Jewish history and education have addressed only partially. [^void-havruta-scholarship]

Why did the disputatio survive in the form it did rather than in other forms? The Scholastic tradition generated many pedagogical techniques: the lectio, the quaestio, the sermo, the reportatio. Not all of them survived with equal vitality into the modern pontifical university. The disputatio, in some form, did survive. Why? The institutional answer is that the disputatio was formalized in statutes and curriculum requirements. But formal requirements can be satisfied without actual practice; many formal requirements are. Something about the disputatio generated enough genuine commitment from enough practitioners across enough generations to sustain it as a living method rather than a pro forma exercise. What that something was is itself an open question. [^void-disputatio-survival]

And the reconstruction question: what actually happens when a severed tradition is reclaimed? The case method provides one data point, but a troubled one, since the Langdellian method is itself now in the early stages of being reclaimed from its nineteenth-century reconstruction. The Thomistic revival of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries provides another data point, one with a different pattern: a tradition that was continuous in one location (the Dominican schools) was deliberately recovered and extended into a broader institutional context (Leo XIII's Aeterni Patris, 1879). The pattern of partial continuity enabling broader reconstruction is different from the pattern of full rupture requiring reconstruction from text alone. Understanding these patterns is preliminary to designing programs that want to recover formation methods. [^void-thomistic-revival]

## Conclusion: Survival as Institutional Evidence

The survival of a pedagogical tradition is not an accident. It is evidence. Evidence about what institutional arrangements are capable of protecting formation methods across disruptions that eliminate most competitors. Evidence about which features of a method are essential and which are accidents of a particular historical context. Evidence about what kind of community is required to sustain a practice that depends on human formation rather than on information transmission.

The three great surviving traditions of the West (the Socratic elenchus in its reclaimed form, the havruta in its continuous communal form, the disputatio in its continuous institutional form) each encode different answers to the same question. Athens says: the method can be recovered from text if the recovery is deliberate and the conditions for practice are consciously rebuilt. Jerusalem says: the method can survive without institutional infrastructure if the community that carries it maintains the mutual obligations that make informal transmission possible. Rome says: the method can survive across great spans of time if the institution maintains the form and the practitioners periodically renew the substance.

None of these answers is complete. None of them is easy. And none of them can be borrowed whole from its original context and applied to a different one. Translation is always required. But translation can only proceed from understanding. Before any contemporary program can decide which lessons from Athens, Jerusalem, or Rome apply to its situation, it must understand each tradition in its own terms: as a set of specific practices that survived specific conditions because specific institutional arrangements protected them.

That understanding is not currently available in any systematic form. It is the work that remains to be done.
