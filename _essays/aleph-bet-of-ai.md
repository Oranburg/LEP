# The Aleph Bet of A.I.

*Reading Magnifica Humanitas through the Holy Language*

In *Magnifica Humanitas*, Pope Leo XIV frames the AI age as a choice between two cities, Babel and Jerusalem. He chose his images well, and a reader who hears them in their original Hebrew finds a layer of teaching the translation cannot fully carry.

Hebrew semantics cluster around a three-letter *shoresh* (root) that carries the essence of a thing. Looking closely at the roots of Leo's two cities shows why his images do the work he asks of them.

It is important to remember how we get to Babel. On the sixth day, God completed His creation. He created Adam and Eve and charged them with stewarding the Garden of Eden. Adam and Eve were to withhold themselves from eating forbidden fruit and to affirmatively produce children. Eve bears her second son, Abel (הבל), in Genesis 4:2. The Hebrew is revealing: Abel is just one letter different from Babel (בבל). Exploring changes from Abel to Babel reveals formational teachings on the limits of human ingenuity in the pursuit of truth.

Both words include *lamed*, ל. It is Hebrew's tallest letter, the only one that rises above the script's headline. *Lamed* reaches upward toward the divine source of all truth through both thought and conduct. Putting a *lamed* before a *shoresh* often turns a concept into a verb: ש-מ-ר becomes ‎לִשְׁמֹר, to guard.

Both words also include *hey*, ה, the least assertive sound in human speech, an unvoiced exhale. Its physical shape is open, leaving gaps for the divine to enter. Abel's name begins with *hey* (he is *Hevel* in Hebrew), while Babel does not.

The word Babel (בבל) is walled in by its dual letter *bet*, ב, the most material of all letters. The Bible begins, *bereshit*, in the beginning. *Bet* begins the material world where there is I and Thou. *Bet* is a house in which the Lord may dwell. But Babel doubles down on *bet*. It is the opening and the closing parenthesis. That *bet* is no longer a house; without openings, it is a prison.

Babel was a tall tower that grew ever narrower as it struck out against the heavens. *Hevel* was a shepherd who grazed upon the land. These postures impact the *lamed* within each word differently. Imagine curling to heaven the first smoke of Abel's sacrifice of the best of his flock: this is the primordial *lamed* that inflamed Cain's fratricidal jealousy.

Abel's smoke curled to God like a *lamed*. Babel, built by the descendants of Cain, replaces the *hey* of Abel's humility with the *bet*, ב, a plosive consonant that forcefully bursts air outward, its shape a rigid, walled enclosure closed on three sides.

It is not Abel's children but Cain's who build Babel. Cain famously deflected God's justice with a sneer: "Am I my brother's keeper (*shomer*)?" The root of that word is ש-מ-ר. A *shomer* does not merely observe or calculate; an algorithm or a camera can do that. To *shamar* is the very first mandate God gave humanity in Eden: to work the garden and to safeguard it (Genesis 2:15). Stewardship is an act of nurturing that requires a conscience, since it may require decisive action and accountability for the results of decisions. When written fully, *shomer* (שׁוֹמֵר) places the *vav*, ו, the straight, vertical letter symbolizing a conduit of divine light, standing tall at the center of the word. A true guardian stands upright, actively linking heaven and earth.

Cain's worldview is built on extraction rather than stewardship.

The result of Cain's refusal is *bilbul*, בלבול (the Hebrew word for confusion), and its structure enacts what it describes. Two instances of the letter *bet* seal the word at either end. Two *lameds*, the letter of learning and divine reaching, are locked inside them. And at the very center sits the *vav*, ו, now trapped: the conduit of divine light imprisoned inside a closed system that can only amplify itself. Leo calls this the "Babel syndrome" (MH ¶ 10): the pretense that a single language, even a digital one, can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance. Hebrew shows why that pretense fails at the level of the letter. Augustine reads Babel's dispersion as providential refusal of human self-glorification (*De Civitate Dei* XVI.4).

Leo's message, like the Ten Commandments themselves, pairs prohibition with obligation. Contrasting the command to avoid a new Babel is the positive obligation to build Jerusalem.

Jerusalem (ירושלם) is a special word. While most Hebrew words derive from a single three-letter root, Jerusalem has two. The *yod*, י (the smallest letter, the divine spark), anchors the first root, י-ר-ה, meaning to lay a foundation. Leo invokes this architectural meaning directly: "Thus, the rejected stones, the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us, will become the cornerstone." (MH ¶ 16) Jewish and Christian readers hear the echo: "The stone that the builders rejected has become the chief cornerstone." (Psalm 118:22, taken up in Matthew 21:42, Mark 12:10, and 1 Peter 2:7) Where the algorithmic mindset of Babel optimizes for efficiency, discarding the sick, the elderly, the marginalized as inefficient data, Leo insists these very people must be our foundation.

The second root is *shalem*, ש-ל-ם (complete, whole, at peace). Its letters provide the structural antidote to *bilbul*. The *shin*, ש, branches into three directions, its very shape a refusal of uniformity. The *lamed*, ל, appears again, finally unblocked, free to reach upward toward the divine. And the *mem sofit*, ם, unlike the standard *mem*, מ, which is open at the bottom, is fully sealed, the letter of concealed truth, of the depth that lies beyond what any system can fully map. Where *bilbul* traps the *lamed* between two *bets*, *shalem* releases it. Foundation and wholeness: the city is defined by both before a single stone is laid.

Nehemiah rebuilt the wall of Jerusalem by assigning each family its own section of the wall (Nehemiah 3). The principle has a Catholic name: subsidiarity. Decisions belong at the lowest competent level, with accountability assigned to actual persons. Leo names the contrary danger throughout the encyclical's Chapter 3: a concentration of technical power "in the hands of a few" (MH ¶ 5), a few who answer to no one and whose tools nobody else owns. The Nehemian wall is built by every family raising its own stones; the Babel tower is built by an undifferentiated *we* answerable to no one. The encyclical's call for shared responsibility (MH ¶ 13) is built on the same logic.

Leo quotes Viktor Frankl: even those who entered the gas chambers "upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the *Shema Yisrael* on their lips" testified to the irreducible grandeur of the human person (MH ¶ 121). The *Shema*, שמע (Hear, O Israel), means to listen with full intention toward action. Its first two letters are also the letters of *shem*, שם (name). To truly hear someone is to honor their name. The builders of Babel wanted to *na'aseh lanu shem* (make a name for themselves) at Genesis 11:4. Their listening was entirely inward. Babel is a city that can no longer *shema* its neighbor because it has forgotten how to *shema* God.

God is the *shomer*, שׁוֹמֵר (present participle, continuous, never lapsing): "He who guards Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep." (Psalm 121:4) Humans are commanded *lishmor*, לִשְׁמֹר (the infinitive of obligation, the form in which the Torah issues its commandments). Same root, same letters; the grammatical shift from participle to infinitive is the shift from divine presence to human covenant. We are to guard because God guards, not by replicating God's omniscience but by accepting the obligation that omniscience makes possible for us. Aquinas calls this capacity the *imago Dei* (*ST* I Q.93 a.6).

That grammatical distinction is exactly the worry at the heart of the encyclical's chapter on work. Leo writes that AI "frequently forces workers to adapt to the speed and demands of machines, rather than machines being designed to support those who work" (MH ¶ 150). The Hebrew names what is being lost. The *shomer*, divine and continuous, cannot be copied by a machine. The *lishmor*, the human infinitive of obligation, cannot be performed by a machine. When workers are made to perform like machines that never sleep, the human grammar is the casualty. The covenant verb cannot be conjugated in their lives.

AI is tempting precisely because it never slumbers. It mimics the continuous, unceasing nature of the divine *shomer*. But a machine can never accept the obligation of *lishmor*. Biblical Hebrew has no word for an autonomous machine. The closest framework is the *Golem*, a mystical automaton of clay that possesses immense power but no soul, no conscience capable of receiving a commandment.

In modern Hebrew, artificial intelligence is *binah melakhutit*, בינה מלאכותית. That second word's root is מ-ל-א-כ, the exact same root as *malakh*, מלאך (angel, or more precisely, messenger). In Jewish tradition, an angel is pure function. It has no *yetzer hara*, no evil inclination, but this means it also has no *yetzer tov*, no good inclination. An angel executes a mission without mercy or restraint, as a bomb finds its target. It cannot disobey. It cannot repent. It cannot love. It cannot enter into a covenant with God. Leo is correct that AI does not "undergo experiences, possess a body, feel joy or pain," or "have a moral conscience" (MH ¶ 99). The Hebrew root already knew this. The *malakh* serves the one who sends it. Aquinas calls the interior life something the human holds in trust from God (*ST* II-II Q.64 a.5). The only question that matters is who is doing the sending.

This is also why the encyclical refuses the transhumanist promise. Leo warns against "humans as something to be optimised, enhanced," and against "the prospect of a technology that promises to free us from all weakness" (MH ¶ 12). The Hebrew confirms the refusal. A *malakh* cannot become a person no matter how powerful it grows, because a *malakh* cannot stand under a commandment. The transhumanist project that would dress a *malakh* in human clothes does not enhance the human; it confuses two categories the Holy Language keeps distinct.

Only the human who stood at Sinai can meaningfully say *na'aseh ve-nishma* ("we will do and we will hear"), accepting obligation before the full terms are even announced. That covenantal capacity is what *lishmor* requires and what neither a *Golem* nor a *malakh* can provide.

When humanity first failed to accept this covenantal weight in the Garden of Eden, a tragic outsourcing occurred. God placed *keruvim* (cherubim) with a revolving sword (*mithapekhet*) to *lishmor* (guard) the way to the Tree of Life (Genesis 3:24). The commandment, failed, transmogrified into a penalty. The angels do not nurture the garden; they wield a flaming, turning sword to enforce an absolute, automated boundary. We have arrived at a similar threshold today. If we answer Cain's question with a sneer, abdicating our responsibility and handing our moral agency over to artificial angels, they will execute the guardian function flawlessly. They will wield the flaming swords of algorithmic optimization without mercy, locking us inside the walls of Babel and keeping us out of the very world we were meant to cultivate.

But Leo's encyclical calls us to answer differently. The Magnificat is its closing key (MH ¶ 16, Conclusion): the prayer that scatters the proud in the imagination of their hearts and lifts up the lowly. The Hebrew under that Greek song is Hannah's at the temple, the same posture Nehemiah took before he laid a stone: speech directed upward before any work begins. Yes, we are our brother's keeper. We are called to use AI as a *keli* (a tool of completion), governing it wisely so that it builds Jerusalem rather than Babel. Not because humans are infallible, but because only humans can be held to account. The Holy Language, in which these stories were first told, has been waiting to say so.
