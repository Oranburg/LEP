## Task 1: The Nehemiah Asymmetry

**Hypothesis:** Classical Jewish scholarship has a deep midrashic and rabbinic interior on the Tower of Babel but no comparable developed tradition treating *Nehemiah's* rebuilding of Jerusalem as a governance parable.

**Verdict: The hypothesis holds, with important qualifications.**

### What the commentators actually do

The comments on Nehemiah 2–6 are predominantly **philological and historical**, not political-theological or governance-theoretical:

**Rashi on Nehemiah 2:17** (the pivotal rallying speech — "Come, let us rebuild the wall of Jerusalem"):

> [on "וָאוֹמַר אֲלֵיהֶם"] לְמָחָר אָמַרְתִּי לָהֶם
> "The next day I spoke to them"

> [on "וְלֹא נִהְיֶה עוֹד חֶרְפָּה"] שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ צָרֵינוּ בּוֹזְזִים וְשׁוֹלְלִים אוֹתָנוּ
> "So that our enemies would no longer plunder and despoil us"

Rashi's gloss is explanatory word-definition, not a governance reading.

**Malbim on Nehemiah 2:17** is the most substantive among the classical commentators on the governance moment:

> [on "ואומר אליהם אתם רואים הרעה"] ר"ל מה שאנחנו ברעה מפני צרי יהודה הוא מפני שירושלים חרבה ואין בית מצודות להשגב שם נגד הצר, לכן לכו ונבנה ולא נהיה עוד חרפה, כי יהיה לנו בית מצודות

The Malbim frames Nehemiah's appeal in terms of *defensive security* (a fortress against the enemy) rather than as a positive theory of communal restoration or civic governance under foreign rule. It is strategic-military exegesis, not social-institutional.

**Malbim on Nehemiah 5:1** (the labor-grievance scene, where workers protest economic exploitation):

> זעקת דלת העם היתה רבה על העשירים ומפרש שבין הזועקים היו שתי כתות
> "The cry of the common people against the wealthy was great — and he explains that among the protesters there were two classes"

This is the most socially attentive passage in the classical corpus on Nehemiah. The Malbim tracks the class distinction between poor workers and wealthy creditors. But he does not develop it into a governance theory; it remains exegetical sociology in service of the plain sense.

**Ralbag on Nehemiah 3:1** mentions the architectural logic of the Sheep Gate and the priests' assignments, again without governance-theoretical elaboration.

### Talmudic treatment of Nehemiah

The rabbinic interest in Nehemiah is almost entirely focused on **canon and chronology**, not governance:

**Bava Batra 15a** (the definitive Talmudic text on biblical authorship):

> עֶזְרָא כָּתַב סִפְרוֹ, וְיַחַס שֶׁל דִּבְרֵי הַיָּמִים עַד לוֹ.
> "Ezra wrote his book and the genealogy of Chronicles up to his own time."

And then immediately:

> מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא עָלָה עֶזְרָא מִבָּבֶל, עַד שֶׁיִּחֵס עַצְמוֹ וְעָלָה. וּמַאן אַסְּקֵיהּ? נְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה.
> "This supports Rav, as Rav Yehuda said in Rav's name: Ezra did not go up from Babylon until he had established his own genealogy and then went up. And who brought him up? Nehemiah son of Hacaliah."

[Aramaic — needs human for transliteration of seg 2]

The Talmud's Nehemiah is the man who *enabled* Ezra's return — a secondary figure in the story of Ezra's canonical authority. The book of Nehemiah itself is subsumed into "Ezra's book" in the Talmudic authorship tradition (BB 15a does not list Nehemiah separately as an author of his own book).

**Rosh Hashanah 3b** cites Nehemiah for regnal-year chronology (what year of Artaxerxes corresponds to Nehemiah's mission in Kislev vs. Nisan). The interest is calendrical-halakhic, not political.

No Talmudic passage was located on Sefaria treating Nehemiah as a model of communal leadership, institutional rebuilding under exile, or governance under foreign rule. Searches across Bavli for "נחמיה חומה" (Nehemiah, wall) returned zero results.

### Midrashic treatment of Nehemiah

The midrashim that cover the Return period (Eichah Rabbah, Yalkut Shimoni on Nach) were checked. Yalkut Shimoni on Nach — which covers the Writings including Nehemiah — returns text on other Near Eastern peoples and genealogies in the relevant section but no governance-parable treatment of Nehemiah was retrieved.

No midrash was located on Sefaria developing the Nehemiah rebuilding narrative (shared labor, night survey, wall-work against opposition, debt-relief decree of Neh 5) as a template for communal governance.

### Steinsaltz introduction to Nehemiah (modern)

The Koren Steinsaltz Tanakh HaMevoar (Hebrew edition) has a section preface for Nehemiah 1:1–2:9 which opens:

> נחמיה משתדל בבניין ירושלים
> "Nehemiah Labors for the Building of Jerusalem"

> לא הרבה ידוע על ייחוסו של נחמיה, אך לפי תפקידו בבית המלוכה הפרסי מסתבר שהיה אחד מאותם בני אצולה שנלקחו מבני יהודה כדי לשרת בחצרות המלכים...

This is the closest thing to a governance framing in the Sefaria corpus: Nehemiah as a Jewish noble in the Persian court who leverages his position for his people. But it is a modern study introduction, not a classical midrashic development.

### Conclusion on the asymmetry hypothesis

**The hypothesis holds.** The Babel narrative (Gen 11) has thick midrashic development across Bereshit Rabbah 38, Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 24, Yalkut Shimoni, and the Bavli, all treating the Tower as a moral-theological parable about idolatry, hubris, and the corruption of language. The classical commentators on Nehemiah 2–6 produce predominantly philological commentary; the Talmud's interest in Nehemiah is canonical (who wrote the book) and chronological (regnal dating). No classical Jewish text was located treating the Nehemiah rebuilding project — its shared communal labor, governance under foreign rule, mixed-population workforce, debt-relief decree, or wall-as-covenant — as a political theology or governance parable in the way Leo XIV deploys it.

**The practical implication for the essay:** Leo XIV is doing something that the Jewish tradition itself does not quite do with Nehemiah. The essay can note this honestly: the Pope reads Nehemiah as a governance text in a way that classical Jewish exegesis does not foreground. This is an opening for Jewish engagement — the essay can take the papal reading seriously while noting the relatively thin rabbinic governance-parable tradition on Nehemiah and asking what Jewish thought *would* build from the same text.

## Task 2: Bereshit Rabbah 38:8 — Brick Passage Verification

### Finding

**The brick-over-person passage is NOT in Bereshit Rabbah 38:8.**

A scan of Bereshit Rabbah sections 37–39 confirmed: no mourning-for-a-brick passage appears anywhere in Bereshit Rabbah. The passage Bereshit Rabbah 38:8 (Sefaria: Midrash Rabbah — TE edition, Torat Emet) discusses:

- R. Berekhiah: Egypt spoke to Cush about the building
- R. Huna: the building was going prosperously (each brick course built on another)
- R. Yudan: they built a tower but not a city
- R. Ḥiyya bar Abba: one-third of the tower was burned, one-third sank, one-third stands
- R. Yishmael: "let us make a name" means idolatry
- R. Shimon ben R. Ḥalapta: "the mouth of a fool is his ruin"

None of these contain the brick-mourning motif.

### Correct citation

The brick-over-person passage is **Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer, chapter 24, paragraph 6** (1-based; segment 5 in Sefaria's 0-based index).

Sefaria URL: https://www.sefaria.org/Pirkei_DeRabbi_Eliezer.24.6

The passage is attributed to **Rabbi Pinḥas**.

### Tier 1 quotation (Hebrew, Sefaria Vocalized Edition)

> רַבִּי פִּנְחָס אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיוּ שָׁם אֲבָנִים לִבְנוֹת אֶת הָעִיר וְאֶת הַמִּגְדָּל. מָה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין? הָיוּ מְלַבְּנִים לְבֵנִים וְשׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתָן כְּיוֹצֵר חֶרֶשׂ, עַד שֶׁבָּנוּ אוֹתוֹ גָּבוֹהַּ שִׁבְעִים מִיל. וּמַעֲלוֹת הָיוּ לוֹ מִמִּזְרָחוֹ וּמִמַּעֲרָבוֹ. אֵלּוּ שֶׁהָיוּ מַעֲלִין אֶת הַלְּבֵנִים הָיוּ עוֹלִים מִמִּזְרָחוֹ, וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁהָיוּ יוֹרְדִין הָיוּ יוֹרְדִין מִמַּעֲרָבוֹ. וְאִם נָפַל אָדָם וָמֵת, לֹא שָׂמוּ אֶת לִבָּם עָלָיו. וְאִם נָפְלָה לְבֵנָה אַחַת, הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין וּבוֹכִין וְאוֹמְרִין: ״אוֹי לָנוּ! אֵימָתַי תַּעֲלֶה אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ?״

> Rabbi Pinḥas says: There were no stones there with which to build the city and the tower. What did they do? They molded bricks and fired them like a potter, until they had built it seventy mils high. And it had ramps on its east and its west. Those who carried bricks up went up on the eastern ramp, and those who came down descended on the western ramp. If a man fell and died, they paid him no heed. But if one brick fell, they would sit and weep, saying: "Woe to us! When will another one come up in its place?"

*Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 24:6*

### What Bereshit Rabbah 38 actually contains (for context)

Bereshit Rabbah 38:8 does contain Babel material treating the builders' speech ("Come, let us make bricks" — Gen 11:3). It includes R. Berekhiah's tradition that Egypt addressed Kush about the building program; R. Huna's teaching that the construction was going well; R. Ḥiyya bar Abba's tradition that one-third of the tower was burned, one-third sank, and one-third stands. The chapter is rich with Babel midrash but does not contain the brick-mourning passage. BR 38 should not be dropped from the essay's midrash discussion — it is the primary midrashic locus for Babel in Bereshit Rabbah — but the brick-mourning citation must be corrected to PRE 24:6.
