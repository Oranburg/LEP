## 1. Working Title

**Lead (Tikvah / Mosaic / Sapir):**

*What Nehemiah Knew: A Jewish Lawyer Reads the Pope's Encyclical on Artificial Intelligence*

Alternate: *Babel and Jerusalem: A Jewish Reading of the New Social Encyclical*

---

## 2. Thesis

Leo XIV's *Magnifica Humanitas* (2026) opens with two Hebrew Bible images (Gen 11:1-9; Neh 2-6) and builds its entire architecture on the contrast between them: Babel as the failure of subsidiarity and the idolatry of unified technological power, Jerusalem under Nehemiah as the model of distributed, accountable rebuilding. The Jewish reader arrives at those texts with midrashic depth that the natural-law tradition does not carry on the inside. That asymmetry is the essay's opening advantage and its intellectual obligation. On Babel we have Rashi, Ramban, Bereshit Rabbah, Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer; on Nehemiah the classical Jewish interior is comparatively thin, which means the Jewish scholar is not receiving a text so much as receiving an invitation to build one. The essay argues that the encyclical's three analytical innovations (the private-transnational-power problem; the transhumanism critique; the new-forms-of-slavery condemnation) each correspond to a live Jewish legal category (respectively: *dina d'malkhuta dina* and *kehilla* governance; *issurei hana'ah*; and the rabbinic prohibition on reducing persons to instruments), and that together they produce a publicly deployable natural-law grammar for American private law that neither law-and-economics nor law-and-political-economy can supply.

## 3. Lede Paragraph (Tikvah register — finished prose, for Seth's reaction)

When Pope Leo XIV chose Nehemiah as the governing image for his encyclical on artificial intelligence, he made a move that the Jewish reader notices before the Catholic one does. We have been inside the book of Nehemiah for centuries, and the Talmud quotes it freely for historical data and halakhic propositions; but we have never, as a tradition, developed Nehemiah 2-6 as a governance parable the way we have developed the Tower of Babel. The Pope has done something we have not: he has read our texts with a purpose. That is not a criticism. It is an invitation, and this essay is an attempt to accept it from the inside out.

---

## 4. Closing Paragraph (Tikvah register — finished prose, for Seth's reaction)

The *Magnificat* says that God has filled the hungry with good things and sent the rich away empty. Leo XIV's encyclical reads that promise as institutional architecture: the hungry are filled not by redistribution alone but by governance structures that protect their dignity before the transaction is complete, that place accountability at every level of the wall, and that refuse to treat any human being as a brick to be placed or discarded by a project larger than itself. Jewish law arrived at the same architecture by a different route, through the *sachir*'s right to his wage at day's end, through the *kehilla*'s layered competencies, through the *issurei hana'ah* that holds certain things outside the market not because they have no value but because their value exceeds the market form. What the two traditions share at this level is not theology. It is grammar — a shared vocabulary for what law must do when power concentrates and persons are forgotten. That grammar is what Jewish lawyers are positioned to bring to the public square, and this encyclical is as good an occasion as any to begin.

**§ II. When Power Has No Sovereign: *Dina D'Malkhuta* and the AI Platform Problem.**

*Bridges 4 and 6 (institution as carrier of human flourishing; limits on what may be commodified). Encyclical ¶¶ 5, 95, 107-109.*

The encyclical diagnoses a structural failure: private actors who control AI platforms, infrastructure, and data have come to surpass the governing capacity of many states (¶ 5). This is not a regulation problem. It is a sovereignty problem. The halakhic tradition's most sophisticated resource here is not a prohibition but a premise: *dina d'malkhuta dina* (the law of the sovereign is law — Bava Kamma 113a-b; Bava Batra 54b-55a; *Mishneh Torah, Hil. Gezelah va-Aveidah* 5:11-18) holds because there is an accountable sovereign whose law has a subject. When that premise fails — when the entity exercising power is not a *malkhuta* in the functional sense — neither the doctrine nor the institutional submission it requires follows. The encyclical's subsidiarity prescription (¶¶ 108-109: "communities and intermediary organizations" must not be "reduced to passive recipients") is the positive prescription the Talmud leaves open. The *kehilla*'s governance architecture (the *bet din* system; *charamim*; discrete institutional competencies at each level) is the model for what subsidiarity looks like in practice when the state cannot reach.

Connection to Seth's *Open Secrets*: the DAO-sovereignty problem *Open Secrets* diagnoses is structurally identical to what Leo XIV describes — decentralized private power with no accountable sovereign, no *dina d'malkhuta* to invoke, no *bet din* to adjudicate. [OS pin TK — Seth to supply chapter/page reference for the specific *Open Secrets* passage where the DAO-sovereignty problem is diagnosed.] Natural-law anchor: *ST* I-II Q.95 a.1-2; *Centesimus Annus* (John Paul II, 1991) ¶ 48.

**§ III. Babel as Diagnosis: Transhumanism and the Brick-Over-Person Error.**

*Bridge 6 (limits on what may be commodified). Encyclical ¶¶ 12, 115-117, 126, 172.*

Leo XIV names transhumanism and posthumanism by name at ¶¶ 115-117 and returns to the "posthumanist mentality" at ¶ 172, condemning the view that treats the human person as "an object to be manipulated or a resource to be optimized." Bereshit Rabbah's Babel commentary gives the Jewish reader the interior of this diagnosis before the encyclical provides the framework [BEIT-MIDRASH PENDING: verified Hebrew text and transliteration of the brick-over-person passage (Bereshit Rabbah 38:8 or adjacent), with the precise claim that when a man fell building the tower the builders did not weep, but when a brick fell they mourned. This finding anchors the § III and § I argument that the Babel midrash and the transhumanism critique are the same observation across millennia].

The Jewish law category is *issurei hana'ah*: goods from which benefit may not be derived (Pesachim 21a-25b; *Mishneh Torah, Hil. Ma'akhalot Asurot* 8). These goods sit outside the commodity form not because they are worthless but because the Torah designates their value as incommensurable with market exchange. The human person is the paradigm: Lev 25:39-43 forbids the permanent reduction of an Israelite to fungible labor. Leo XIV's "new forms of slavery" built into AI supply chains (¶¶ 172-179) are the same violation in twenty-first-century form.

Michael Sandel, *The Case Against Perfection* (2007),[^2] correctly names the problem but cannot provide the principled tradition-grounded account of why certain human capacities sit outside optimization. Bostrom, Kurzweil, and Harari[^3] are the foil whose logic both traditions refute; they appear here to name the target, not as positive interlocutors. Connection to Seth's *Sovereign Charities*: the sphere-of-justice argument (that certain communal functions are destroyed when subjected to market logic) is the institutional parallel to this section's argument about persons.

**§ IV. Subsidiarity, the *Kehilla*, and the Layered Wall.**

*Bridge 4 (institution as carrier of human flourishing). Encyclical ¶¶ 13, 96, 108-109, 153.*

"No one is so weak that they cannot play their part... all are given their own section of the wall" (¶ 13). This is subsidiarity stated as building-site management, and it has a precise Jewish institutional analog: the *kehilla*'s allocation of distinct competencies to each institution (synagogue, *chevra kadisha*, *gemach*, *bet din*) replicates exactly the governance logic Leo XIV reads out of Nehemiah. The king's institutional limits in Deut 17:14-20 supply the Torah's top-down constraint on concentrated power. *Dina d'malkhuta dina*'s vertical structure supplies the bottom-up principle that legitimacy flows through accountable layers, not around them.

Natural-law anchors: *Quadragesimo Anno* ¶¶ 79-80 (subsidiarity defined); *Caritas in Veritate* (Benedict XVI, 2009) ¶ 57; *ST* I-II Q.95. Connection to Seth's LotF (Law of the Firm): the firm as institution, the assignment of discrete competencies, and the subsidiarity principle map onto corporate governance. The Nehemiah model is teaching material: each team rebuilds its own section of the wall, answerable upward but not micromanaged. Connection to AotV (*Architects of the Void*): the entrepreneurial lawyer as sub-creator maps onto Leo XIV's "wise architect" at ¶ 236 and onto the Nehemiah parable at ¶ 241.

**§ V. The Dignity of Work Before the Transaction Is Complete.**

*Bridge 5 (dignity of commercial work). Encyclical ¶¶ 148-156, 173.*

Leo XIV calls at ¶ 156 for "verifiable measures to protect employment, retraining and participation of workers" at the point of technological introduction, not merely as a reaction after displacement. The *sachir* law anticipates this by structure: the wage is owed by day's end (Lev 19:13; Deut 24:15), the employer's obligations attach before the worker seeks them (Bava Metzia 76a-77a; *Mishneh Torah, Hil. Sechirut* 11:1-3), and the first question at the heavenly tribunal is whether you dealt faithfully in business (Shabbat 31a: "*nasata v'natata b'emunah?*"). The protection is ex ante, not remedial. That is exactly what Leo XIV wants from AI governance: protection built into the architecture of the transition, not tacked on afterward.

The encyclical names *Rerum Novarum* (Leo XIII, 1891) themes as "still operative today" (¶ 30): the primacy of human labor over finance or productivity, and the inseparable link between Gospel proclamation and just social order. *Laborem Exercens* (John Paul II, 1981) ¶¶ 6-10, 19 is the middle term. The integralist interlocutors (Vermeule and Deneen) receive footnote-depth acknowledgment here as serious critics of liberalism's institutional decay, with the distinction that Leo XIV's subsidiarity-and-multilateralism response to concentrated power is not the integralist project.[^4]

**§ VI. Natural Law as Shared Grammar, Not Common Creed.**

*Bridges 4 and 6. Encyclical ¶¶ 229, 240-241.*

The convergences documented across §§ I-V are real and they are not coincidental: the Babel-syndrome diagnosis, the subsidiarity prescription, the limits-on-commodification doctrine, and the dignity-of-labor floor are not sectarian propositions. They are publicly deployable natural-law grammar. The essay closes by naming three questions these convergences cannot yet answer from within either tradition alone, pointing to the next round of legal scholarship: (1) what governance structures does subsidiarity actually prescribe for AI platforms under current positive law; (2) whether the *dina d'malkhuta dina* doctrine's functional premise can be extended to private actors who exercise quasi-sovereign power; (3) what the *issurei hana'ah* model contributes to the commodification-of-data question that *Sovereign Charities* and *Open Secrets* have both circled. Jonathan Sacks as the bridge figure throughout the closing: the Jewish and Catholic traditions share a grammar at the natural-law level without requiring agreement at the theological level. Walzer, *Spheres of Justice* (1983),[^5] as the modern analog for what that kind of shared grammar produces.
