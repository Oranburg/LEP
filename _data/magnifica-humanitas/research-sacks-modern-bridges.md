## II. Does Sacks Use Nehemiah 3 in This Book?

**Probable answer: no — but the absence is provisional, not confirmed.**

The Rabbi Sacks Legacy Trust publishes a full index for the book at rabbisacks.org/book-indexes/the-home-we-build-together-recreating-society/. That index contains no entry for Nehemiah in any form. The index covers Tanakh references from Genesis through the prophets; Nehemiah does not appear. The wall-building civic model Sacks deploys in the book is drawn from the Tabernacle (Mishkan) narrative in Exodus (Terumah), not from Nehemiah 3.

The Terumah-based argument — that shared constructive labor by voluntary contribution of diverse gifts creates belonging — is Sacks's operative civic model throughout the book. The title itself picks up Parashat Terumah language. His Nehemiah material, if any, is not indexed.

**Practical consequence:** Do not cite Sacks to Nehemiah 3 without a copy of the book in hand. The civic-renewal attribution to Sacks is accurate in general (Tabernacle / society-as-home model), but the specific Nehemiah 3 claim is unverified and the index evidence runs against it.

## III. Sacks as Bridge-Voice: Accurate Framing

Sacks is a genuine and notable modern bridge between biblical governance literature and civic-renewal argument. The correct attribution is to his Tabernacle-as-society model, not to Nehemiah 3 specifically. His argument that society is built, not received — that participation in shared constructive projects generates civic identity — applies to the Nehemiah wall-building reading by analogy, but Sacks himself appears not to make that move in this book.

Frame as: "a notable modern bridge between the biblical building-project literature and governance argument," not as "the interpreter of Nehemiah."

## IV. Other Modern Jewish Bridge-Voices on Nehemiah

### Yuval Levin — "The Nehemiah Option" (AEI, 2024 / Tikvah Ideas)

The clearest modern Jewish voice explicitly reading Nehemiah as a civic-renewal model. Levin, writing for AEI and presented at the Tikvah Jewish Leadership Conference, coins the phrase "The Nehemiah Option" and builds his argument on Nehemiah 4:17: workers rebuilding with a trowel in one hand and a spear in the other. His focus is on simultaneous building and defense of American institutions, not on Nehemiah 3's household-by-household allocation specifically, but the framing of Nehemiah as a civic renewal text is explicit and sustained. Published at aei.org/op-eds/american-renewal-the-nehemiah-option/ and tikvah.org/library/yuval-levin-on-american-renewal/. Also the subject of a *Mosaic* podcast (July 2025).

This is the most citable modern Jewish voice for Nehemiah-as-governance-model.

### Dov Zakheim — *Nehemiah: Statesman and Sage* (Maggid, 2016)

A full-length scholarly treatment reading Nehemiah as a political biography. Zakheim, former Pentagon comptroller, frames Nehemiah's covenant in ch. 10 as a constitutional governance model — "free men consenting to curtail their natural liberty for the sake of a greater good" — and places Nehemiah in dialogue with modern democratic theory. Reviewed in *Jewish Action*. Does not appear to focus on Nehemiah 3's household-by-household wall assignment specifically, but the governance reading of the whole book is thorough. Citable as a second notable bridge-voice.

Full citation: Dov S. Zakheim, *Nehemiah: Statesman and Sage* (Maggid 2016).

### Michael Walzer — *In God's Shadow: Politics in the Hebrew Bible* (Yale, 2012)

Walzer treats Ezra-Nehemiah primarily through the lens of the "holy seed" and Ezra's intermarriage polemic, not through Nehemiah 3's wall-building. His Nehemiah angle is exclusion and nativism, which is the opposite of the civic-renewal reading. Not usable as a bridge-voice for the governance / civic-renewal argument; usable as a counterpoint.

### David Novak — *Covenantal Rights* (Princeton, 2000); *The Jewish Social Contract* (Princeton, 2005)

Novak works from covenant theory to political theology but does not appear to read Nehemiah 3 specifically. His argument supports the general move (covenant as governance ground), but the Nehemiah citation would have to come from you, not from him.
