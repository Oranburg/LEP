# Audience Repositioning Advice: v3 → v4

## 1. Candidate Ledes (three options)

**A (sharpest):**
Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical reaches for two Hebrew Bible images — Babel and Jerusalem — to name the moral choice the AI age forces on humanity. A Jewish law professor reading Leo in the original language finds that the Pope's images argue more than any translation can deliver: the letters themselves carry the teaching.

**B (warmer):**
Pope Leo XIV built *Magnifica Humanitas* on two Hebrew words, and he chose better than he may have known. Babel and Jerusalem, read in the language in which they were first written, contain the very argument Leo is making — walled enclosure versus open foundation, extraction versus stewardship, the algorithm versus the covenant.

**C (most accessible, mildest Jewish-lens signal):**
A new Pope has given Catholics a framework for the AI age, and he grounded it in the Hebrew Bible. What *Magnifica Humanitas* says about Babel and Jerusalem becomes sharper — and more practically useful — when you read those words in their original Hebrew.

Recommendation: A. It names Seth's position (Jewish law professor), states the concrete promise (letters carry the teaching), and opens with Leo's argument, not with Hebrew philology.

## 2. Candidate Promises (second sentence of lede)

**A (concrete, strongest):**
The letters themselves carry the teaching.

**B (slightly more explanatory):**
The Hebrew alphabet encodes in its very shapes the argument Leo is making about AI, conscience, and the limits of algorithmic reason.

**C (most concrete about what the reader gets):**
Hearing those words in Hebrew reveals why Leo's images work — and why no algorithm can ever fulfill the guardian role G-d assigned to human beings.

Recommendation: C. It delivers a specific payoff (why the images work; why no algorithm qualifies) rather than a vague promise about layered meaning.

## 3. Author Note (one sentence, end-of-piece)

Seth Oranburg is a professor of business law at The Catholic University of America Columbus School of Law, where he writes on law, technology, and the governance of emerging systems from a Jewish legal perspective.

Note: Do not lead with "Jewish law professor" in the bio — that reads as credential-padding. The bio earns the Jewish lens without asserting it.

## 4. Closing Kicker Recommendation

"Has been waiting to say so" is the stronger close for this audience. "Has been speaking all along" is more concessive and softens the essay's whole claim at the moment it needs to land. The harder version is also more honest: Hebrew philology *has not been* a standard source for Catholic encyclical commentary, so the waiting-to-be-heard claim is accurate, not arrogant. Keep it.

## 5. Tone Audit

**Current register of the three sample paragraphs:**

- *Lamed* paragraph: academic-Jewish-studies. "Putting a lamed before shoresh often turns a concept into a verb" is a grammatical instruction, not a persuasive claim. The reader is being taught Hebrew, not walked through Leo's argument.
- *Bilbul* paragraph: scholarly, borderline accessible-public-intellectual. The close — "Hebrew shows why that pretense fails at the level of the letter" — is the best sentence in the paragraph because it ties philology to Leo. But the letter-by-letter buildup reads as exposition for its own sake.
- *Malakh* paragraph: accessible-public-intellectual, the essay's best-calibrated passage. Leo's encyclical language appears directly, the Hebrew root observation lands on a doctrinal claim, and "the only question that matters is who is doing the sending" is a genuine kicker.

**Current register overall:** scholarly / academic-Jewish-studies, with flashes of accessible-public-intellectual.

**Target register:** accessible-public-intellectual throughout, with one or two scholarly observations retained where they anchor the strongest claims.

**Specific tone moves for v4:**
1. Every paragraph that begins with a Hebrew letter or root name should open instead with Leo's encyclical claim, then introduce the Hebrew as confirmation rather than as curriculum.
2. Cut or compress the four "structural anatomy" paragraphs (lamed, bet, hey, Cain's name parenthetical). Keep the insight; drop the grammar lesson.
3. The *bilbul* paragraph works; trim the setup and open with "Leo calls this the Babel syndrome" rather than burying it at the end.
4. The *malakh* paragraph is the model for all others: lead with Leo, land on Hebrew, close with a claim the reader can carry out.
