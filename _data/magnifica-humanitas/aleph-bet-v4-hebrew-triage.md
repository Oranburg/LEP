## Classifications

**1. The *shoresh* methodology paragraph (line 33)**
COMPRESS. The Sefer Yetzirah citation and the root-as-Platonic-essence claim are both needed to justify the essay's whole method. But "the Pope chose his images well, and a Catholic reader who hears them in their original Hebrew finds a layer of teaching the translation cannot fully carry" — that's the lede; don't repeat the justification. Cut the second sentence to one clause: "Hebrew semantics cluster around a three-letter *shoresh* that carries the essence of the thing; the roots of Babel and Jerusalem show why Leo's images work."

**2. The *lamed* tallest-letter exposition (line 39)**
COMPRESS. The lamed-as-learning-and-action link is load-bearing for the Babel/Abel contrast that follows. But the example about *lishmor* is doing double duty here and again in the *shomer* paragraph. Drop it here; introduce it once, later, where it earns full development.

**3. The free-will paragraph (line 41)**
CUT. One transitional sentence ("humans have free will, so they can choose to learn good or bad things") does no philological work. The sentence is scaffolding the writer needed while drafting. A reader doesn't need it.

**4. The *tsimtsum* paragraph (line 43)**
CUT. *Tsimtsum* is a Lurianic kabbalistic concept, not a biblical-Hebrew observation, and Magnifica Humanitas operates entirely outside Kabbalah. The paragraph imports a second tradition without doing the work of justifying the import, and it doesn't connect to Leo at any point.

**5. The *hey* humility paragraph (line 45)**
COMPRESS. The *hey* / open-shape / humility observation is needed to set up the Abel-has-*hey*, Babel-does-not contrast. Keep that one sentence. Cut the Moshe Rabbeinu / "humblest man" gesture — it's a parenthetical that dilutes the structural argument.

**6. The *bet* materiality paragraph (line 47)**
KEEP. The prison-vs.-house reading of doubled *bet* is the clearest visual demonstration of the essay's method and maps directly onto Leo's Babel-syndrome language. Cannot cut without losing the architectural argument.

**7. The posture paragraph — tower vs shepherd (line 49)**
KEEP. "Babel was a tall tower … *Hevel* was a shepherd who grazed upon the land" earns its words by setting up the contrasting *lamed* readings in lines 51 and 55. It is the pivot between the letter-analysis and the argument.

**8. The *lamed* curling-smoke paragraph (line 51)**
KEEP. Abel's smoke as a physical *lamed* is the essay's most memorable image. Earns its space.

**9. The Kayin parenthetical (line 53)**
CUT. The *kof* / *nun sofit* descending-stroke analysis is internally interesting but the parenthetical interrupts the Abel-smoke → Babel transition and does no work the surrounding prose requires. Cain is already characterized; his letters add nothing the argument needs.

**10. The Abel's-smoke / Babel-replaces-*hey* paragraph (line 55)**
KEEP. This sentence ("Babel replaces the *hey* of Abel's humility with the *bet*, a plosive consonant") synthesizes the *hey* and *bet* sections and is the conceptual hinge of the Babel analysis. Essential.

**11. The extended *shomer* / *shamar* / *vav* paragraph (line 57)**
KEEP. "Am I my brother's keeper" is the connective tissue between Cain, the Eden mandate, and the AI-stewardship argument. The *vav*-at-center-of-*shomer* image is the paragraph's argumentative payoff. This one paragraph does the work of linking the philology to Leo's positive obligation. Keep in full.

**12. The *bilbul* keystone paragraph (line 61)**
KEEP. *Bilbul* is the structural keystone: Leo's own term ("Babel syndrome" MH ¶ 10), the Hebrew word for it, and its internal letter-prison enacting its meaning in one image. The Augustine citation at the end earns its place by anchoring the claim for a Catholic reader. Cannot cut.

**13. The *havah* / *Chavah* paragraph (line 63)**
CUT. "The echo is homiletic, not etymological" is the author undercutting his own observation. If the essay cannot stand behind it, it should not be in the essay. The paragraph adds a suggestive parallel that is immediately disclaimed, producing a net-zero argumentative result.

**14. The Jerusalem two-roots paragraph (line 67)**
KEEP. The *yod* / foundation / rejected-stone → Leo MH ¶ 16 is the cleanest Leo-engagement in the whole essay. Hebrew observation directly enables a reading of a specific encyclical paragraph. Cannot cut.

**15. The *shalem* paragraph (line 69)**
KEEP. *Shalem* as structural antidote to *bilbul* — the *lamed* finally unblocked — completes the Babel/Jerusalem arc that is the essay's spine. Essential.

**16. The Frankl / *Shema* / *shem* paragraph (line 73)**
COMPRESS. The Frankl quotation is indispensable (Leo MH ¶ 121, strong Catholic-Jewish convergence moment). The *Shema* / *shem* / Babel-*shem* triangle is good. But the Nehemiah sidebar at the end of the paragraph is a second example when one is enough. Cut the Nehemiah theology-of-*shema* sentence; keep the Babel inward-listening contrast.

**17. The *shomer* / *lishmor* grammatical-shift paragraph (line 75)**
KEEP. Present participle → infinitive of obligation is the most precise grammatical argument in the essay. It delivers the divine-vs.-human distinction Leo needs for his AI ethics and connects to Aquinas *imago Dei*. One of the three or four non-negotiable paragraphs.

**18. The Golem framework paragraph (line 77)**
KEEP. The Golem paragraph is the direct bridge to the AI argument. It also sets up the *malakh* paragraph. One sentence; tight; no fat.

**19. The *malakh* / *binah melakhutit* paragraph (line 79)**
KEEP. Modern-Hebrew AI terminology shares a root with *malakh*. This is verifiable, surprising, and maps onto Leo MH ¶ 99 with precision. The no-*yetzer* / no-covenant / no-repentance sequence is the essay's most direct theological claim about AI. The Aquinas life-in-trust citation closes it for Catholic readers. Keep in full.

**20. The *na'aseh ve-nishma* paragraph (line 81)**
KEEP. The Sinai covenantal acceptance before full terms are known is the positive answer to the Cain-sneer question. Without it, the essay ends at "AI cannot accept obligation" without saying what can. Essential conclusion logic.

**21. The keruvim / *mithapekhet* / Eden ending (line 83)**
KEEP. The closing image — outsourced guardianship becomes a flaming automated sword — is the argumentative payoff of everything that preceded. It also provides the rhetorical warrant for the final Leo appeal. Essential.

## The Keepable Core (if v4 targets 50 / 50 Hebrew-Leo balance)

Non-negotiable (8): *bet*-as-prison (6), tower-vs.-shepherd pivot (7), Abel's-smoke-as-*lamed* (8), *hey*-replaced-by-*bet* hinge (10), *shomer* / *vav* / stewardship (11), *bilbul* keystone (12), *shalem* antidote (15), *shomer*-participle-vs.-*lishmor*-infinitive (17), *malakh* / *binah melakhutit* (19), Golem (18), *na'aseh ve-nishma* (20), keruvim ending (21).

That is twelve, not eight. The essay's second half (from *shomer* to the ending) is almost entirely keeper material. The cut targets are concentrated in the first half, in the Abel/Cain analysis before the argument reaches Leo.

Expendable (can cut or compress to one sentence each): free-will transitional (3), *tsimtsum* (4), Kayin parenthetical (9), *havah*/*Chavah* (13), Nehemiah theology-of-*shema* tail (16 partial), *lamed* / *lishmor* duplication in line 39 (2 partial).

The *shoresh* methodology paragraph (1) compresses but stays. The *hey* paragraph (5) compresses to one sentence. The result is a tighter first half and a full second half — which is the right shape for an essay whose argument matures as it goes.
