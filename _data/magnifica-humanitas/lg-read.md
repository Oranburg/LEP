# Law and Governance: Deep Read Notes

## 1. Core Argument (two sentences)

Law does not merely regulate governance; it constitutes the conditions under which collective decision-making becomes possible, and the same legal structures that enable governance can destroy it. Legal scholarship currently lacks a unified vocabulary for asking what a legal rule does to an institution's capacity to make decisions, monitor conduct, impose sanctions, and adapt — this book provides one.

## 2. Thesis Paragraph (verbatim, from STRATEGIC-PLAN.md final draft)

> "Legal scholarship treats governance as a domain-specific problem. Corporate law scholars analyze boards through fiduciary duties. Administrative law scholars analyze agencies through delegation and judicial review. Securities regulation analyzes exchanges through oversight design. Each body of scholarship is deep within its domain. None asks the general question that cuts across all of them: when a legal rule acts on an institution, what does it do to that institution's capacity to make decisions, monitor conduct, impose sanctions, and adapt over time?
>
> This book asks that question. Its central claim is that law does not merely regulate governance. Law constitutes the conditions under which governance becomes possible, and the same legal structures that enable collective decision-making can destroy it. A legal rule that increases decision-making authority while eliminating adaptive capacity does not strengthen governance; it creates brittle power. That duality is not visible from within any single doctrinal field. It becomes visible only when governance itself is treated as the object of legal analysis.
>
> The book does not romanticize institutional power. Governance can exclude, entrench, coerce, and discriminate. The analytical framework developed here asks what law does to governance capacity, not whether that capacity is being exercised justly. Those are separate questions, both essential, and the framework is incomplete without the second. But the second cannot be answered well without the first, and legal scholarship currently lacks a unified vocabulary to ask the first at all."

## 3. Chapter / Part Structure

**Front Matter + Introduction**
Opening examples: Silver v. NYSE (1963), Michigan State/Nassar, Israeli daycare fine study. Each shows legal intervention addressing a visible problem while degrading the governance institution that managed the problem. Establishes the gap: none of the predecessor traditions (legal institutionalism, commons scholarship, corporate governance, transaction cost economics) supplied a legal method for evaluating what law does to governance capacity.

**Part I: Defining Governance and Its Legal Conditions (Chapters 1–4)**
- Ch. 1: Governance Defined — four-element functional definition: decision-making, monitoring, sanctions, adjustment.
- Ch. 2: Languages of Governance — surveys seven field-specific vocabularies (corporate, new governance, political science, Ostrom/commons, nonprofit, network, DAO/blockchain). Each is entity-bound; none travels across institutional forms.
- Ch. 3: Intellectual Traditions — positions the book against legal institutionalism, Fuller, Hart & Sacks, Ostrom, Williamson. Closest predecessors but none asked the constitutive legal question.
- Ch. 4: Legal Conditions — six (or five; count inconsistency flagged for repair) legal conditions governance institutions require: permission/recognition, membership boundaries, external enforceability, fiduciary structure, standing, accountability.

**Part II: Theory (Chapters 5–8)**
- Ch. 5: Club Goods — Buchanan's 1965 framework applied to governance. Governance is excludable, nonrivalrous up to congestion, quality-dependent on membership constraints. Currently framed as "governance IS a club good"; REWRITE-PLAN calls for repositioning to "club good as primary analytical lens."
- Ch. 6: Spillovers and Joint Products — Cornes & Sandler joint products framework. Governance produces both member benefits and external spillovers (monitoring → disclosure; sanctions → deterrence; adjustment → resilience).
- Ch. 7: Degradation Mechanisms — seven mechanisms by which law destroys governance capacity: procedural ossification, norm displacement, and five others. Key original contributions are mechanisms 2 and 3.
- Ch. 8: The Method — seven-step analytical method for evaluating what a legal rule does to governance. Currently under revision; the bidirectional question (law enables OR degrades) is the novel move. Four outcomes: enable / discipline / degrade / mixed.

**Part III: Applications (Chapters 9–15)**
- Ch. 9: Network Governance — Diamond Dealers Club, Bernstein's DDC and cotton-trade studies, Greif on Maghribi traders, Milgrom-North-Weingast Champagne Fairs. Seven-step method applied in full.
- Ch. 10: NYSE / Silver — 60-year arc from Silver v. NYSE (1963) through demutualization, Alpine, Jarkesy, Loper Bright. "Most original doctrinal contribution" per reviewer.
- Ch. 11: Corporate Governance — Shareholder rights movement as category error (treating a club good as a public good). Say-on-pay, proxy advisors, mandatory voting. Dual-class literature flagged for addition (Bebchuk & Kastiel; Gompers/Ishii/Metrick valuation discount).
- Ch. 12: Universities and Nonprofits — "Sovereign charity" and "exclusive inclusion" concepts. Standing proposal under Thole flagged for development.
- Ch. 13: Knowledge Institutions — Law reviews, peer review systems. Currently cut to 49 lines; adverse selection application under revision.
- Ch. 14: Anti-Discrimination — Strongest sociologically honest section. Governance vs. exclusion entanglement treated directly.
- Ch. 15: Research Horizons — To be consolidated with Conclusion.

**Conclusion**
Current draft leads with "governance has club good structure" — REWRITE-PLAN calls for reframing to lead with the jurisprudential reorientation. Closing sentence: "Legal analysis has learned to see rights, incentives, and remedies with great precision; its next task is to learn to see governance, and to judge law by what law makes of it."

## 4. Doctrinal / Theoretical Lineage and Six Bridges Mapping

L&G is not a natural-law or morality-of-markets project in its current form. It is an institutional jurisprudence project drawing primarily on institutional economics (Williamson, Coase, Buchanan, Ostrom), corporate law theory (Berle-Means, Bainbridge, Bebchuk, Blair-Stout), administrative law, and club good economics. The bridges below identify where the natural-law tradition has potential purchase on L&G's argument, not where the book currently makes those connections.

**Bridge 4 (Institution as carrier of human flourishing)** is the dominant structural alignment. L&G's entire argument is that governance institutions are not merely efficient coordination mechanisms but the preconditions of collective self-governance — which is the Aristotelian-Thomistic claim about the polis restated at the level of legal doctrine. Subsidiarity (*Quadragesimo Anno* ¶¶ 79-80; *Centesimus Annus* ¶ 48) is the closest Catholic social teaching parallel: the principle that decisions live at the lowest competent level maps directly onto L&G's argument that law's imposition of higher-level review on lower-level governance institutions destroys the institutional capacity it claims to improve. The *kehilla* and *bet din* are the Jewish institutional parallels. L&G does not invoke any of these; they are available.

**Bridge 2 (Moral architecture of property)** connects where L&G deals with governance of shared resources — the Ostrom commons chapters, the network chapters, the DAO governance work in *Open Secrets*. The natural-law qualification on property (Aquinas, *ST* II-II Q.66; Lev 25) that orients it toward the common good is structurally parallel to L&G's argument that governance capacity is a shared resource that law must not destroy. The jubilee and sabbatical year structures (Lev 25) represent institutionalized adaptation mechanisms — direct analogues to L&G's fourth governance element (adjustment).

**Bridge 5 (Dignity of commercial work)** has secondary relevance to Chapter 11 and the say-on-pay chapters: when governance degrades into process (ISS-driven rubber-stamping), it displaces the practical judgment of actual directors, which is a dignity-of-work claim in the boardroom. The Talmud's "*nasata v'natata b'emunah*" (Shabbat 31a) names the standard L&G is trying to preserve: governance means dealing faithfully in business, not completing forms.

**Bridge 6 (Limits on what may be commodified)** surfaces in the Tether/dual-class/SPAC stacking discussion from the Foldy call: the VOC narrative and the Nikola/Binance/Tether pattern all involve removing governance accountability in exchange for liquidity or returns, a structural analog to selling something that should not be sold. Lev 25:23 ("the land shall not be sold in perpetuity") encodes the same insight as L&G's degradation thesis: there are institutional goods that perpetual alienation destroys.

## 6. Magnifica Humanitas Convergences (strongest 3–4)

**Convergence 1: Subsidiarity and governance destruction.** *Magnifica Humanitas* Chapter 2 (subsidiarity, common good, solidarity) articulates subsidiarity as the principle that higher-level authority must not displace the capacity of lower-level institutions to manage their own affairs — not merely as administrative efficiency but as a condition of human dignity. L&G's central degradation thesis is the legal instantiation of subsidiarity's violation: when law imposes procedural review, mandatory participation, or remedy structures that destroy an institution's capacity for self-governance, it commits the act subsidiarity forbids. The Israeli daycare fine (norm displacement, L&G's degradation mechanism 3 or adjacent) is the degradation of a governance institution that subsidiarity would have protected. Silver v. NYSE (the introduction's lead case) is sixty years of subsidiarity violation in securities regulation. The convergence is structural, not merely analogical.

**Convergence 2: Concentrated private power and the technocratic paradigm.** *Magnifica Humanitas* Chapter 3 names two threats: concentrated private power that escapes accountability, and the technocratic paradigm that replaces practical judgment with process. L&G's dual-class / SPAC / stacking analysis (from the Foldy call) addresses the first directly: legal structures that concentrate control while eliminating accountability are not just efficiency problems but governance destructions. L&G's say-on-pay analysis (Ch. 11) is the technocratic paradigm applied to corporate governance: ISS proxy templates replace director judgment with a compliance checklist. The encyclical's diagnosis and L&G's analytical apparatus are naming the same phenomenon from different standpoints.

**Convergence 3: The institution as more than an efficient contract.** *Magnifica Humanitas* consistently treats institutions as carriers of human vocation, not merely as coordination devices. L&G's jurisprudential reorientation makes the parallel legal move: governance institutions are the objects legal analysis has been missing, not reducible to the bilateral relationships they host. L&G's critique of corporate governance scholarship (it can only see the board-shareholder dyad, not the governance institution) maps onto the encyclical's critique of markets that reduce everything to transaction. The book's closing sentence — "legal analysis has learned to see rights, incentives, and remedies; its next task is to learn to see governance" — is, structurally, the encyclical's call to see institutions as moral objects, stated in legal-theoretical terms.

**Convergence 4: The VOC and the dual-master problem as a moral template.** *Magnifica Humanitas* Chapter 3 (if it addresses the technocratic paradigm and the subordination of politics to finance, following the line from *Laudato Si'* and *Laudate Deum*) speaks to governance captured by a single interest at the expense of the common good. The VOC's dual-master problem — directors who were supposed to serve shareholders but served the Dutch state, leaving shareholders with a secondary market and no accountability — is the historical prototype of every L&G degradation case: governance institutions captured or hollowed out, leaving the form of accountability while removing its substance. The encyclical's common-good framework names what was missing.
