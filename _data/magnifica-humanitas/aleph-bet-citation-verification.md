## 1. Aquinas, ST I Q.93 a.1 — imago Dei / rational governance

CORRECTED — sharper pinpoint recommended.

Q.93 a.1 confirms that the image of God exists in man and is grounded in intellectual nature. It establishes the *presence* of the imago Dei in humans. The draft's use ("Aquinas calls this capacity the imago Dei") is defensible, but the sharpest pinpoint for the claim that the image consists specifically in the human capacity for rational self-governance is **Q.93 a.4** (image present in all humans through their intellectual nature, the natural aptitude for knowing and loving God) and especially **Q.93 a.6** (the image is located in the mind, not the body — "the image of God is not found even in the rational creature except in the mind"). If the essay's argument is that the imago Dei is the capacity for covenantal governance, a.6 is sharper than a.1. Recommend: **ST I Q.93 a.6**, or cite both: **ST I Q.93 a.1, a.6**.

Verified against: New Advent (Dominican Province translation), https://www.newadvent.org/summa/1093.htm

## 2. Aquinas, ST II-II Q.64 a.1 — interior life held in trust from God

CORRECTED.

Q.64 a.1 is on whether it is lawful to kill plants and animals. It contains no discussion of the human interior life, stewardship of the person, or life held in divine trust. The right pinpoint is **ST II-II Q.64 a.5** (on suicide), where Aquinas explicitly states: "Life is God's gift to man, and is subject to His power, Who kills and makes to live." The article reasons that taking one's own life transgresses against God because humans hold life in divine trust rather than as owners — precisely the stewardship/delegation argument the draft makes in the malakh passage.

Replace: ST II-II Q.64 a.1 → **ST II-II Q.64 a.5**

Verified against: New Advent (Dominican Province translation), https://www.newadvent.org/summa/3064.htm

## 3. Augustine, De Civitate Dei XVI.4 — Babel's dispersion as providential refusal of self-glorification

CONFIRMED with a gloss note.

XVI.4 is Augustine's Babel chapter. It covers the tower, the pride of the builders, the divine punishment through linguistic confusion, and the resulting dispersion. The characterization "providential refusal of human self-glorification" is a fair interpretive gloss: Augustine describes the punishment as targeted at human pride ("As the tongue is the instrument of domination, in it pride was punished"). The dispersion is framed as divine judgment against pride, which the draft summarizes as providential refusal of self-glorification. The gloss is not inaccurate, though readers expecting precise paraphrase should know Augustine stresses punitive judgment more than providential design. XVI.3 is genealogical; XVI.5 addresses God's "coming down" as metaphor. XVI.4 is the correct chapter.

Verified against: New Advent, https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/120116.htm

## 4. Sefer Yetzirah 2:2 — twenty-two letters as building blocks of creation

CONFIRMED — 2:2 is the stronger pinpoint; 2:1 is also relevant.

SY 2:1 introduces the foundational structure: "The foundations are the twenty-two letters, three mothers, seven double, and twelve single letters." SY 2:2 states the divine creative act: "He hath formed, weighed, transmuted, composed, and created with these twenty-two letters every living being, and every soul yet uncreated." Both verses bear on the claim, but 2:2 is the passage that most explicitly names the letters as the instruments by which God created all things. The draft's citation of 2:2 is correct. If space permits, citing both (SY 2:1–2) is more complete; 2:2 alone is sufficient.

Verified against: Sefaria (Voices, chapter 2 commentary), https://voices.sefaria.org/sheets/503863
