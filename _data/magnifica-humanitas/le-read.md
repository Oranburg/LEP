# LE (Exercens / Exercitor): Read for *Magnifica Humanitas* Engagement

## Core Argument (two sentences)

Pope John Paul II's *Laborem Exercens* (1981) takes its title from the same Latin participial form (*exercens*, the active present participle of *exercere*) that Roman commercial and maritime law used for the entrepreneur-operator of a venture (*exercitor navis*, subject of the *actio exercitoria*, Digest 14.1, Ulpian), and the shared morphology is not decorative: both traditions name, in the active voice and continuous aspect, a human agent constituted by sustained productive activity. The essay traces this convergence through Roman law, the encyclical, and the broader Catholic social tradition, argues that the standard divide between labor law and business associations rests on a category the Latin tradition does not recognize, and draws three implications for how a Catholic law school teaches the law of work and enterprise.

## Seth's Read of *Laborem Exercens*

Seth reads LE as a personalist anthropology of work organized around one grammatical decision: the encyclical's title modifies *homo* with a present active participle, identifying the human being not by a property (worker, laborer) but by an activity in process. The two pivotal moves are (a) the priority of the subjective over the objective dimension of work — the person doing the work matters more than the thing produced — and (b) the refusal of both Marxist alienation and capitalist instrumentalism by insisting that work constitutes the agent through its exercise. The encyclical's second section (§§ 5-7) carries the core of this argument; Seth plans to verify the English translation against the Latin at those passages before drafting.

## Jewish Business Law Connections (Bridge 5)

Seth does not yet have a dedicated Jewish law section in the LE scaffold, but the connections are available in the corpus and should be built into the essay:

- The wage paid same day (Lev 19:13; Deut 24:15) and the laborer's right not to be oppressed sit inside the same anthropological claim: the worker's dignity is real and its violation is a direct injustice (*Mishneh Torah, Hil. Sechirut* 11-13; *Choshen Mishpat* § 331).
- The Talmudic question asked at the heavenly tribunal (*nasata v'natata b'emunah* — "did you deal faithfully in business?", Shabbat 31a) equates commercial work with moral self-constitution in precisely the way LE's participial grammar does: the person *is* her faithful exercise of the venture.
- The *po'el* (laborer) / *sachir* (hired worker) distinction in halakha corresponds to LE's subjective/objective distinction: the *po'el* who stops work midday due to changed circumstances (Bava Metzia 76a-77a) exercises judgment the *sachir* bound by a fixed term cannot, and the halakha treats this asymmetry as morally significant.
- Maimonides's *Hil. Sechirut* 11 on the employer's duty to the worker and the worker's duty to work faithfully supplies the covenantal vocabulary that LE's personalism imports without naming.

These connections belong in the essay's Section V (implications for a Catholic law school), where the Jewish legal heritage of a Catholic institution's civilizational predecessor is one of the three structural payoffs.

## What LE Establishes That *Magnifica Humanitas* Extends

*Magnifica Humanitas* (Leo XIV, 2026) is already in the LE source file (03 - Catholic Doctrine Sources.md) as the terminus of the encyclical lineage. The specific passages flagged are §§ 241-242 (Nehemiah, the wall, the New Jerusalem).

LE establishes: the worker is constituted by sustained, active exercise of work; this activity has both subjective dignity (the person) and objective output (the product); neither the Marxist (who reduces work to alienated output) nor the capitalist (who reduces the worker to a unit of labor input) captures the *exercens*.

*Magnifica Humanitas* extends this to the AI age by asking what happens when the machine exercises the *exercere* instead. The encyclical's §§ 241-242 image (each family assigned its own section of the wall; Nehemiah 3 throughout) is the counter-claim: the work of rebuilding cannot be delegated to an algorithm because the rebuilding is constitutive of the rebuilder. The LEP fellowship essay should articulate this extension explicitly: AI can automate the objective dimension of work (*technê*, the thing produced) but cannot be the subject of the subjective dimension (*phronêsis*, the person constituted). The grammar of *exercens* says so: the participle is active, present, and human.

## Strongest Single Move for *Magnifica Humanitas*

The philological find, if it survives verification: *exercens* in the encyclical title and *exercitor* in the Roman commercial law are the same grammatical figure doing the same anthropological work in two traditions that did not borrow from each other at the relevant moment, both choosing the active participle over available alternatives. This matters for the LEP engagement because it means the Catholic law school's claim to teach business law differently is not a pious overlay on secular doctrine but a recovery of a vocabulary the secular doctrine already carried. The fellowship that grows out of this trains lawyers to be *exercens* in helping clients be *exercitor* — active, sustained exercisers of judgment in service of another's active, sustained exercise of a venture.

## JP–LE Relation

LE supplies the anthropological norm (the person constituted by the sustained exercise of a venture) that JP shows being systematically destroyed by the Deskilling Loop. JP is diagnosis; LE is norm. Both converge on the AI-age question that *Magnifica Humanitas* poses: when the machine displaces the *exercens*, what has been lost and what can be recovered? The LEP fellowship pitch can use this two-project structure as its intellectual backbone — JP establishes the problem with institutional precision, LE establishes the anthropological claim with Latin philological depth, and *Magnifica Humanitas* names the current moment in which both come to a head.
