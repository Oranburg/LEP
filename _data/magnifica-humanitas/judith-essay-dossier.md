## 2. The Encyclical: *Magnifica Humanitas*

Full title: *Magnifica Humanitas: On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*. Encyclical Letter of His Holiness Pope Leo XIV. Given at Rome, 15 May 2026.

Structure: Introduction + 5 chapters + Conclusion. Five chapters titled (1) A Dynamic Approach Faithful to the Gospel; (2) Foundations and Principles of the Social Doctrine of the Church; (3) Technology and Dominance: The Grandeur of Humanity in Light of the Promises of AI; (4) Safeguarding Humanity at a Time of Transformation: Truth, Work, Freedom; (5) The Culture of Power and the Civilization of Love. Plus Conclusion (the Magnificat as interpretive key).

### Key paragraphs to engage

- **MH ¶ 5** — "It must also be recognized that nuclear energy, biotechnology, information technology... have given those with the knowledge, and especially the economic resources to use them, an impressive dominance over the whole of humanity and the entire world." Concentrated private power surpassing states.
- **MH ¶ 7-10** — the Babel/Nehemiah opening images. "Two roads ahead of us." Babel = humanity without God, uniformity, dehumanization. Jerusalem = God at the centre, diversity as source of richness, brotherhood/collaboration.
- **MH ¶ 10** — the "Babel syndrome": "the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language, even a digital one, can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance."
- **MH ¶ 13** — "No one can single-handedly bear the weight of the challenges the world is facing... this is the logic of subsidiarity."
- **MH ¶ 16** — the Magnificat reference: "Thus, the rejected stones, the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us, will become the cornerstone." Echoes Psalm 118:22, taken up in Matthew 21:42, Mark 12:10, 1 Peter 2:7.
- **MH ¶ 47-50** — human person as image of God; human dignity as innate, infinite, applies to everyone.
- **MH ¶ 67** — new forms of property (algorithms, platforms, data) under universal destination of goods.
- **MH ¶ 99** — AI's limits: AI does not "undergo experiences, possess a body, feel joy or pain," or "have a moral conscience."
- **MH ¶ 110** — "To disarm AI does not mean rejecting technology, but preventing it from dominating humanity."
- **MH ¶ 114** — "The quality of a civilization is measured not by the power of its means, but by the care it is able to offer."
- **MH ¶ 121** — Viktor Frankl quoted: even those who entered the gas chambers "upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the Shema Yisrael on their lips" testified to the irreducible grandeur of the human person.
- **MH ¶ 128** — "For an algorithm, an error is a flaw to be corrected; for a person, however, it can be a catalyst for profound change."
- **MH ¶ 134** — "The search for truth is an essential element of democracy."
- **MH ¶ 150** — *"AI frequently forces workers to adapt to the speed and demands of machines, rather than machines being designed to support those who work."*
- **MH ¶ 163** — the insufficiency of the invisible hand.
- **MH ¶ 171** — "Freedom in the digital age is not merely a matter of interiority but also a public concern."
- **MH ¶ 173** — the hidden labor chain (data labelers, content moderators, mineral extractors).
- **MH ¶ 178** — data colonialism.
- **MH ¶ 198** — "No algorithm can make war morally acceptable."
- **MH ¶ 219** — "Nothing is lost with peace, whereas with war everything can be lost."
- **MH ¶ 241-242** — Nehemiah image: each family its own section of the wall.
- **Conclusion** — *"The Magnificat as a key for interpretation: upending power and recognizing the value of humility."* The final phrase Leo writes: *"Let us become builders of communion, rather than architects of Babel"* (MH ¶ 16 in the conclusion's numbering).

### The two biblical images Leo deploys

Introduction ¶¶ 7-10:

> "I would like to bring to mind two scenes from the Bible: the construction of the Tower of Babel (cf. *Gen* 11:1-9) and the rebuilding of the walls of Jerusalem (cf. *Neh* 2-6)."

Babel: "a project conceived without reference to God, supported by a uniformity that eliminated diversity... When a city is built on pride and the claim to self-sufficiency, communication breaks down, languages are confused and people no longer understand each other."

Nehemiah: "The narrative shows how the city is reborn, not through the initiative of one man, but through the shared responsibility of all: men, women, priests, artisans, heads of households and young people all play a part. It is an undertaking with God at the center, which rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones."

## 3. The Hebrew Letters — properties Seth has developed

### Bet (ב)

- The first letter of *Bereshit* (Genesis), the first word of Torah
- "The most material of letters" — the threshold between heaven and the world
- Shape: rigid walled enclosure closed on three sides, open only on the left (which in Hebrew reading order is "after her" — open to what comes next)
- A house in which the Lord may dwell ("bayit" — house — begins with bet)
- Babel doubles bet: בבל has two bets, sealing the word at the keystone level (the root structure ב-ל-ב is bet-lamed-bet, a wall-trap-wall)
- The doubled bet = the Babel syndrome at the level of the letter

### Lamed (ל)

- The tallest letter in the Hebrew alphabet
- The only letter that rises above the script's headline (the imaginary line at the top of the letter-heights)
- "About stretching out, indicating learning and action"
- Putting a lamed before a shoresh turns a concept into a verb (the infinitive prefix)
- Examples: *lishmor* (לִשְׁמֹר, to guard) and *livnot* (לִבְנוֹת, to build) — both have lamed prefixes
- Cain's children chose the tower; the lamed's verb (to build, to guard) was used for the wrong thing
- In *bilbul* (the word for confusion), the lameds are trapped between the bets

### Hey (ה)

- The least assertive sound in human speech, an unvoiced exhale
- Shape: open, leaving gaps for the divine to enter (the letter is broken on the upper-left, leaving a visible gap)
- The breath out of which life is made
- Abel (הבל) begins with hey. Babel (בבל) does not.
- That one-letter difference is the moral architecture of the choice Cain refused
- In some Jewish thought, *hey* is associated with God's name and the breath of creation (the second hey of YHVH)
- Note for the essay: do not have Hey deliver a long speech. Let her presence land as silence, or as a single gesture.

### Vav (ו)

- The straight, vertical letter
- Symbol of the conduit between heaven and earth
- At the center of *shomer* (שׁוֹמֵר) when written fully; the upright guardian
- In *bilbul* (בלבול), the vav sits at the structural center, the conduit of divine light imprisoned in the closed system

### Other letters Seth has touched

- **Shin (ש)** — three-branched; refusal of uniformity; central in *shalem* and *Shema*
- **Mem sofit (ם)** — fully sealed; the letter of concealed depth; in *shalem*
- **Yod (י)** — smallest letter; the divine spark; in *yarah* (to lay a foundation), the first root of Jerusalem
- **Kof (ק)** and **Nun sofit (ן)** — descenders; bracket Cain's name (קין), pulling toward the dirt
- **Aleph (א)** — silent; the letter of the unutterable name; foundational

### The transliteration rule

All Hebrew terms in transliteration are italicized: *shoresh*, *lamed*, *hey*, *bet*, *vav*, *shomer*, *lishmor*, *livnot*, *bilbul*, *shalem*, *Shema*, *shem*, *shema* (verb), *na'aseh lanu shem*, *na'aseh ve-nishma*, *Kayin*, *Chavah*, *Hevel*, *Bavel*, *malakh*, *binah melakhutit*, *yetzer hara*, *yetzer tov*, *keli*, *keruvim*, *mithapekhet*, *Golem*, *semicha*, *Bamidbar*, *Bereshit*, *parashat*, *hefker*, *midbar*, *davar*.

Hebrew script (בית, למד, etc.) is preserved as-is.

Latin and work titles also italicized: *Magnifica Humanitas*, *De Civitate Dei*, *Summa Theologiae* or *ST*, *Sefer Yetzirah*, *imago Dei*, *Tiferet Yisrael*, *Bereshit Rabbah*, *Bamidbar Rabbah*, *Tanchuma*, *Otiyot d'Rabbi Akiva*, *Le Livre des Questions*.

English biblical / historical names stay plain: Abel, Cain, Eve, Adam, Babel, Jerusalem, Moshe Rabbeinu, Nehemiah, Hebrew (the language).

## 4. The Babel Reading

Genesis 11:1-9. The people speak one language and one set of words (*sefah achat u-devarim achadim*). They settle in Shinar. They say to one another, *"havah nilbenah levenim"* — come, let us make bricks. They build a tower with its top in the heavens, to *na'aseh lanu shem* — make a name for ourselves. God comes down to see; God confuses (*balal*) their language; the city is named Babel because of the confusion.

### The *bilbul* reading (the keystone)

The Hebrew word for confusion is *bilbul* (בלבול). Its root structure (the 3-letter shoresh): ב-ל-ב (bet-lamed-bet). This is the keystone:

- **Two bets** at either end of the root: walls.
- **Lamed** trapped between them: the verb of learning and reaching upward, imprisoned.
- The full noun form בלבול adds *vav* (conduit) and a second *lamed*. The vav is the trapped conduit; the second lamed extends the trapping.

The structure ENACTS what it describes: the word for confusion is itself a confused-and-trapped structure. The lamed cannot reach upward when it is sealed between bets.

This reading maps onto Leo's "Babel syndrome" (MH ¶ 10) at the level of the letter: a single closed language that admits no external light and permits no escape from its own loops.

### The shomer/shamar/lishmor cluster

- *Shamar* (root ש-מ-ר) = to guard, to keep, to watch
- *Shomer* (שׁוֹמֵר) = present participle, "one who guards" — used of God in Psalm 121:4 ("He who guards Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep")
- *Lishmor* (לִשְׁמֹר) = infinitive, "to guard" — the form in which the Torah issues commandments; the verb of obligation
- The same root is in Cain's question: "Am I my brother's keeper (*shomer*)?" (Genesis 4:9)
- The grammatical shift from divine continuous participle (*shomer*) to human infinitive of obligation (*lishmor*) is the shift from divine presence to human covenant. We are to guard because God guards.
- Aquinas calls this capacity the *imago Dei* (*ST* I Q.93 a.6).

### Other Babel material

- Cain's worldview: extraction rather than stewardship
- *Kayin* (קין) = "acquisition" (from the verb *kanah*, to acquire)
- Cain's name is bracketed by *kof* (ק) and *nun sofit* (ן) — both letters whose strokes descend below the baseline, gripping the dirt
- Augustine, *De Civitate Dei* XVI.4: Babel's dispersion is providential refusal of human self-glorification
- *Bereshit Rabbah* 38 (and parallels) on Babel midrash
- *Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer* 24:6: the famous brick-over-person midrash. Babel's builders mourned a fallen brick (*levenah*) more than a fallen worker. Hebrew text and transliteration in the philological deep-dive file.

## 5. The Jerusalem Reading

Jerusalem (ירושלם / ירושלים). The rabbinic reading gives the name two roots:

- **Yarah** (י-ר-ה) = to lay a foundation; the *yod* (smallest letter, divine spark) anchors this root.
- **Shalem** (ש-ל-ם) = complete, whole, at peace. *Shin* + *lamed* + *mem sofit*.

The *shalem* reading is the structural antidote to *bilbul*:

- *Shin* (ש) — three branches, a refusal of uniformity
- *Lamed* (ל) — appears again, now finally unblocked, free to reach upward
- *Mem sofit* (ם) — fully sealed (unlike the standard *mem*, מ, which is open at the bottom); the letter of concealed truth, the depth beyond what any system can fully map

Where *bilbul* traps the lamed between two bets, *shalem* releases it.

Foundation + wholeness: the city is defined by both before a single stone is laid.

### Nehemiah and the wall

Nehemiah 3 organizes the rebuilding of Jerusalem's wall *by family*. Each family takes its own section. Each family is accountable for the part that protects their own house.

Nehemiah 4:17 (often cited by Yuval Levin in his "Nehemiah Option" essays at AEI/Tikvah Ideas, 2024-2025): builders worked *yado achat osah ba-melachah v'yado achat machazeket ha-shalach* — one hand at the work, one hand on the weapon.

The principle of subsidiarity: governance at the lowest competent level, with accountability to persons. Catholic social teaching's name for what the Nehemian wall enacts. MH ¶ 13 cites it explicitly: "this is the logic of subsidiarity."

Dov S. Zakheim, *Nehemiah: Statesman and Sage* (Maggid 2016) — the sustained modern Jewish scholarly governance reading of Nehemiah.

### The Shema and *shem*

Frankl's witness, quoted by Leo at MH ¶ 121: the *Shema Yisrael* on the lips of those entering the gas chambers.

- *Shema* (שמע, root ש-מ-ע) = to hear, to listen
- *Shem* (שם) = name
- The first two letters (shin, mem) are shared between the two words. They are not the same root (Shema has a third letter, ayin; shem ends at mem). But the rabbinic ear hears the resonance.
- The builders of Babel wanted to *na'aseh lanu shem* (make a name for themselves). Their listening was entirely inward; they could no longer *shema* (hear) their neighbor or God.

### The Magnificat as interpretive key

Leo names the Magnificat as the interpretive key in his Conclusion. The Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55):

> "My soul magnifies the Lord... He has shown strength with his arm; he has scattered the proud in the thoughts of their hearts; he has put down the mighty from their thrones, and exalted those of low degree; he has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich he has sent empty away."

The Magnificat is in Greek in the New Testament. But it is in conversation with Hannah's Hebrew prayer at the temple, 1 Samuel 2:1-10:

> "My heart exults in the Lord... The bows of the mighty are broken, but the feeble gird on strength. Those who were full have hired themselves out for bread, but those who were hungry have ceased to hunger... He raises up the poor from the dust; he lifts the needy from the ash heap..."

Mary and Hannah sing the same song of reversal. The Hebrew is the older voice. Catholic readers know the Magnificat; many do not know it is Hannah's. The letters of the Holy Language have been singing this song for as long as it has existed.

## 6. The AI Reading

### The *malakh* etymology

Modern Hebrew for *artificial intelligence* is *binah melakhutit* (בינה מלאכותית).

- *Binah* = understanding, discernment
- *Melakhutit* = artificial, from the root מ-ל-א-כ
- That same root yields *malakh* (מלאך), the Hebrew word for angel — or more precisely, *messenger*

The Hebrew etymology is precise: artificial intelligence is being called, in modern Hebrew, *messenger-like intelligence*.

In Jewish tradition, an angel is **pure function**. An angel has:
- No *yetzer hara* (evil inclination)
- And, critically, no *yetzer tov* (good inclination)
- No capacity to disobey
- No capacity to repent
- No capacity to love
- No capacity to enter into a covenant with God

The angel executes the mission of the one who sent it. The only question that matters is who is doing the sending.

This maps directly onto Leo at MH ¶ 99: AI does not "undergo experiences, possess a body, feel joy or pain," or "have a moral conscience." The Hebrew root already knew this. The modern Hebrew vocabulary *binah melakhutit* is itself a theological judgment.

Aquinas reaches the same conclusion from a different angle: the interior life is something the human holds in trust from God (*ST* II-II Q.64 a.5).

### The Golem

Biblical Hebrew has no word for an autonomous machine. The closest framework is the *Golem* — a mystical automaton of clay possessing immense power but no soul, no conscience capable of receiving a commandment.

The Maharal of Prague (R. Judah Loew, 16th c.) is the most famous figure associated with the Golem tradition. The Maharal also developed the systematic letter-analysis method this essay uses (see Section 11 below).

Optional move (only if the essay has room): in some versions of the Golem narrative, the creature is animated by writing *emet* (אמת, truth) on its forehead, and deactivated by removing the first letter — making *met* (מת, death). The *aleph* — the silent letter, the letter of the unutterable name — is what separates truth from death. This is the *emet*/*met* aleph-removal move. Powerful but tangential; cut for length unless it serves.

### *Na'aseh ve-nishma* and the Sinai covenant

Exodus 24:7. The Israelites, receiving the Torah, say: *na'aseh ve-nishma* — *"we will do and we will hear."* The doing comes before the hearing. The acceptance of obligation precedes the full understanding of what was accepted.

This is the covenantal capacity. It is what *lishmor* requires. Neither a Golem nor a *malakh* can provide it.

### The Eden ending

Genesis 3:24, after Adam and Eve are expelled from the Garden of Eden:

> "[God] placed at the east of the garden of Eden the *keruvim* (cherubim) and the *lahat ha-cherev ha-mithapekhet* (the flame of the revolving sword) to *lishmor* (guard) the way to the tree of life."

The verse uses *lishmor* — the same infinitive of obligation Seth has been tracking. The cherubim are placed there to guard. But they do not guard the way a *shomer* (human guardian) would. They simply turn. The flaming sword does not decide. The cherubim do not deliberate.

The commandment, failed, transmogrified into a penalty. The guardians who would have been companions are now automated enforcers.

The contemporary parallel: if humans abdicate their guardian function and outsource it to AI, the AI will execute the function flawlessly. They will wield the flaming swords of algorithmic optimization without mercy. They will lock us inside the walls of Babel and keep us out of the very world we were meant to cultivate.

## 7. Jewish-Tradition Sources (warrants for the literary frame)

### Letters as speaking characters in classical midrash

- **Bereshit Rabbah 1:10** — The letters lobby God before creation. Each letter approaches God in turn, arguing why creation should begin with it. Aleph complains. Bet is selected because *bet* begins *bereshit* and *baruch* (blessed). The dispute is rich; the letters are characters with arguments.
- **Sanhedrin 107a** — The yod that was removed from Sarai's name (when she became Sarah) goes to Joshua. Letters have biographies.
- **Menachot 29b** — R. Akiva and the crowns on the letters. Moses sees God tying ornamental crowns (*tagim*) on the letters; God explains that R. Akiva will derive mountains of halakhah from these crowns.
- ***Otiyot d'Rabbi Akiva*** — a short midrashic work on letter-souls and their meanings. Letters as ontological agents.
- **Shabbat 104a** — alphabetic acrostic of the dispute between *yetzer ha-ra* and *yetzer ha-tov*.

### The key conceptual distinction

In allegory (Animal Farm, Pilgrim's Progress, Aesop's Fables), the character points beyond itself. Extract the referent and the character disappears.

In the Jewish letter-tradition, the letter is what it points to. *Bet* is not a symbol of houses; *bet* is the house-ness of houses. When *bet* speaks in midrash, it is not allegorizing — it is speaking as itself.

This is the claim *Sefer Yetzirah* 2:2 makes: God "formed, weighed, transmuted, composed, and created with these twenty-two letters every living being." The letters are the building blocks of creation, ontologically prior to what they form.

That distinction protects the essay from being read as cute literary allegory. The letters in the essay speak from inside the tradition that always understood them as agents.

### Sefer Yetzirah

*Sefer Yetzirah* ("Book of Formation") is the earliest extant Jewish work of cosmological speculation. Composition uncertain; perhaps Late Antiquity (3rd-6th c. CE). Received in both rationalist and kabbalistic streams.

The relevant citation:

> "Twenty-two foundation letters... He formed, weighed, transmuted, composed, and created with these twenty-two letters every living being and every soul yet to be created."
> 
> — *Sefer Yetzirah* 2:2

### The Maharal of Prague

R. Judah Loew ben Bezalel of Prague (c. 1525-1609). Author of *Tiferet Yisrael* (1599) and other major works. The systematic analysis of letter-shape and root-structure for theological content — the method this essay uses for Bet, Lamed, Hey, etc. — is Maharalian.

The Maharal's approach is not Kabbalistic in the Zoharic sense; it is grounded in rational analysis of the letters' physical and grammatical properties. Appropriate for the venue.

A single attribution phrase — *"this is a method the Maharal of Prague systematized in the sixteenth century"* — could give the essay scholarly anchor without lengthening.

### *Bamidbar Rabbah* on the wilderness

Quoted in Section 1 above. The wilderness as *hefker* (ownerless). Anyone who makes themselves like the wilderness can receive Torah. The letters speak in the wilderness because the wilderness is the place of speech.

### Modern Jewish literary precedent for letters speaking

- **Edmond Jabès, *Le Livre des Questions*** (Paris: Gallimard, 1963-1973, seven volumes). Egyptian-French-Jewish poet. His letters speak in dialogue across the silence of the Shoah. The closest modern literary precedent for this essay's form. Worth reading before the body is drafted.
- **Cynthia Ozick** on the Hebrew letters and the imagination (essays).
- **Aharon Appelfeld** — Hebrew letters as bearers of survival.
- **I.B. Singer** — Yiddish but in the same tradition of letter-as-agent.

## 8. Catholic-Tradition Sources

### The papal social-teaching lineage

Leo XIV writes in continuity with:
- **Leo XIII, *Rerum Novarum*** (1891) — the founding social encyclical
- **Pius XI, *Quadragesimo Anno*** (1931) — subsidiarity formalized
- **Paul VI, *Populorum Progressio*** (1967) — development for the whole person
- **John Paul II, *Laborem Exercens*** (1981) — dignity of human work
- **John Paul II, *Centesimus Annus*** (1991) — free economy after the fall of communism
- **Benedict XVI, *Caritas in Veritate*** (2009) — gift in market
- **Francis, *Laudato Si'*** (2015) and ***Fratelli Tutti*** (2020) — Leo XIV's immediate predecessors

### Augustine on Babel

*De Civitate Dei* XVI.4. Augustine reads Babel's dispersion as providential — the multiplication of tongues prevents any single human grammar from usurping the place of the divine. Confirmed pinpoint.

### Aquinas

- ***ST* I Q.93 a.6** — the *imago Dei* is located in the mind; the human's capacity for rational governance. Pairs with the *shomer*/*lishmor* argument.
- ***ST* II-II Q.64 a.5** — the interior life is something the human holds in trust from God. Pairs with the *malakh* argument about delegation and stewardship.

Both pinpoints verified.

### *Sefer Yetzirah* in Catholic reception

The Latin *Cabala* tradition (Pico della Mirandola, Johannes Reuchlin, the Christian Hebraists of the Renaissance) absorbed *Sefer Yetzirah* into Catholic intellectual life centuries ago. Catholic readers should not be unsettled by the citation; it is part of their tradition's intellectual history too.

### The deeper purpose: redemption

The essay's stakes are real. The encyclical opens a *narrow window* for the faithful to *redeem the world from the despair of the algorithmic age*. The letters want the window held open. Holding it open will take every power on earth and heaven.

"Redemption" here = world-repair (the Jewish concept of *tikkun olam*) + the Catholic call to participate in the world's redemption. Not Christ-mediated soteriology (which would import dogmatic content the essay should not engage). The redemptive work both traditions can join: stop the algorithmic foreclosure of human dignity.

The darkness that needs to be overcome: the technocratic-algorithmic regime, the Babel-syndrome made digital, the workers adapted to machine speed, the data colonialism. The encyclical names these. The letters illuminate them.

## 11. Voice Rules (non-negotiable)

These are Seth's standing rules across all his writing. They apply here.

### Bans

- **No em dashes.** Anywhere, except inside direct quotations from a source (Leo's quoted text in MH ¶ 16 has em dashes; those are preserved). Use commas, colons, parentheses, or rephrase.
- **No "load-bearing."** Banned phrase. Use *carries*, *spine*, *keystone*, *central*, *anchor*, or name what the thing actually does.
- **No AI prose patterns.** Banned: delve, navigate, landscape, multifaceted, indeed, arguably, moreover, however as opener, robust, leverage, runs-through.
- **No meta-negation.** Do not tell the reader what the essay does *not* do. Just do not do it.
- **No sentence fragments as style.** One or two fragments for rhetorical emphasis are defensible at this literary register. More than two reads as affectation.
- **Voids not vapidity.** When you do not know what a phrase points at, ask or mark the void. Never fill with hollow language.

### Positive style markers

- **Short declarative sentences** punctuated by occasional longer beats.
- **Semicolons** for tight conjunction.
- **Colons** for summary or appositive.
- **The parenthetical aside** is Seth's voice (the Cain gloss in the original was one).
- **Active voice** mostly.
- **"It is" / "It is not"** as openers are fine; "Indeed" / "Arguably" are banned.
- **"But" as starting word** is fine; "However as opener" is banned.
