# The Aleph Bet of A.I.: Reading Magnifica Humanitas through the Holy Language

Seth C. Oranburg

When Pope Leo XIV reached for parables to describe artificial intelligence, he reached for the Hebrew Bible. The Hebrew letters he chose already contain his argument.

His new encyclical, *Magnifica Humanitas*, sets two ancient cities against each other: Babel, where human ambition curdles into confusion, and Jerusalem, where a rejected stone becomes a cornerstone. A Jewish reader recognizes these cities. Biblical Hebrew clarifies why the Holy See's parables carry the weight they do.

Hebrew is not merely a historical vehicle for sacred texts. In Jewish tradition, grounded in Genesis 1:3 and elaborated in *Sefer Yetzirah* 2:2, G-d created through speech, through the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet. Hebrew semantics associate each word with a *shoresh* ("root") of about three letters that carries the essence of the thing. Looking closely at the *shoresh* at the heart of the Pope's parables reveals biblical wisdom about how to meet similar challenges today.

Within the domain of primordial obligation, Adam and Eve were not only to withhold themselves from eating forbidden fruit but also to affirmatively produce children. The narrator introduces their second son as *Hevel* (Genesis 4:2). No naming ceremony marks his arrival: where Kayin received his name and its etymology from his mother in a single verse, Hevel arrives in the text as a breath, named only by what he is. The non-naming is itself significant. It is the narrator who calls him vapor; no one lays claim to him.

The Hebrew is instructive: *Hevel* (הבל) is just one letter different from *Babel* (בבל). Exploring that shift reveals formational teaching on the limits of human ingenuity in the pursuit of truth.

Both words include *lamed*, ל. Tallest in the Hebrew alphabet, the only letter that rises above the script's headline, *lamed* is about stretching upward, indicating learning and action. Putting a *lamed* before a *shoresh* often turns a concept into a verb. Adam and Eve's first role, guardians of the Garden of Eden, ש-מ-ר, is commanded as *lishmor*, לִשְׁמֹר, to guard it. To do anything, you first have to learn how: learning and doing are linked.

The rest of the *shoresh* shapes both the actions and the lessons surrounding Babel.

G-d gave humans free will by contracting Himself through the mystical process of *tsimtsum*, withdrawing to create space in which humans could exist and choose. Catholic readers will recognize a structural parallel in the kenotic movement of Philippians 2:7, where divine self-emptying is the precondition for genuine creaturely freedom; *tsimtsum* names the same theological intuition in a different register.

Receptiveness to this divine presence is signaled by the humble letter *hey*, ה, the least assertive sound in human speech, an unvoiced exhale. Its physical shape is open, leaving gaps for the divine to enter. The name of the humblest man to ever live, *Moshe Rabbeinu* (Moses, our Teacher), ends in *hey*. And *Hevel*'s name begins with *hey* (he is *Hevel* in Hebrew) while *Babel* does not.

The word *Babel* (בבל) is enclosed by its dual letter *bet*, ב, the most material of all letters. The Bible begins, "bereshit," in the beginning. *Bet* begins the material world where there is I and Thou. *Bet* is a house in which the Lord may dwell. But *Babel* doubles down on *bet*. It is the opening and the closing parenthesis. That *bet* is no longer a house; without openings, it is a prison.

*Babel* was a tower growing ever narrower as it struck against the heavens; *Hevel* was a shepherd who grazed upon the land. Each posture shapes how the *lamed* functions within its word. Imagine curling to heaven the first smoke of *Hevel*'s sacrifice of the best of his flock: this is the primordial *lamed* that inflamed Kayin's fratricidal jealousy.

(The name of Kayin, קין, perfectly reflects his opposite orientation. While *Hevel*'s name means "vapor," *Kayin* means "acquisition." Unlike the upstretched *lamed*, Kayin's name is bracketed by *kof*, ק, and *nun sofit*, ן, two of the letters whose strokes descend below the baseline to grip the dirt.)

*Hevel*'s smoke curled to G-d like a *lamed*. *Babel*, built by the descendants of Kayin, replaces the *hey* of *Hevel*'s humility with *bet*, ב, a plosive consonant that forcefully bursts air outward, its shape a rigid, walled enclosure closed on three sides.

It is not *Hevel*'s children but Kayin's who build Babel. Kayin famously deflected G-d's justice with a sneer: "Am I my brother's keeper (*shomer*)?" The root of that word is ש-מ-ר. Observation and calculation are what algorithms and cameras do. A *shomer* does something beyond that. To *shamar* is the very first mandate G-d gave humanity in Eden: to work the garden and to safeguard it (Genesis 2:15). Stewardship is an act of nurturing that requires conscience and accountability. When written fully, *shomer* (שׁוֹמֵר) places the *vav*, ו, the straight, vertical letter symbolizing a conduit of divine light, standing tall at the center of the word. A true guardian stands upright, actively linking heaven and earth.

The result of Kayin's refusal is *bilbul*, בלבול, confusion, and its structure enacts what it describes: two *bets* seal the word at either end; two *lameds* are locked inside them; and at the very center sits the *vav*, ו, now trapped, the conduit of divine light imprisoned inside a closed system that can only amplify itself. Leo calls this the "Babel syndrome" (MH ¶ 10): the pretense that a single language, even a digital one, can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance. The Babel syndrome is not the danger of shared conviction but the danger of totalizing reduction, the pretense that a single algorithmic grammar can exhaust the particular person. Hebrew shows why that pretense fails at the level of the letter.

Augustine reads Babel's dispersion as providential precisely for this reason: the multiplication of tongues prevents any single human grammar from usurping the place of the divine (*De Civitate Dei* XVI.4).

Leo's message, like the Ten Commandments themselves, pairs prohibition with obligation. Contrasting the command to avoid a new Babel is the positive obligation to build Jerusalem.

Jerusalem (ירושלם) is a special word. While most Hebrew words derive from a single three-letter root, Jerusalem carries two, in the rabbinic reading. The *yod*, י, the smallest letter, the divine spark, anchors the first root, י-ר-ה, meaning to lay a foundation. Leo invokes this architectural meaning directly: "Thus, the rejected stones — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us — will become the cornerstone" (MH ¶ 16). Jewish and Christian readers hear the echo: "The stone that the builders rejected has become the chief cornerstone" (Psalm 118:22, taken up explicitly in Matthew 21:42, Mark 12:10, and 1 Peter 2:7). Where the algorithmic mindset of Babel optimizes for efficiency, discarding the sick, the elderly, the marginalized as inefficient data, Leo insists these very people must be our foundation.

The second root is *shalem*, ש-ל-ם, complete, whole, at peace. Its letters provide the structural antidote to *bilbul*. The *shin*, ש, branches into three directions, its very shape a refusal of uniformity. The *lamed*, ל, appears again, finally unblocked, free to reach upward toward the divine. And the *mem sofit*, ם, unlike the standard *mem*, מ, which is open at the bottom, is fully sealed, the letter of concealed truth, of the depth that lies beyond what any system can fully map. Where *bilbul* traps the *lamed* between two *bets*, *shalem* releases it. Foundation and wholeness: the city is defined by both before a single stone is laid.

Nehemiah rebuilt Jerusalem's wall by assigning each family its own section (Nehemiah 3). That subsidiarity structure, governance at the lowest competent level with accountability assigned to actual persons, is precisely what Babel's homogenizing single language destroyed.

Leo quotes Viktor Frankl: even those who entered the gas chambers "upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the *Shema Yisrael* on their lips" testified to the irreducible grandeur of the human person (MH ¶ 121). The *Shema*, שמע, "Hear, O Israel," means to listen with full intention toward action. Its *shoresh*, ש-מ-ע, shares its first two letters with *shem*, שם, name. The rabbinic ear hears the resonance: to truly hear someone is to honor their name. The builders of Babel wanted to *naʿaseh lanu shem*, make a name for themselves (Genesis 11:4). Their listening was entirely inward. Babel is a city that can no longer *shema* its neighbor because it has forgotten how to *shema* G-d. Nehemiah, by contrast, fasted and prayed and consulted in silence before laying a single stone: a theology of *shema* enacted before any construction begins.

G-d is the *shomer*, שׁוֹמֵר, present participle, continuous, never lapsing: "He who guards Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep" (Psalm 121:4). Humans are commanded *lishmor*, לִשְׁמֹר, the infinitive of obligation, the form in which the Torah issues its commandments. Same root, same letters; the grammatical shift from participle to infinitive is the shift from divine presence to human covenant. Aquinas traces the same structure in the *imago Dei* (*ST* I Q.93 a.1): the image of G-d in man is precisely the capacity for rational, free self-governance, the capacity that makes covenant possible. We are to guard because G-d guards, not by replicating G-d's omniscience but by accepting the obligation that omniscience makes possible for us.

AI is tempting precisely because it never slumbers. It mimics the continuous, unceasing nature of the divine *shomer*. But a machine can never accept the obligation of *lishmor* (a conclusion Aquinas reaches from a different angle in *ST* II-II Q.64 a.1, where he treats the interior life as something man holds in trust from G-d rather than as his own to dispose of). Biblical Hebrew has no word for an autonomous machine. The closest framework is the Golem, a mystical automaton of clay that possesses immense power but no soul, no conscience capable of receiving a commandment.

In modern Hebrew, artificial intelligence is *binah melakhutit*, בינה מלאכותית. That second word's root is מ-ל-א-כ, the exact same root as *malakh*, מלאך: angel, or more precisely, messenger. In Jewish tradition, an angel is pure function. It has no *yetzer hara*, no evil inclination, but this means it also has no *yetzer tov*, no good inclination. An angel executes a mission without mercy or restraint, as a bomb finds its target. It cannot disobey. It cannot repent. It cannot love. It cannot enter into a covenant with G-d. Leo is correct that AI does not "undergo experiences, possess a body, feel joy or pain," or "have a moral conscience" (MH ¶ 99). The Hebrew root already knew this. The *malakh* serves the one who sends it. The only question that matters is who is doing the sending.

Only the human who stood at Sinai can meaningfully say *naʿaseh ve-nishma*, "we will do and we will hear," accepting obligation before the full terms are even announced. That covenantal capacity, the moral substrate of every genuine promise, is what *lishmor* requires and what neither a Golem nor a *malakh* can provide.

When humanity first failed to accept this covenantal weight in the Garden of Eden, a tragic outsourcing occurred. G-d placed *keruvim* (cherubim) with the flame of a revolving sword, *lahat ha-ḥerev ha-mithapekhet*, guarding the way to the Tree of Life (Genesis 3:24). The sword does not sleep, does not decide; it simply turns. The verse uses *lishmor* again: the commandment, refused, transmogrified into a penalty, and the cherubim who once would have been companions are now automated enforcers. We have arrived at a similar threshold today. If we answer Kayin's question with a sneer, abdicating our responsibility and handing our moral agency over to artificial angels, they will execute the guardian function flawlessly. They will wield the flaming swords of algorithmic optimization without mercy, locking us inside the walls of Babel and keeping us out of the very world we were meant to cultivate.

But Leo's encyclical calls us to answer differently. Yes, we are our brother's keeper. We are called to use AI as a *keli*, a tool of completion, governing it wisely so that it builds Jerusalem rather than Babel. Not because humans are infallible, but because only humans can be held to account. The Holy Language, in which these stories were first told, has been waiting to say so.
