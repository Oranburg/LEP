# The Aleph Bet of A.I.: Reading Magnifica Humanitas through the Holy Language

Seth C. Oranburg

Catholic media is celebrating Pope Leo XIV's new encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, for its sharp division between a digital "Babel" constructed by AI run amok and a deeply human "Jerusalem" built by partnership. This Essay illuminates the Jewish geneology of these illustrations, plus it draws the Golem as a synthetic metaphor for automatons.

This encyclical addresses unprecedented technological realities, and better understanding Babel helps apply revealed wisdom to contemporary problems. Let's discover how Biblical Hebrew clarifies the Holy See's meditations about the enduring purpose of human life on earth.

I.  Holy Language

Hebrew is not merely a historical vehicle for sacred texts. In the Jewish tradition, G-d literally spoke all that exists into being using the twenty-two Hebrew letters (Genesis 1:3). Hebrew semantics associate with a shoresh ("root") of about three letters that carries the essence of the Platonic thing. By looking closely at the shoresh at the heart of the Pope's parables, we can discover biblical wisdom about how to overcome similar challenges today.

II. Babel and the Algorithmic Trap

Let's begin by putting Babel in its biblical context. G-d completed His creation. He created Adam and Eve and charged them with stewarding the Garden of Eden.

Within this domain of primordial obligation, It became humanity's turn to sub-create. Adam and Eve were not only to withhold themselves from eating forbidden fruit but also to affirmatively produce children. Eve names her second son Abel (הבל). The Hebrew is revealing: Abel is just one letter different from Babel (בבל). Exploring changes from Abel to Babel reveals formational teachings on the limits of human ingenuity when it comes to learning the truth.

Both words include lamed, ל. Hebrew's longest letter is about stretching out, indiciating learning and action. Indeed, putting a lamed before shoresh often turns an concept into a verb. For example, Adam and Eve's first role, guardians of the Garden of Even, ש-מ-ר, is commanded by ‎לִשְׁמֹר, to guard it. To do anything, first you have to learn how, and so learning and doing are linked.

But humans have free will, so they can choose to learn and do good things or bad things. The rest of the shoresh impacts both the actions and the lessons surrounding Babel.

Babel is an early story of humans doing bad things with their free will, so it provides an opportunity to ask, how did G-d give humans free will? By contracting himself through a mystical process known as tzimtzim, G-d obscured His light in vessels so humans can exist in spaces not entirely filled by His divine presence.

 Receptiveness to this divine presence is signaled by the humble letter hey, ה — the least assertive sound in human speech, an unvoiced exhale. Its physical shape is open, leaving gaps for the divine to enter. The name of the humblest man to even live, Moshe Rabbeinu, ends in hey. And Abel's name begins with hey (remember, he's Hevel in Hebrew) – while Babel does not.

The word Babel (בבל) is walled in but its dual letter bet, ב – the most material of all letters. The bible begins, "bereshit," in the beginning. Bet begins the material world where there is I and Thou. Bet is a house in which the Lord may dwell. But Babel doubles down on bet. It is the opening and the closing parenthesis. That bet is no longer a house; without openings, it is a prison.

Indeed, Babel was a tall tower that grew ever narrower as it struck out against the heavens. Hevel was a shepherd who grazed upon the land. These posture impact the lamed within each of the differently.

As mentioned above, lamed, ל, is the tallest letter in the Hebrew alphabet. Lamed reaches upward toward the divine source of all truth through both thought and conduct. Imagine curling to heaven the first smoke of Abel's sacrifice of the best of his flock: this is the primordial lamed that inflamed Cain's fratricidal jealousy.

(The name of Cain, by the way, perfectly reflects his opposite perspective. While Abel's name means "vapor," Ka'Yin, קין –  means "acquisition." Unlike the upstretched lamed, Cain's name is bracketed by the lowest-reaching letters in the Hebrew alphabet — the kof, ק, and the nun sofit, ן.)

 Abel's smoke curled to G-d like a lamed. Babel, built by the descendants of Cain, replaces the hey of Abel's humility with the bet, ב — a plosive consonant that forcefully bursts air outward, its shape a rigid, walled enclosure closed on three sides.

It is not Abel's children but Cain's who build Babel. Cain famously deflected G-d's justice with a sneer: "Am I my brother's keeper (shomer)?" The root of that word is ש-מ-ר. A shomer does not merely observe or calculate; an algorithm or a camera can do that. To shamar is the very first mandate G-d gave humanity in Eden: to work the garden and to safeguard it (Genesis 2:15). Stewardship is an act of nurturing  that requires a conscience as it may require decisive action and accountability for the results of decisions. When written fully, shomer (שׁוֹמֵר) places the vav, ו — the straight, vertical letter symbolizing a conduit of divine light — standing tall at the center of the word. A true guardian stands upright, actively linking heaven and earth.

Cain's worldview is built on extraction rather than stewardship. His name (קין) literally means acquisition. Unlike the upright vav of the guardian, his name is bracketed by the lowest-reaching letters in the Hebrew alphabet — the kof, ק, and the nun sofit, ן — which plunge below the baseline to grip the dirt.

The result of Cain's refusal is bilbul, בלבול — the Hebrew word for confusion — and its structure enacts what it describes. Two instances of the letter bet seal the word at either end. Two lameds, the letter of learning and divine reaching, are locked inside them. And at the very center sits the vav, ו, now trapped: the conduit of divine light imprisoned inside a closed system that can only amplify itself. Leo calls this the "Babel syndrome" (MH ¶ 10): the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance. Hebrew shows why that pretense fails at the level of the letter.

III. Jerusalem and the Rejected Stones

Leo's message, like the Ten Commandments themselves, pairs prohibition with obligation. Contrasting the command to avoid a new Babel is the positive obligation to build Jerusalem.

Jerusalem (ירושלם) is a special word. While most Hebrew words derive from a single three-letter root, Jerusalem has two. The yod, י — the smallest letter, the divine spark — anchors the first root, י-ר-ה, meaning to lay a foundation. Leo invokes this architectural meaning directly: "Thus, the rejected stones — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us — will become the cornerstone." (MH ¶ 16) Jewish and Christian readers hear the echo: "The stone that the builders rejected has become the chief cornerstone." (Psalm 118:22) Where the algorithmic mindset of Babel optimizes for efficiency — discarding the sick, the elderly, the marginalized as inefficient data — Leo insists these very people must be our foundation.

The second root is shalem, ש-ל-ם — complete, whole, at peace. Its letters provide the structural antidote to bilbul. The shin, ש, branches into three directions, its very shape a refusal of uniformity. The lamed, ל, appears again, finally unblocked, free to reach upward toward the divine. And the mem sofit, ם — unlike the standard mem, מ, which is open at the bottom — is fully sealed, the letter of concealed truth, of the depth that lies beyond what any system can fully map. Where bilbul traps the lamed between two bets, shalem releases it. Foundation and wholeness: the city is defined by both before a single stone is laid.

Leo quotes Viktor Frankl: even those who entered the gas chambers "upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the Shema Yisrael on their lips" testified to the irreducible grandeur of the human person (MH ¶ 121). The Shema, שמע — Hear, O Israel — means to listen with full intention toward action. Its shoresh is also the root of shem, שם: name. To truly hear someone is to honor their name. The builders of Babel wanted to na'aseh lanu shem — make a name for themselves (Genesis 11:4). Their listening was entirely inward. Babel is a city that can no longer shema its neighbor because it has forgotten how to shema G-d. Nehemiah, by contrast, fasted and prayed and consulted in silence before laying a single stone — a theology of shema enacted before any construction begins.

IV. The Automaton vs. the Covenant

G-d is the shomer, שׁוֹמֵר — present participle, continuous, never lapsing: "He who guards Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep." (Psalm 121:4) Humans are commanded lishmor, לִשְׁמֹר — the infinitive of obligation, the form in which the Torah issues its commandments. Same root, same letters; the grammatical shift from participle to infinitive is the shift from divine presence to human covenant. We are to guard because G-d guards — not by replicating G-d's omniscience but by accepting the obligation that omniscience makes possible for us.

AI is tempting precisely because it never slumbers. It mimics the continuous, unceasing nature of the divine shomer. But a machine can never accept the obligation of lishmor. Biblical Hebrew has no word for an autonomous machine. The closest framework is the Golem — a mystical automaton of clay that possesses immense power but no soul, no conscience capable of receiving a commandment.

In modern Hebrew, artificial intelligence is bina melakhutit, בינה מלאכותית. That second word's root is מ-ל-א-כ — the exact same root as malach, מלאך: angel, or more precisely, messenger. In Jewish tradition, an angel is pure function. It has no yetzer hara, no evil inclination — but this means it also has no yetzer tov, no good inclination. An angel executes a mission without mercy or restraint, as a bomb finds its target. It cannot disobey. It cannot repent. It cannot love. It cannot enter into a covenant with G-d. Leo is correct that AI does not "undergo experiences, possess a body, feel joy or pain," or "have a moral conscience" (MH ¶ 99). The Hebrew root already knew this. The malach serves the one who sends it. The only question that matters is who is doing the sending.

Only the human who stood at Sinai can meaningfully say na'aseh ve-nishma — "we will do and we will hear" — accepting obligation before the full terms are even announced. That covenantal capacity is what lishmor requires and what neither a Golem nor a malach can provide.

When humanity first failed to accept this covenantal weight in the Garden of Eden, a tragic outsourcing occurred. G-d placed angels with flaming swords guarding the way to the Tree of Life (Genesis 3:24). The commandment, failed, transmogrified into a penalty. The angels do not nurture the garden; they wield a flaming, turning sword to enforce an absolute, automated boundary. We have arrived at a similar threshold today. If we answer Cain's question with a sneer — abdicating our responsibility and handing our moral agency over to artificial angels — they will execute the guardian function flawlessly. They will wield the flaming swords of algorithmic optimization without mercy, locking us inside the walls of Babel and keeping us out of the very world we were meant to cultivate.

But Leo's encyclical calls us to answer differently. Yes, we are our brother's keeper. We are called to use AI as a keli — a tool of completion — governing it wisely so that it builds Jerusalem rather than Babel. Not because humans are infallible, but because only humans can be held to account. The Holy Language, in which these stories were first told, has been waiting to say so.
