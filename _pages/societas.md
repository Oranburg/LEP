# Societas

*Societas* is the Latin word for partnership. Aquinas develops it in *Summa Theologiae* II-II q. 78, especially article 2 ad 5, as the structure of capital deployment that escapes the prohibition on usury. Capital deployed into a partnership at shared risk earns a return justly, because the investor remains a co-owner who shares in the loss. The same capital extended as a loan at fixed interest (*mutuum*) is usury, because the lender keeps the capital safe and demands a return as well.

The distinction is the conceptual ancestor of every modern partnership form: the general partnership, the limited partnership, the limited liability company, the joint venture, the syndicated venture-capital fund, the equity tranche of a private-equity acquisition. All of these structures share the move Aquinas makes: the capital provider participates in the upside of the venture because the capital provider also bears the downside.

Maimonides reaches the same conclusion from the Jewish tradition through independent reasoning. In *Mishneh Torah*, Hilkhot Mattenot Aniyim 10:7, *shutafut* with the poor is named as the highest of the eight rungs of *tzedakah*. The Talmudic *isqa* device (developed in Bava Metzia) operationalizes the same principle in commercial law: capital is deployed half as loan and half as deposit-at-risk, so that return follows shared risk rather than fixed interest. The medieval *heter isqa* contract carries the device into the modern Jewish-law system.

The Salamanca school developed the Aquinas distinction into a full commercial moral theology in the sixteenth century. See the [doctrinal lineage]({{ '/lineage/' | relative_url }}) page for the figures and the path through Leo XIII, John Paul II, and Leo XIV.

## How the doctrine reads actual transactional documents

The frame is not theology applied to law. The frame is the conceptual structure of the legal forms themselves, drawn from the tradition that produced them. A lawyer trained in this tradition reads documents differently.

Take a Series Seed term sheet. Six provisions in that document carry the partnership-versus-loan question.

**The liquidation preference.** A 1x non-participating preferred share preserves the investor as a co-owner who shares in the loss if the venture underperforms. A 2x participating preferred share converts the investor into a senior creditor with a guaranteed return ahead of the founders. The first honors the *societas* structure. The second slides toward *mutuum*.

**The drag-along provision.** A drag-along that protects minority founders against a forced sale below their preference preserves the partnership. One that compels them to sell on majority terms collapses the partnership into a creditor relationship.

**The vesting schedule.** A four-year vest with a one-year cliff treats the founder's labor as the partnership contribution Aquinas describes. An immediate-vesting schedule with a generous severance treats the founder as a salaried employee with a payout option.

**The participation rights.** Pro-rata rights that let early investors continue to participate at later rounds preserve the partnership. Anti-dilution provisions that ratchet the preferred shares' conversion price down at the expense of the founders shift the structure toward a creditor relationship.

**The protective provisions.** Investor consent rights over fundamental venture decisions (board composition, additional financings, sale of the company) are consistent with the partnership structure when they protect against expropriation. The same rights, scaled too aggressively, give the investor effective control over the partnership in a way that is inconsistent with shared deployment at shared risk.

**The conversion mechanics.** Automatic conversion at qualified financing preserves the partnership through a venture's growth stages. Investor-friendly anti-conversion thresholds that lock in preference rights preserve the creditor posture.

A lawyer trained in the *societas* vocabulary reads these clauses with the partnership-versus-loan question in hand. The point is not to argue Aquinas with the client. The point is to see the structural reality of what the document does, and to advise accordingly.

## Why this matters for the practice

The lawyer who reads deal documents through this frame is a different lawyer than the one who treats every provision as a market default to be negotiated for whichever side hired her. The first lawyer can tell a founder which terms preserve the venture as a partnership and which terms make it something else. The second lawyer cannot, because she does not have the conceptual structure that lets her name the difference.

The program teaches the first kind of lawyer.
