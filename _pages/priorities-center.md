# Priority: Toward a Catholic Business Law Center?

The question mark is the priority. The program's directors do not yet present a Catholic Business Law Center as a settled program decision. They present the five-year horizon and the Board conversation that decides whether the program grows into one.

## The question

CUA Columbus Law currently operates an entrepreneurship clinic, a course set centered on business associations and transactional drafting, and the Law and Entrepreneurship Program as the umbrella institutional identity. The strategic question over the next three to five years is whether to consolidate at the entrepreneurship core or to grow toward a fuller business-law center.

The argument for growth is institutional, not aesthetic. Topics beyond entrepreneurship (investment, nonprofit governance, corporate compliance, regulated-industry transactions, Catholic Social Thought as applied to the corporation) merit a bigger name than a single-topic program carries. A center is an ambition that could become a five-year plan.

## The trail-blazer

The fellowship model already running at CUA Columbus Law, in particular through Joel Alicea's Project on Constitutional Originalism, is the structural template. Fellowships support students through dedicated stipends and support future faculty through pre-academic appointments. The pattern is portable to business-law subject matter, and the program intends to apply it.

## The five-year horizon

| Phase | Period | What happens |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core | AY 2026-27 | The five priorities at full operating tempo. The Board's strategic input is the resource. |
| Expansion | AY 2027-28 | Mentorship cohort grows; adjunct slate expands; a transactional writing module enters the curriculum; the Leo Fellowship in Entrepreneurial Lawyering opens to visiting scholars. |
| Center shape | AY 2028-29 | The program's institutional shape converges on a center. Naming question opens; a second standing convening enters the calendar. |
| Recognized peer | AY 2029-30 to AY 2030-31 | The center is recognized as a peer to the named business-law centers it set out to join. Recognition is measured in peer assessment, in transactional employer presence at OCI, in faculty movement at the senior level, and in citation traffic to the program's publication outputs. |

The trajectory does not require the named-center gift to land for the center to be recognized as such. It does require the named-center gift to land for the center to operate at endowed steady state without continued operating-budget asks.

## The faculty pipeline the trajectory requires

The growth path requires three classes of faculty additions on distinct tracks: three to six adjuncts drawn from the Washington transactional bar; a second and eventually third clinical faculty position; and one or two Leo Fellows per year, structured to identify candidates for longer-term faculty roles.

The Board of Visitors' networks are central to identifying adjunct candidates. The Dean's faculty hiring processes are central to the clinical and tenure-track lines. The Catholic Advancement office at CUA, with Peggy Kacergis as the institutional partner, is central to the endowment sequence.

## What the program asks of the Board

The Board is asked to:

1. React to the five-year horizon and to the trajectory's pace. Is the timeline right, or is it too compressed?
2. Advise on the naming question. Does the institutional rename happen earlier and the gift follow, or does it follow the gift?
3. Identify adjunct, fellowship, and clinical candidates from Board members' own networks.
4. Hear the trajectory as the longer-term institutional move that the other four priorities bridge toward, not as a separate fundraising ask.

The endowment conversation runs through CUA Advancement on a separate track. The Board's strategic input here feeds the directors' planning conversations with the Dean and with Peggy Kacergis, not a request for Board-led fundraising.
