# Partnership at Shared Risk

{% include figure.html name="callout-societas-shutafut" alt="Two converging nodes labeled societas (Catholic) and shutafut (Jewish), both flowing into a single convergence node labeled Partnership at shared risk." caption="The convergent doctrine the rule rests on." %}

*Societas* requires that the capital provider share the venture's risk. *Mutuum* extracts a fixed return regardless of outcome. Every commercial instrument resolves into one or the other once the label is stripped away. Modern drafting hides which one a deal is doing behind language that floats free of the structure underneath. A preferred share can be a partnership stake or a disguised debenture. A token can be an equity-like claim on a shared venture or a fixed-yield instrument dressed in governance theater. A loan can be a pure debt or, by its participation and contingency terms, a partnership in commercial substance. The lawyer's work is to read past the label to the structure, and to draft so that the structure matches what the parties say they are doing.

The convergent doctrine on which the rule rests is set out at [the lineage page]({{ '/lineage/' | relative_url }}). Two traditions, Catholic and Jewish, reasoning independently across two millennia, arrive at the same conclusion: partnership at shared risk is the just form of capital deployment.

## I. The clause-level test

Three questions classify any term in any instrument. The questions are blunt by design. They force the drafter past the label and onto the structure.

**First. Does the holder's return rise and fall with the venture's outcome?** A preferred share whose dividend accrues at a fixed rate, compounds, and must be paid before the common holder sees a dollar is functioning as a debt instrument no matter what the cap table calls it. An option grant whose strike is set so far out of the money that it pays only on a moonshot is functioning as a lottery ticket, not a partnership stake. The clause earns its *societas* characterization when the holder's payoff genuinely tracks venture outcomes across the realistic distribution of results, not only at the tails.

**Second. Does the holder bear the downside, not only the upside?** This is the question the legal label most often obscures. Liquidation preferences, redemption rights, anti-dilution ratchets, and put options can transfer the venture's downside to other holders while preserving the upside for the senior tranche. When the structure works that way, the senior tranche has converted itself from *societas* into *mutuum* by contract, even where the security is nominally equity. The test is operational: in the realistic loss scenarios, does the holder actually lose money proportional to its stake, or does the structure route the loss to someone else.

**Third. Does the holder have governance rights commensurate with the risk?** A partner who bears the venture's downside has a moral and doctrinal claim to a voice in the venture's decisions. A creditor whose return is fixed has no such claim and traditionally accepts covenants in place of votes. When governance rights and risk exposure are mismatched, the structure is unstable and the parties have drafted past each other. Common stock with no protective provisions plus preferred stock with full board control and veto rights describes a structure in which the risk-bearers (common, including the founders and the employees) have no voice and the parties holding the contractual downside protection (preferred) hold all the votes. That is the inverse of *societas*. It is the form Aquinas named usurious dressed in modern corporate clothing.

A clause passes the test when it answers yes to all three. A clause that answers no on any prong is a *mutuum*-leaning provision, and the drafter should know it and price it accordingly. Mixed answers describe most real terms. The work is to know which way each clause tilts and what the aggregate looks like.

## II. The Series Seed read clause by clause

Six provisions carry most of the *societas* / *mutuum* freight in a standard venture financing. Each is read here against the three-question test.

**Liquidation preference.** A 1x non-participating preferred is the cleanest *societas* form modern venture practice has produced. The investor takes back its principal in a downside and converts to common in an upside, sharing proportionally either way. A 1x participating preferred shifts toward *mutuum*: the investor takes back principal first and then participates in the residual, capturing both the debt-style downside protection and the equity-style upside. A 2x or 3x participating preferred completes the slide. The investor's return is increasingly insulated from the venture's outcome, and the common holders (founders and employees, the labor side of the deal) bear the venture's risk on terms the preferred holders have contractually opted out of. The shape is debt-with-equity-kicker, priced and called equity. The test names what the label conceals.

**Drag-along.** A drag-along that lets the preferred majority force a sale at a price that satisfies preferences but leaves nothing for common is a *mutuum* clause regardless of where it sits in the document. It transfers the downside of an underperforming venture onto the common holders by extinguishing their position at a price the preferred chose. A drag-along conditioned on a minimum price-per-common-share, or on a majority of common consent, restores the *societas* character. Drafters who treat the drag-along as boilerplate have not read the clause.

**Vesting.** Founder vesting is the *societas* mechanism that binds labor to the venture's risk. The founder who walks before vesting loses the unvested stake, exposing labor to the same downside the capital side accepted. A standard four-year vest with one-year cliff is the working form. Acceleration on change of control, double-trigger or single-trigger, calibrates how much downside the founder retains when control shifts. Full single-trigger acceleration is a *mutuum*-leaning provision: it converts the founder's equity into a fixed claim payable on a triggering event regardless of subsequent performance.

**Pro rata and participation rights.** These give the investor the option to maintain its proportional stake through subsequent rounds. Standing alone, they are neutral. Coupled with a pay-to-play provision (the investor must participate in subsequent rounds or lose preferred status), they reinforce *societas*: the holder must continue to share the venture's capital risk to keep the senior position. Pay-to-play is one of the few provisions that operates as a structural *societas* enforcement mechanism inside the standard term sheet.

**Protective provisions.** Veto rights over specified actions (issuance of senior securities, sale of the company, changes to the charter, incurrence of debt above a threshold) are governance rights. The question the test asks is whether they are commensurate with the holder's risk exposure. A 20% preferred holder with veto over a Series B issuance is exercising governance proportional to its stake. A 5% preferred holder with veto over all future financings is exercising governance well past its risk and is functioning as a creditor with covenants disguised as a partner with votes.

**Conversion mechanics.** Anti-dilution protection (broad-based weighted average, narrow-based weighted average, full ratchet) calibrates how much of the down-round risk the preferred holder bears. Broad-based weighted average is the standard *societas* form: the preferred takes a proportional hit alongside the common in a down round. Full ratchet is *mutuum*: the preferred is held harmless and the common absorbs the entire dilution. Most term sheets choose somewhere between. The drafter who understands which way each calibration tilts is doing the work the test requires.

## III. Employee equity and the priority of labor

*Laborem Exercens* states the rule the encyclical tradition had been building toward for ninety years: labor has priority over capital because labor is the human person at work.[^fn-laborem-exercens] The employee stock ownership plan is the institutional form that takes the rule seriously enough to assign ownership, not only wages, to the worker.

The three-question test applied to an ESOP runs as follows. The employee's return rises and falls with the venture's outcome (yes, when the plan holds real equity rather than synthetic units). The employee bears the downside in the form of equity that may go to zero and labor that has been priced below market in exchange for the stake (yes, when vesting tracks tenure and the plan is funded with real shares). The employee has governance rights commensurate with the risk (rarely; this is where most ESOPs slide back toward *mutuum*). The drafting question is whether the plan's governance terms match the risk the worker has accepted.

Where ESOPs slide. A plan that grants phantom units settling in cash at a formula price is *mutuum* in equity clothing: the worker has a contractual claim on a payment, not a partnership in the venture. A plan that issues real shares but strips voting rights through a trust whose trustees are appointed by the board is *mutuum* with respect to governance even where it is *societas* with respect to economics. A plan whose vesting is back-loaded to the point that ordinary turnover forfeits the bulk of the grants is *mutuum* by attrition: the venture has used the equity label to discount labor without delivering the partnership.

The cleanest *societas* employee structures pair real shares, ordinary-course vesting, and either direct voting or a trustee-mediated arrangement that runs to the employees' interest rather than the company's. They are rarer than the deferred-comp-dressed-as-equity structure that dominates the market, and they take more drafting work. The work is the doctrine.

## IV. DAO governance tokens

The decentralized autonomous organization asks the *societas* / *mutuum* question in code. A governance token issued proportional to capital contribution, conferring votes on the venture's decisions, and exposing the holder to the venture's downside through the token's loss of value if the protocol fails, replicates *societas* in cryptographic form. A token sale promising a fixed yield, a buy-back at a guaranteed price, or a payment stream insulated from the venture's commercial outcome is *mutuum* in new clothing. The legal label affixed to the token is doctrinally irrelevant. The structure decides the question.

The three-question test applied to a DAO. Return tracks outcome (yes, when the token's price and the protocol's treasury share genuinely move with adoption and revenue). Holder bears the downside (yes, when there is no put, no redemption right, no protocol-guaranteed buyback, and the treasury is exposed to operational risk). Governance commensurate with risk (yes, when voting power is one-token-one-vote or weighted in a way that maps to economic exposure; no, when the token confers symbolic voting on a protocol whose substantive decisions are made by an off-chain foundation or core developer team).

Most token offerings that the US Securities and Exchange Commission has treated as securities are *mutuum* in the older vocabulary: the buyer's return depended on the efforts of others, and the buyer bore no genuine partnership in the venture. Most token offerings that have survived regulatory scrutiny are *societas*-leaning: the buyer is a participant in a network whose governance and economics are genuinely shared. The regulatory conversation that the present industry conducts in *Howey*-test vocabulary is the conversation the tradition has been having for seven hundred years in *societas* / *mutuum* vocabulary. Both vocabularies are asking whether the holder is a partner in a venture or a creditor in disguise.

The drafting consequence. A token structure that intends *societas* should ensure the three prongs are satisfied on the face of the code and the surrounding documents: economic exposure to the protocol's outcome, no synthetic downside protection, governance rights with real subject matter. A token structure that intends *mutuum* (a yield-bearing instrument with no partnership claim) should say so plainly and accept the regulatory consequences. The structures that combine *mutuum* economics with *societas* marketing are the ones the regulatory enforcement actions reach, and the doctrine names why.

## V. AI-platform equity and the Babel question

Leo XIV's *Magnifica Humanitas* takes Babel and Nehemia as the governing images for the AI age.[^fn-magnifica-humanitas] Babel is the platform whose capital is gathered, whose labor is given, whose data is harvested, and whose returns flow upward to a tower the contributors do not own. Nehemia is the structure in which each family rebuilds the wall adjacent to its own house. The Nehemian arrangement is partnership at shared risk in built form.

The AI platform is the present-generation Babel question. The compute is paid for by capital. The labor is the human work of training, fine-tuning, and reinforcement-learning-from-human-feedback. The data is the corpus of human production the model is trained on, contributed by parties who in most cases received nothing for it and in many cases did not know they were contributing. The returns flow to the equity holders of the platform company. The three-question test applied to the contributor (the labeler, the data subject, the small developer building on the API) returns three no's. Return does not track outcome. Downside is not borne in any proportion. Governance is nil.

The Nehemian platform is the same structure assembled differently. Each contributor holds an equity-adjacent share of the value its contribution creates: revenue-share for labeled data, royalty streams for copyrighted training material, governance tokens for protocol contributors, profit-participation for the trainers and reinforcement-learning workforce. The structure is technically buildable. The question is whether the present generation of platform drafters will build it. The doctrine names the form. The lawyer drafts the form or drafts past it. There is no neutral document. See [the lineage page §VIII]({{ '/lineage/#viii-the-anatomy-applied-reading-modern-instruments' | relative_url }}) for the convergent framing, and the forthcoming Heter Iska essay at [/LEP/essays/heter-iska-operative/]({{ '/essays/heter-iska-operative/' | relative_url }}) for the Jewish commercial form built around the same principle.

## VI. The rule's purpose

Partnership at shared risk is not only morally appropriate. It is the form in which justice toward the worker, the founder, the data contributor, and the small investor takes shape in commercial life. The three-question test is a working tool, not a piety. It is the tool by which a transactional lawyer turns the convergent doctrine into the terms of a deal.

A drafter who knows the test can do three things the unaided drafter cannot. The drafter can read an opposing term sheet and name precisely which clauses tilt toward *mutuum* and which sustain *societas*, which makes negotiation a conversation about structure rather than a contest of market-norm assertions. The drafter can audit a client's own form documents and find the places where the firm has been issuing *mutuum* under an equity label without intending to. The drafter can build new instruments (the next generation of AI-equity participation rights, contributor-token structures, employee-ownership vehicles) on a doctrinal foundation that two millennia of commercial law worked out and that the present generation has mostly forgotten.

The lawyer who reads the deal through the test recovers the judgment that the checklist has been displacing. See [the Deskilling Loop essay]({{ '/essays/deskilling-loop/' | relative_url }}) for the long account of what the drafter loses when judgment migrates to the form, and the forthcoming Jurisgenesis essay at [/LEP/essays/jurisgenesis-drafting/]({{ '/essays/jurisgenesis-drafting/' | relative_url }}) for the theory of practice this rule sits inside. The convergence the [lineage page]({{ '/lineage/' | relative_url }}) records is not historical decoration. It is the doctrinal ground for the rule the drafter applies on Monday morning to the document on the screen.

The rule, restated. Capital that shares the venture's risk earns its return justly and forms a partnership with the venture's other contributors. Capital that extracts a fixed return regardless of outcome forms a debt, and a debt is what it should be drafted and priced as. The instruments that confuse the two are the instruments the doctrine has been built to read. The drafter's work is to read them, and to draft the next ones so the structure matches the words.
