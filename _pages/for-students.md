# For Students

The Law and Entrepreneurship Program exists for the student who knows that law school is a serious investment of three years and tuition and is asking what those three years can be made to do. This page sets out, plainly, what the program offers, what it does not, and where its graduates go.

## What the program promises

Two things together.

First, three years that are worth living. A cohort, a calendar of events that matter, a faculty that takes seriously what students are thinking about, a clinic that puts students in front of real clients with real ventures, and an intellectual frame (the *societas* tradition of partnership-as-justice) that gives a student something to think about that the bar exam does not test.

Second, a path to the work. Real transactional simulation, real clinic clients, real externships at firms and agencies that Washington makes accessible, real mentorship from practitioners who came through CUA Law or who chose to give time to CUA students. The program is structured around the question every law student eventually asks: did this make me employable in the way I want to be employed?

The two are not in tension. The same infrastructure (mentorship, simulations, clinical practice, alumni network) that makes the three years worth living is what makes the path to the work real.

## The three-year arc

**1L year.** The required curriculum sets the foundation: Contracts, Civil Procedure, Torts, Criminal Law, Property, Constitutional Law, Legal Research and Writing. The LEP overlay in 1L is an introduction. An optional one-credit pass-fail Business Boot Camp gives students with no prior business background the vocabulary and orientation they need to make informed 2L course choices. Lecture-series events from practitioners and entrepreneurs are open to all students, not just those who have committed to the business path.

**2L year.** This is the year the program does most of its work. A transactional simulation course walks students through a deal from term sheet to closing, with the documents drafted, the diligence run, and the negotiation positions held. Substantive electives (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, Mergers and Acquisitions, Tax, Antitrust, Bankruptcy, Banking Law) are taken at the depth the student's career direction warrants. The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic places 2L and 3L students with real client ventures under faculty supervision; students counsel founders on entity formation, governance, contracts, intellectual property, regulatory questions, and financing.

**3L year.** Externships and capstone work. Washington's federal agencies, congressional offices, regulatory shops, K Street firms, in-house departments, and startup counsel positions are accessible in a way they are not from most other law schools. The program's faculty and alumni open doors that the student's resume alone cannot. Final-year clinic work, a substantive writing project tied to a faculty member's research, and a moot court or transactional competition close out the experience.

## The Washington advantage, used seriously

Most Washington law schools mention their location and then teach the same curriculum they would teach in Cleveland. The program treats Washington as the principal asset it is.

The federal regulatory state is where business law is being written right now: SEC, CFTC, FDIC, OCC, FTC, DOJ Antitrust, IRS, CFPB, FCC. The fintech and digital-asset bar runs through DC. K Street is two metro stops away. The major law firms maintain their largest regulatory practices in their DC offices. Trade associations, think tanks, and advocacy organizations on every side of every business-regulation question have their offices here. The program is structured so that students see practitioners from these institutions in classrooms, mentor those students through Sugarman-style monthly meetings, and supervise externships during the school year and the summer.

## Cohort, mentorship, and community

A student who chooses LEP becomes part of a small annual cohort whose members are committed to the same intellectual and professional direction. The cohort meets together regularly. Each member is paired with a faculty mentor and a practitioner mentor. The program funds attendance at one major conference each year. The Cluny Institute next door at CUA is a sister program in the same Catholic intellectual register; co-programming runs across the academic year. The Catholic intellectual tradition that grounds the program is not window dressing. It is the reason graduates of LEP can articulate, when they sit across a deal table from a counterparty, why a partnership-at-shared-risk structure is the just form of capital deployment.

## The clinic

The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic is one of the three operational components of the program. Students under faculty supervision counsel real client ventures on the work that transactional lawyers actually do: entity formation and choice-of-entity analysis, founder agreements, equity allocations, vesting, intellectual property assignment, employment classification, customer and vendor contracts, intellectual property licensing, regulatory questions, financing rounds. The clinic builds for the student a portfolio of real work that is shown to employers in interviews. It also builds for the founder a relationship with a future lawyer who understands their business.

## Where this leads

Graduates of the program go to a spread of employers. Some patterns hold. The transactional track produces associates at the New York and Washington offices of national firms, with regulatory specialties advantaged by the Washington location. The entrepreneurial track produces in-house counsel at growth-stage ventures, partners at the boutique transactional firms that serve startups, and small-business or community-development lawyers serving the kind of client the clinic teaches the student to serve. The regulatory and government track produces honors-program hires at the federal agencies and offices that the program's externships introduced the student to. The public-interest track produces lawyers who do mission work with the financial literacy and transactional fluency to make their organizations operate well.

A few graduates each year do not practice law. The program counts those as successes too, because the same training that makes a good transactional lawyer makes a good first-five-years operator at a venture.

## How to engage with the program

**Prospective students.** Direct questions about admissions to the CUA Columbus School of Law admissions office. The LEP-specific question of fit is one the program is happy to answer directly: write to the program directors. The program does not maintain a separate application; students apply through the regular CUA admissions process and self-identify their interest.

**Current CUA students.** Attend the public lectures. Sign up for the Business Boot Camp in 1L. Apply for the clinic in 2L. Email the program directors to request the cohort and mentorship pairing.

**Alumni and practitioners.** Mentorship slots are limited and we are deliberately conservative about how many we open per year. Reach out to the program directors with a brief note about the kind of mentorship you can offer and the kind of student you would be most useful to.
