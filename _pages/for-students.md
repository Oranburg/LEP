## What "business law" actually means

Some students arrive at law school knowing they want to do business law. Most do not, because the term covers a wide range of work that does not have a single shape. Business law is a family of practices that includes: drafting and negotiating commercial contracts (transactional work), advising boards and management on governance (corporate counsel), structuring deals when companies are bought, sold, or merged (M&A), structuring how companies raise money (securities and capital markets), regulating how financial markets and institutions operate (banking, fintech, broker-dealer, investment-adviser regulation), advising on tax structuring (tax), advising founders on building companies from formation (startup and venture counsel), and advising on the operational regulation of commercial life (antitrust, employment, IP licensing, real estate, bankruptcy). The work is *transactional* in the sense that it builds, modifies, and ends commercial relationships through documents and meetings. It is the counterpart to *litigation*, which resolves disputes through courts.

The program is designed to give students exposure to enough of the family to find their place in it, and depth in the parts that connect to the program's intellectual frame: partnership, work, and the just deployment of capital.

## The three-year arc

**1L year.** The required curriculum sets the foundation: Contracts, Civil Procedure, Torts, Criminal Law, Property, Constitutional Law, Legal Research and Writing. The LEP overlay in 1L is an introduction.

The Business Boot Camp launches Fall 2026. One credit, pass-fail. Meets weekly for an hour. Open to every 1L regardless of prior background; explicitly designed for the English major, the History major, the career-changer, the Engineer, and anyone who came to law school without a prior business or economics course. The reading list is short: a primer on legal-economic vocabulary, two cases that introduce the basic moves of transactional analysis, one Harvard Business Review essay, and one founder interview. The instructor is one of the co-directors (the assignment is set when the catalog is finalized). The point is not coverage; the point is to give the 1L the vocabulary and orientation needed to make informed 2L course choices.

Lecture-series events from practitioners and entrepreneurs are open to all students, not just those who have committed to the business path.

**2L year.** This is the year the program does most of its work. Recommended sequence for students who took the Boot Camp:

| Semester | Recommended courses |
|---|---|
| 2L Fall | Business Associations (required for the track); Federal Income Tax (recommended); one elective in the student's interest area |
| 2L Spring | Securities Regulation; Mergers and Acquisitions Survey OR enter the LEP Clinic first semester; one elective |

The Transactional Practicum, when it launches in Spring 2028, will be a 2L spring course of two credits, capped at twenty. Students will walk through one complete deal: term sheet, due diligence, drafting of the principal documents, exchange of correspondence with the opposing side, advising the client, negotiating the open issues, and drafting closing documents. The course is structured on the Case Western LLEAP 3 model. The instructor will be a co-director or a practitioner adjunct.

**3L year.** Externships and capstone work. Washington's federal agencies, congressional offices, regulatory shops, K Street firms, in-house departments, and startup counsel positions are accessed through CUA Law's existing externship program; the LEP-specific dimension is the program's faculty and practitioner network. Final-year work includes: continued clinic representation, a substantive writing project, and either the Transactional Practicum or a moot court / transactional competition. Recommended 3L sequence:

| Semester | Recommended courses |
|---|---|
| 3L Fall | LEP Clinic (continuing); Banking and Financial Regulation OR Antitrust; one externship |
| 3L Spring | Transactional Practicum (when launched) OR independent study with a faculty member; Bar-required electives; final externship or capstone |

## The Washington advantage, used seriously

Most Washington law schools mention their location and then teach the same curriculum they would teach in Cleveland. The program treats Washington as the principal asset it is.

The federal regulatory state is where business law is being written right now: SEC, CFTC, FDIC, OCC, FTC, DOJ Antitrust, IRS, CFPB, FCC. The fintech and digital-asset bar runs through DC. K Street is two metro stops away. The major law firms maintain their largest regulatory practices in their DC offices. Trade associations, think tanks, and advocacy organizations on every side of every business-regulation question have their offices here. The program is structured so that students see practitioners from these institutions in classrooms, meet them through monthly cohort events, and supervise externships during the school year and the summer.

Externships in business law are processed through CUA Law's existing externship office. The LEP-specific contribution is access to practitioner contacts that the standard externship office does not maintain: specifically, the network of Catholic business-law practitioners and the program's growing network in fintech regulatory practice, federal agency honors programs, and DC transactional boutiques.

## Cohort, mentorship, and community

A student who chooses LEP becomes part of a small annual cohort whose members are committed to the same intellectual and professional direction. The cohort meets monthly. Each member is paired with a faculty mentor and a practitioner mentor.

The mentor pool is being built across Summer 2026. The target categories for the first AY 2026-27 cohort are: two BigLaw transactional partners (DC offices), one in-house general counsel at a growth-stage venture, one fintech regulatory counsel, one community-business or small-firm transactional lawyer, and one entrepreneur with legal-counsel responsibilities. Names will be added to the program's mentor list as commitments are confirmed; the program is conservative about announcing mentors before they have agreed to a specific mentorship slot. Existing CUA Law alumni in the practitioner network feed this pool.

The program funds attendance at one major conference each year for cohort members in good standing. The conference selection and funding cap are being set for AY 2026-27; the working target is up to one thousand dollars per student for registration and travel for one conference per year, drawn from the program's budget or fellowship support.

The Cluny Institute next door at CUA is a sister program in the same Catholic intellectual register; co-programming runs across the academic year. Cluny is not a teaching program of LEP; the two co-programs share themes and audiences.

The Catholic intellectual tradition that grounds the program is not a religious-affiliation requirement and never will be. The program is open to students of all faiths and none. The Catholic frame matters because it is the *substance* of what the program teaches about partnership, work, and the just deployment of capital. A student does not need to be Catholic to engage with that substance any more than a student of constitutional law needs to be a Federalist to engage seriously with originalism. What is required is intellectual seriousness about the question of what business law is for.

## How the Catholic intellectual frame shows up in actual transactional work

The frame is not theology applied to law. The frame *is* the doctrinal vocabulary the lawyer uses to read the deal.

Take a Series Seed term sheet. Six provisions in that document carry the partnership-versus-loan question that runs through the *societas* tradition. The liquidation preference: a 1x non-participating preferred share preserves the investor as a co-owner who shares in the loss if the venture underperforms; a 2x participating preferred share converts the investor into a senior creditor with a guaranteed return. The first honors the *societas* structure; the second slides toward *mutuum*. The drag-along provision: a drag-along that protects minority founders against a forced sale below their preference preserves the partnership; one that compels them to sell on majority terms collapses the partnership into a creditor relationship. The vesting schedule: a four-year vest with a one-year cliff treats the founder's labor as the partnership contribution Aquinas describes; an immediate-vesting schedule with a generous severance treats the founder as a salaried employee with a payout option. The participation rights, the protective provisions, the conversion mechanics: each one carries the same question.

A lawyer trained in this program is taught to read these clauses with the *societas* vocabulary in hand. The point is not to argue with the client about Aquinas. The point is to see the structural reality of what the document does, and to advise the client accordingly.

## The clinic

The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic is the operational center of the program. Students under faculty supervision counsel real client ventures on the work that transactional lawyers actually do. Client intake, conflict screening, malpractice coverage, and supervision are administered through CUA Law's existing clinical infrastructure.

The work the student does over the clinic year includes the standard document portfolio that an entry-level transactional lawyer needs to be able to produce. The target is for every clinic student to graduate with the following portfolio (redacted as required for client confidentiality):

- A formation package: certificate of formation, operating agreement or bylaws, organizational consent
- A founder agreement with vesting, IP assignment, and confidentiality
- A standard NDA
- A customer or vendor master services agreement
- A SAFE or convertible note (or a Series Seed term sheet review with redlines)
- An employment classification analysis and offer letter package
- A regulatory analysis (entity-type compliance, securities Reg D / Rule 506(b), 409A valuation question, or a similar concrete regulatory question relevant to the client)
- A board consent or other governance document tied to a real client matter

These deliverables are the student's portfolio for job interviews. Hiring partners ask to see them. The clinic exists in part so that the student can hand over real work product that demonstrates the kind of lawyer the program produced.

The client pipeline is being deepened in AY 2026-27 with explicit subject-matter focus on entrepreneurial and community business representation. The program is in development on standing client-pipeline relationships with Catholic-affiliated mission organizations (community development institutions, small-business lenders, faith-based nonprofits) so that the clinic's mission orientation is operational, not aspirational.

## Where this leads

The program has not yet graduated its first dedicated LEP cohort; the first will graduate in 2029. The placement plan reflects what the program is designed to feed. The transactional track is designed to feed associates into the New York and Washington offices of national firms, with regulatory specialties advantaged by the Washington location. The entrepreneurial track is designed to feed in-house counsel positions at growth-stage ventures, partners at the boutique transactional firms that serve startups, and small-business or community-development lawyers serving the kind of client the clinic teaches the student to serve. The regulatory and government track is designed to feed honors-program hires at the federal agencies and offices that the program's externships introduce the student to. The public-interest track is designed to feed lawyers who do mission work with the financial literacy and transactional fluency to make their organizations operate well.

Placement data for the current CUA Columbus School of Law is published in the school's ABA-required disclosures and Standard 509 report at law.edu. The LEP-specific data set will build over the next three years.

**If you are a prospective student (0L or considering CUA):**

1. Submit your CUA Columbus School of Law admission application through the regular admissions process. There is no separate LEP application.

3. Email the program directors to ask about the monthly cohort meeting and to be added to the cohort mailing list.

**If you are a current 2L:**

1. Apply for the LEP Clinic through CUA Law's clinical-program application for the spring or fall semester.
2. Email the program directors to request a practitioner mentorship pairing for AY 2026-27.
3. Attend the cohort monthly meetings.

**If you are a current 3L:**

1. Apply for an externship at a federal agency, regulatory shop, or DC firm office through the CUA Law externship office, with LEP support on practitioner contacts.
2. Continue the clinic if you began it as a 2L.
3. Consider an independent-study writing project with a faculty member on a question that connects the program's intellectual frame to your career direction.

Reach out to the program directors with a brief note describing the kind of mentorship you can offer (transactional, regulatory, in-house, entrepreneurial, community business, fintech, agency honors-program path) and the kind of student you would be most useful to. ### The Catholic intellectual identity

**Do I have to be Catholic?**
No. The program is open to students of all faiths and none. There is no religious-affiliation requirement and there never will be one. Your grade and your standing in the cohort do not depend on engaging with the tradition on its own theological terms.

## Glossary

These terms come up on the program's intellectual-frame pages (Lineage, Societas, Magnifica Humanitas). Plain definitions follow. None of them is required vocabulary for any course; they are the conceptual furniture of what the program teaches.

*Societas* (Latin): partnership. In Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* II-II q. 78 a. 2 ad 5, capital deployed into a partnership at shared risk earns a return justly, because the investor remains a co-owner who shares in the loss. The conceptual ancestor of modern partnership and venture-capital forms.

*Mutuum* (Latin): a loan. Specifically, a loan at fixed interest with no risk to the lender. In Aquinas, *mutuum* taken at interest is the structure that constitutes usury. The contrast with *societas* is the central distinction in the partnership-form-of-justice tradition.

*Shutafut* (שֻׁתָּפוּת, Hebrew): partnership. In Maimonides's *Mishneh Torah*, Hilkhot Mattenot Aniyim 10:7, *shutafut* with the poor is named as the highest of the eight rungs of *tzedakah*: strengthening the hand of the one who has fallen, by partnership, so he need not depend on others.

*Tzedakah* (צְדָקָה, Hebrew): justice-and-charity. The Hebrew term has no English equivalent; it carries both meanings without separating them. The point is that what English calls "charity" Hebrew treats as a matter of justice.

*Isqa* (עִסְקָא, Aramaic): the Talmudic half-loan, half-deposit commercial partnership structure that lets Jewish commercial law treat capital deployment as partnership rather than as interest-bearing debt. Developed in Bava Metzia. The mechanism that operationalizes the highest rung in actual commercial life.

*Heter isqa* (Hebrew + Aramaic): the medieval and modern legal instrument that formally structures business arrangements as *isqa* partnerships to satisfy the Jewish-law prohibition on interest. Still used today.

*Exercitor navis* (Latin): the Roman law of the commercial operator of a ship. The Ulpian-era jurisprudence on shared maritime venture is the structural ancestor on which Aquinas built the *societas* doctrine. See Digest 14.1.

*Magnifica Humanitas*: the encyclical of Pope Leo XIV, given at Rome on 15 May 2026. Subtitle: *On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*. The most recent statement of the Catholic social tradition in which the program is grounded. Read the full text at [vatican.va](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html).
