# For Students

The Law and Entrepreneurship Program exists for the student who knows that law school is a serious investment of three years and tuition and is asking what those three years can be made to do. This page sets out, plainly, what the program offers right now, what is being built for AY 2026-27 and AY 2027-28, and where the program intends to take graduates.

## The three-year arc

**1L year.** The required curriculum sets the foundation: Contracts, Civil Procedure, Torts, Criminal Law, Property, Constitutional Law, Legal Research and Writing. The LEP overlay in 1L is an introduction. The one-credit pass-fail Business Boot Camp launching Fall 2026 gives students with no prior business background the vocabulary and orientation they need to make informed 2L course choices. The reading list is short. The point is not coverage. The point is to make business-law as a career feel accessible to the 1L who feels behind. Lecture-series events from practitioners and entrepreneurs are open to all students, not just those who have committed to the business path.

**2L year.** This is the year the program does most of its work. Substantive electives (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, Mergers and Acquisitions, Tax, Antitrust, Bankruptcy, Banking Law) are taken at the depth the student's career direction warrants. The Transactional Practicum, when it launches in Spring 2028, will walk students through a deal from term sheet to closing with documents drafted, diligence run, and negotiation positions held. The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic places 2L and 3L students with real client ventures under faculty supervision; students counsel founders on entity formation, governance, contracts, intellectual property, regulatory questions, and financing.

**3L year.** Externships and capstone work. Washington's federal agencies, congressional offices, regulatory shops, K Street firms, in-house departments, and startup counsel positions are accessible from CUA's existing externship program. The LEP-specific dimension is the program's faculty and practitioner network, which will introduce students to specific opportunities the standard externship office does not reach. Final-year clinic work, a substantive writing project tied to a faculty member's research, and a moot court or transactional competition close out the experience.

## The Washington advantage, used seriously

Most Washington law schools mention their location and then teach the same curriculum they would teach in Cleveland. The program treats Washington as the principal asset it is.

The federal regulatory state is where business law is being written right now: SEC, CFTC, FDIC, OCC, FTC, DOJ Antitrust, IRS, CFPB, FCC. The fintech and digital-asset bar runs through DC. K Street is two metro stops away. The major law firms maintain their largest regulatory practices in their DC offices. Trade associations, think tanks, and advocacy organizations on every side of every business-regulation question have their offices here. The program is structured so that students see practitioners from these institutions in classrooms, mentor students through monthly meetings, and supervise externships during the school year and the summer.

## Cohort, mentorship, and community

A student who chooses LEP becomes part of a small annual cohort whose members are committed to the same intellectual and professional direction. The cohort meets monthly. Each member is paired with a faculty mentor and a practitioner mentor. The program funds attendance at one major conference each year. The Cluny Institute next door at CUA is a sister program in the same Catholic intellectual register; co-programming runs across the academic year.

The Catholic intellectual tradition that grounds the program is not a religious-affiliation requirement and never will be. The program is open to students of all faiths and none. The Catholic frame matters because it is the *substance* of what the program teaches about partnership, work, and the just deployment of capital. A student does not need to be Catholic to engage with that substance any more than a student of constitutional law needs to be a Federalist to engage seriously with originalism. What is required is intellectual seriousness about the question of what business law is for.

## The clinic

The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic is the operational center of the program. Students under faculty supervision counsel real client ventures on the work that transactional lawyers actually do: entity formation and choice-of-entity analysis, founder agreements, equity allocations, vesting, intellectual property assignment, employment classification, customer and vendor contracts, intellectual property licensing, regulatory questions, financing rounds. The clinic builds for the student a portfolio of work product the student takes into job interviews. Client intake, conflict screening, malpractice coverage, and supervision are administered through CUA Law's existing clinical infrastructure.

## Where this leads

The program has not yet graduated its first dedicated LEP cohort; the first will graduate in 2029. The placement plan reflects what the program is designed to feed. The transactional track is designed to feed associates into the New York and Washington offices of national firms, with regulatory specialties advantaged by the Washington location. The entrepreneurial track is designed to feed in-house counsel positions at growth-stage ventures, partners at the boutique transactional firms that serve startups, and small-business or community-development lawyers serving the kind of client the clinic teaches the student to serve. The regulatory and government track is designed to feed honors-program hires at the federal agencies and offices that the program's externships introduce the student to. The public-interest track is designed to feed lawyers who do mission work with the financial literacy and transactional fluency to make their organizations operate well.

Placement data for the current CUA Columbus School of Law is published in the school's ABA-required disclosures and Standard 509 report. The LEP-specific data set will build over the next three years.

## How to engage with the program

**Prospective students.** Direct questions about admissions to the CUA Columbus School of Law admissions office. The LEP-specific question of fit is one the program is happy to answer directly: write to the program directors. The program does not maintain a separate application; students apply through the regular CUA admissions process and self-identify their interest. A formal LEP cohort application process is being developed for AY 2026-27.

**Current CUA students.** Attend the public lectures. Sign up for the Business Boot Camp in 1L when it opens for registration. Apply for the clinic in 2L through the CUA Law clinical-program application. Email the program directors to request the cohort and mentorship pairing.

**Alumni and practitioners.** Mentorship slots are limited and the program is deliberately conservative about how many are opened per year. Reach out to the program directors with a brief note about the kind of mentorship you can offer and the kind of student you would be most useful to.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is the program actually running yet?

The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic is operating now at CUA Law. The cohort structure, the monthly meetings, the formal mentorship pairings, the Business Boot Camp, and the practitioner-supervised practicum course are scheduled for launch in AY 2026-27 and AY 2027-28. The status table at the top of this page tells you which components are operating now and which are being built. The program will be formally constituted at CUA, named, and authorized through the standard institutional process.

### Who runs the program?

Alexander Hoffarth, Visiting Assistant Professor of Law and the founding Director of the program, who has built the program since arriving at CUA. Seth Oranburg joins as co-director on August 20, 2026, after a faculty tenure at the University of New Hampshire Franklin Pierce School of Law. Both are listed on the People page. ### What is the Catholic intellectual tradition doing in a business law program? Do I have to be Catholic?

The Catholic intellectual tradition is the substance the program teaches about partnership, work, and the just deployment of capital. The convergence with the Jewish *shutafut* tradition is part of the same substance. You do not have to be Catholic. You do not have to be religious at all. The program is open to students of all faiths and none. The tradition shows up in the program in the way that a tradition shows up in any serious legal program: as the source of the doctrinal vocabulary you are taught to use precisely. You will not be graded on your religious commitment. You will be expected to take the intellectual content seriously the way you would in any rigorous class.

### What is the cohort? How do I join it?

The LEP cohort is the group of students who self-identify with the program's intellectual and professional direction and who commit to the monthly meetings, the mentorship pairings, the events, and (when it launches) the clinic. Joining is by self-identification and a short conversation with the directors. The cohort size will be small, on the order of fifteen to twenty-five students per class year as the program reaches steady state. There is no separate admission requirement beyond CUA Law admission. A formal cohort application process is being developed.

### What does the program cost? Is there scholarship money?

Tuition and admission-cycle scholarships are administered by the CUA Columbus School of Law admissions office, not by the program. The program is in development on a dedicated LEP Fellowship that would carry stipend support, conference travel, and research funding for a small number of students per class. That fellowship is not yet announced. Standard CUA Law need-based and merit-based aid is available through the regular admissions process.

### What courses does LEP offer that other students cannot take?

LEP-specific courses are: the Business Boot Camp (1L fall, one credit, pass-fail, launching 2026); the Transactional Practicum (2L spring, two credits, launching 2028); the Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic (2L and 3L). The substantive business law electives (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, M&A, Tax, Antitrust) are open to all CUA Law students. LEP students get a curated sequencing and mentorship layer on top.

### Who teaches the clinic? Are there real clients?

The clinic is supervised by CUA Law faculty and administered through CUA Law's clinical-program infrastructure. Clients are real ventures: early-stage startups, small businesses, community-development organizations, nonprofit ventures. Client intake, conflict checks, and malpractice coverage are handled through CUA's existing clinical-program apparatus. The clinic is being deepened in AY 2026-27 to focus more explicitly on entrepreneurial and community business representation.

### What firms and agencies does the program place students at?

The program has not yet graduated its first dedicated cohort. The first LEP cohort graduates in 2029. CUA Law's school-wide placement data is published in its annual Standard 509 report and is available from the CUA Law career office. The LEP-specific placement record will be built and published as cohorts graduate. The program's design is oriented to four placement paths: transactional BigLaw, entrepreneurial counsel (in-house and boutique), federal regulatory practice (agency honors programs and K Street regulatory specialists), and public-interest with business-law facility.

### Who are the practitioner mentors?

The mentor network is being built over Summer 2026 with a target of fifteen to twenty-five practitioner mentors across the four placement paths for the first AY 2026-27 cohort. Names will be published as commitments are confirmed. The mentor pool draws from the CUA Law alumni network, the broader Catholic legal practitioner community, and the practitioner network of the co-directors and their colleagues.

### What is the Cluny Institute and why is it on the People page?

The Cluny Institute is a separate CUA program founded by Luke Burgis (Associate Professor of Business at CUA) in December 2024 with a $2.5 million Templeton Foundation grant. The Institute works on dialogue across technology, religion, and culture. The Institute is adjacent to LEP in intellectual focus (the AI age, work, human flourishing read through the Catholic tradition); the two programs co-program where their themes meet. Burgis is not a teaching member of LEP. He is named on the People page as an institutional collaborator at CUA, not as LEP faculty.

### Is this program CUA-authorized?

The program operates within the Columbus School of Law and is being formally constituted through the standard CUA institutional process for AY 2026-27. This site (oranburg.law/LEP) is the personal working space of the co-directors and is not a CUA-authorized communication; CUA-authorized program communications will appear on the CUA Law website at law.edu when the program is formally launched. ### Will LEP show on my transcript or diploma?

The Business Boot Camp, the Transactional Practicum, and the clinic will show on your transcript as the courses they are. A formal LEP cohort designation (akin to Penn's Wharton certificate or Northwestern's transactional-law certificate) is under discussion with the CUA Law administration. Whether it lands as a transcript notation, a diploma supplement, or a separate letter of completion will be announced when the institutional review is complete.

### What if Seth Oranburg leaves CUA in three years? Does the program survive?

The program is institutionally CUA's, not personally any individual faculty member's. Alexander Hoffarth is the founding Director and continues. The clinic operates through CUA's clinical infrastructure. The relationships with the Cluny Institute and other CUA centers are inter-program, not inter-personal. The program's intellectual identity (the *societas* spine) is owned by the tradition, not by any one scholar.

## Glossary

These terms come up on the program's intellectual-frame pages (Lineage, Societas, Magnifica Humanitas). Plain definitions follow. None of them is required vocabulary for any course; they are the conceptual furniture of what the program teaches.

*Societas* (Latin): partnership. In Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* II-II q. 78 a. 2 ad 5, capital deployed into a partnership at shared risk earns a return justly, because the investor remains a co-owner who shares in the loss. The conceptual ancestor of modern partnership and venture-capital forms.

*Mutuum* (Latin): a loan. Specifically, a loan at fixed interest with no risk to the lender. In Aquinas, *mutuum* taken at interest is the structure that constitutes usury. The contrast with *societas* is the central distinction in the partnership-form-of-justice tradition.

*Shutafut* (שֻׁתָּפוּת, Hebrew): partnership. In Maimonides's *Mishneh Torah*, Hilkhot Mattenot Aniyim 10:7, *shutafut* with the poor is named as the highest of the eight rungs of *tzedakah*: strengthening the hand of the one who has fallen, by partnership, so he need not depend on others.

*Tzedakah* (צְדָקָה, Hebrew): justice-and-charity. The Hebrew term has no English equivalent; it carries both meanings without separating them. The point is that what English calls "charity" Hebrew treats as a matter of justice.

*Isqa* (עִסְקָא, Aramaic): the Talmudic half-loan, half-deposit commercial partnership structure that lets Jewish commercial law treat capital deployment as partnership rather than as interest-bearing debt. Developed in Bava Metzia. The mechanism that operationalizes the highest rung in actual commercial life.

*Heter isqa* (Hebrew + Aramaic): the medieval and modern legal instrument that formally structures business arrangements as *isqa* partnerships to satisfy the Jewish-law prohibition on interest. Still used today.

*Exercitor navis* (Latin): the Roman law of the commercial operator of a ship. The Ulpian-era jurisprudence on shared maritime venture is the structural ancestor on which Aquinas built the *societas* doctrine. See Digest 14.1.

*Magnifica Humanitas*: the encyclical of Pope Leo XIV, given at Rome on 15 May 2026. Subtitle: *On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*. The most recent statement of the Catholic social tradition in which the program is grounded.
