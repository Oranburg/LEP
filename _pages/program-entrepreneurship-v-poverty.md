# Entrepreneurship v. Poverty

A year-themed event series, a clinic-deepening initiative, and a curriculum overlay that treats entrepreneurship as the answer to poverty in measurable ways. Two framings below; the program substance is the same.

## The problem

Law schools treat poverty as a subject for legal aid and public-benefits practice, not as a problem entrepreneurial capital and entrepreneurial law can answer. The result is a generation of lawyers who think of poverty as a downstream client condition rather than as the upstream absence of the legal infrastructure (formation, finance, contract, employment, regulation) that makes dignified work possible. Pope Leo XIV's *Magnifica Humanitas* (15 May 2026) returns the Church to the Nehemiah image: the walls of Jerusalem are the legal-institutional infrastructure in which a free people can work and live without fear or dependence. The program turns that image into a teachable, measurable curriculum.

## The intervention

A year-themed event series, a clinic-deepening initiative, and a curriculum overlay. The year theme for AY 2026-27 is *Entrepreneurship v. Poverty*. The event slate includes the Magatte Wade keynote (entrepreneurship as the answer to poverty), an SBA capital-policy panel, a DOJ Antitrust panel on the JetBlue / Spirit decision (the conditions for entrepreneurial partnership in regulated markets), and a practitioner panel on transactional drafting (partnership form in actual documents). The clinic deepens its service to early-stage entrepreneurial ventures led by low-and-moderate-income founders, modeled on Cardozo's Entrepreneurship and Community Business Clinic. The curriculum overlay adds the *societas* reading lens (drawing on Aquinas, Maimonides, and the Salamanca commercial moral theology) to Business Associations, Securities Regulation, and the transactional practicum.

## Deliverables

1. Four year-theme public events with verified attendance, recorded and published.
2. A clinic client pipeline of ten to fifteen active entrepreneurial ventures per year, with documented client deliverables (formation, founder agreement, SAFE or convertible note review, employment classification, regulatory analysis).
3. A curriculum overlay document (the *societas* reading lens) used in three to five courses by AY 2027-28.
4. A published year-theme essay framing the program intellectually.
5. A measurable poverty-reduction outcome metric for clinic clients (job creation, revenue, sustained operations at one year and three years).

## Timeline

**Year 1:** Full event slate (four events). Clinic deepening to ten active clients. Year-theme essay published. Curriculum overlay drafted.

**Year 2:** Event slate continues on the year-two theme. Clinic to fifteen clients. Curriculum overlay in three courses. First one-year client outcome data.

**Year 3:** Event slate continues. Clinic stabilized at fifteen to twenty clients. Curriculum overlay in five courses. First three-year client outcome data.

## Budget

Three-year total: **$450,000 to $700,000**.

| Line item | Three-year range |
|---|---|
| Faculty time (curation, curriculum overlay) | $90,000 to $135,000 |
| Clinic deepening (clinical fellow, intake capacity) | $180,000 to $270,000 |
| Programming (four events per year, three years) | $90,000 to $135,000 |
| Dissemination (year-theme essay, recordings, web) | $30,000 to $45,000 |
| Indirect costs (15 percent) | $58,500 to $90,000 |

## Metrics

Event attendance and recording views. Clinic client count, deliverable count, client survival and employment outcomes at one and three years. Curriculum-overlay course adoption. Annual report to the funder with client-outcome data table.

## The problem

Law schools treat poverty as a client condition for legal aid and public-benefits practice, not as the upstream absence of legal infrastructure (formation law, capital-formation rules, contract enforcement, employment classification, sensible regulation) that makes dignified entrepreneurial work possible. Lawyers trained inside this framing become ministers to dependence rather than builders of the conditions in which people can work for themselves. The program retrains the next generation to see the legal infrastructure for what it is: the precondition of free, productive, dignified work.
