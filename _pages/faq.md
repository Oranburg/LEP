## About the program

**Is the program actually running yet?**
The Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic is operating now at CUA Law. The cohort structure, the monthly meetings, the formal mentorship pairings, the Business Boot Camp, and the practitioner-supervised practicum course are scheduled for launch in AY 2026-27 and AY 2027-28. The status table on the [For Students overview]({{ '/for-students/' | relative_url }}) tells you which components are operating now and which are being built. The program will be formally constituted at CUA, named, and authorized through the standard institutional process.

**Who runs the program?**
Alexander Hoffarth, Visiting Assistant Professor of Law and the founding Director, has built the program since arriving at CUA. Seth Oranburg joins as co-director on August 20, 2026, after a faculty tenure at the University of New Hampshire Franklin Pierce School of Law. Both are listed on the [People page]({{ '/people/' | relative_url }}).

**Is this program CUA-authorized?**
The program operates within the Columbus School of Law and is being formally constituted through the standard CUA institutional process for AY 2026-27. This site (oranburg.law/LEP) is the personal working space of the co-directors and is not a CUA-authorized communication; CUA-authorized program communications will appear on law.edu when the program is formally launched. The clinic, the courses, and the degree credit are administered through CUA Law in the standard way.

**Will LEP show on my transcript or diploma?**
The Business Boot Camp, the Transactional Practicum, and the clinic will show on your transcript as the courses they are. A formal LEP cohort designation (akin to Penn's Wharton certificate or Northwestern's transactional-law certificate) is under discussion with the CUA Law administration. Whether it lands as a transcript notation, a diploma supplement, or a separate letter of completion will be announced when the institutional review is complete.

**What if Seth Oranburg leaves CUA in three years? Does the program survive?**
The program is institutionally CUA's, not personally any individual faculty member's. Alexander Hoffarth is the founding Director and continues. The clinic operates through CUA's clinical infrastructure. The relationships with the Cluny Institute and other CUA centers are inter-program, not inter-personal. The program's intellectual identity is owned by the tradition, not by any one scholar.

**What is the Cluny Institute and why is it on the People page?**
The Cluny Institute is a separate CUA program founded by Luke Burgis (Associate Professor of Business at CUA) in December 2024 with a $2.5 million Templeton Foundation grant. The Institute works on dialogue across technology, religion, and culture. The Institute is adjacent to LEP in intellectual focus; the two programs co-program where their themes meet. Burgis is not a teaching member of LEP. He is named on the People page as an institutional collaborator at CUA, not as LEP faculty.

## The Catholic intellectual identity

**Do I have to be Catholic?**
No. The program is open to students of all faiths and none. There is no religious-affiliation requirement and there never will be one. Your grade and your standing in the cohort do not depend on engaging with the tradition on its own theological terms.

**How does the Catholic frame show up in courses day to day?**
The frame is the doctrinal vocabulary the lawyer uses to read the deal. Take a Series Seed term sheet. Six provisions in that document carry the partnership-versus-loan question that runs through the *societas* tradition. The liquidation preference: a 1x non-participating preferred share preserves the investor as a co-owner who shares in the loss; a 2x participating preferred share converts the investor into a senior creditor with a guaranteed return. The first honors the *societas* structure; the second slides toward *mutuum*. The drag-along provision, the vesting schedule, the participation rights, the protective provisions, the conversion mechanics: each one carries the same question. A lawyer trained in this program is taught to read these clauses with the *societas* vocabulary in hand.

**I am Jewish, Muslim, or non-religious. Will I feel out of place?**
The Jewish *shutafut* tradition is treated on the same page as the Catholic *societas* tradition because the two converge on the same conclusion through independent reasoning. The program teaches both as a single intellectual inheritance. A non-Catholic student will see the same intellectual seriousness applied to the Maimonidean tradition as to the Thomistic one. The expectation is intellectual engagement, not religious commitment.

**Is the Catholic frame graded? Do I have to write a paper on Aquinas?**
No. You will write papers and take exams in the substantive courses on the doctrinal content of those courses. The intellectual frame is the why-the-doctrine-takes-this-shape context that runs through the program; it is not a separate exam subject.

## Cohort, admissions, and cost

**What is the cohort? How do I join it?**
The LEP cohort is the group of students who self-identify with the program's intellectual and professional direction and who commit to the monthly meetings, the mentorship pairings, the events, and the clinic. Joining is by self-identification and a short conversation with the directors. The cohort size will be small, on the order of fifteen to twenty-five students per class year as the program reaches steady state. There is no separate admission requirement beyond CUA Law admission. A formal cohort application process is being developed.

**What does the program cost? Is there scholarship money?**
Tuition and admission-cycle scholarships are administered by the CUA Columbus School of Law admissions office, not by the program. The program is in development on a dedicated LEP Fellowship that would carry stipend support, conference travel, and research funding for a small number of students per class. That fellowship is not yet announced. Standard CUA Law need-based and merit-based aid is available through the regular admissions process.

**How is the LEP cohort different from the existing CUA Business Law track?**
CUA Law has substantive business law course offerings that any CUA student can take. The LEP cohort adds three things on top of those courses: a sequenced curriculum recommendation, a mentorship pairing (faculty plus practitioner), and access to the program's events, clinic priority, and cohort community. The Business Boot Camp and the Transactional Practicum, when launched, will be LEP-led courses. You do not have to be in the LEP cohort to take the substantive business law electives at CUA.

**Why should I choose CUA-LEP over Cardozo, Brooklyn, Case Western, or American?**
The program is being built specifically to compete with those schools, not the top-twenty schools that win on resources LEP does not have. The wager is that Washington taken seriously, plus the Catholic intellectual tradition treated as substantive doctrine, plus a small cohort with genuine mentorship and a working clinic, will produce a better path to transactional, regulatory, and entrepreneurial practice than the schools you are weighing against. The wager is being tested now. If you want operational data on the first cohort, that data will not exist until 2029.

## Courses and curriculum

**What courses does LEP offer that other students cannot take?**
LEP-specific courses are the Business Boot Camp (1L fall, one credit, pass-fail, launching 2026), the Transactional Practicum (2L spring, two credits, launching 2028), and the Law and Entrepreneurship Clinic (2L and 3L). The substantive business law electives are open to all CUA Law students; LEP students get a curated sequencing and mentorship layer on top.

**Who teaches the substantive business law electives?**
The substantive electives (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, M&A, Tax, Antitrust, Bankruptcy, Banking Law) are taught by CUA Law's existing faculty. The program does not duplicate or replace these courses; it provides the cohort, mentorship, simulation, and clinic infrastructure that wraps around the substantive electives. The CUA Law faculty roster is at law.edu.

**Will the Boot Camp and the Practicum be offered every year?**
Yes. The Boot Camp launches Fall 2026 and will run every fall thereafter. The Transactional Practicum is targeted for Spring 2028 launch with annual spring offerings.

**Bar-tested courses: what should I still plan to take?**
The LEP track does not displace the courses you should take for bar preparation. The substantive electives the program recommends (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, Federal Income Tax) are all bar-relevant in most jurisdictions. The standard CUA Law bar-prep advice from the registrar and from the Academic Success program applies; LEP does not change it.

**I came to law school with zero business background. Is this program for me?**
Yes. The Business Boot Camp is designed for you specifically. The reading list is short. The instructor will assume no prior business or economics coursework. The point of the course is to give a 1L who arrived without the vocabulary the orientation needed to make informed 2L course choices.

**What does "transactional simulation" actually mean? What do I do in that class?**
You take one deal from term sheet to closing. You draft the documents on the buy-side or the sell-side (depending on the role you are assigned). You read and redline the documents the other side produces. You do due diligence on the simulated target company. You advise the client on the open issues. You negotiate against the students playing the other side. At the end of the semester, you have closed a transaction and produced a closing binder. The course is modeled on Case Western's LLEAP 3, which has run for a decade as a peer program's flagship transactional course.

## The clinic

**Who teaches the clinic? Are the clients real?**
The clinic is supervised by CUA Law faculty and administered through CUA Law's clinical-program infrastructure. Clients are real ventures: early-stage startups, small businesses, community-development organizations, nonprofit ventures. Client intake, conflict checks, and malpractice coverage are handled through CUA's existing clinical-program apparatus.

**Is the clinic ABA-approved for credit?**
Yes. CUA Law's clinical-program apparatus is ABA-approved and the LEP Clinic is a clinical course on the CUA Law schedule with academic credit.

**Are the clinic clients revenue-generating ventures or friends-and-family LLCs?**
The standard mix is early-stage ventures with real revenue or near-revenue, small businesses, nonprofit and community-development organizations, and occasional pre-revenue startups whose legal needs match what the clinic can supervise responsibly. The clinic deepens the client pipeline through partnerships with Catholic-affiliated mission organizations and small-business networks; the goal is a stable, vetted pipeline rather than ad-hoc client intake.

**How many client matters does a clinic student carry per semester?**
The target is two to three active matters per student per semester, scaled to the complexity of each matter. A student finishing the clinic should have substantive responsibility for at least three completed deliverables that go into the document portfolio.

**What documents will I actually draft in the clinic?**
The target portfolio is: a formation package (certificate of formation, operating agreement or bylaws, organizational consent); a founder agreement with vesting, IP assignment, and confidentiality; a standard NDA; a customer or vendor master services agreement; a SAFE or convertible note or term sheet review with redlines; an employment classification analysis and offer letter package; a regulatory analysis (entity-type compliance, Reg D / Rule 506(b), 409A valuation question, or similar); a board consent or other governance document tied to a real client matter. A student who completes the clinic should leave with a redacted document package suitable for showing to hiring partners in interviews.

**What if I want to litigate, not transact?**
The LEP clinic is transactional. CUA Law has separate litigation clinics that you would apply to instead. The LEP cohort is welcoming to litigators who want exposure to the transactional vocabulary, but the clinic is not the right vehicle for someone whose primary direction is courtroom advocacy.

## Career outcomes and placement

**What firms and agencies does the program place students at?**
The program has not yet graduated its first dedicated cohort. The first LEP cohort graduates in 2029. CUA Law's school-wide placement data is published in its annual Standard 509 report and is available from the CUA Law career office. The LEP-specific placement record will be built and published as cohorts graduate.

**What does LEP do for me when I am interviewing at BigLaw?**
Four concrete things. First, a redacted document portfolio from the clinic that you can hand to a hiring partner as proof of what you have actually done. Second, named faculty and practitioner mentors who pick up the phone for callbacks. Third, a curated set of substantive electives sequenced for transactional work. Fourth, a cohort identification that signals to recruiters that you committed to a serious program rather than collecting unrelated credits.

**I do not want BigLaw. Does LEP still help me?**
Yes. The program's design serves four paths in addition to BigLaw: in-house counsel at growth-stage ventures, partner at a boutique transactional firm, federal regulatory practice (agency honors programs and K Street regulatory specialists), and public-interest with business-law facility. The clinic and the cohort serve these paths as directly as they serve the BigLaw path.

**What is bar passage looking like for LEP students?**
Bar passage is a school-wide question; the LEP track does not displace the courses you should take for bar preparation, so LEP students sit for the bar with the same preparation as any CUA Law student in the same elective track. Detailed CUA Law bar-passage data is published with the school's Standard 509 report.

## Mentors, externships, and the DC advantage

**Who are the practitioner mentors?**
The mentor network is being built across Summer 2026 with the target categories on the [For Mentors page]({{ '/for-students/mentors/' | relative_url }}). Names will be published as commitments are confirmed. The mentor pool draws from the CUA Law alumni network, the broader Catholic legal practitioner community, and the practitioner network of the co-directors and their colleagues.

**How are mentorship pairings made? What happens if it does not work?**
You meet with the directors to discuss your career direction and interests. The directors match you with a faculty mentor and a practitioner mentor whose work and disposition fit yours. The minimum commitment is two meetings per semester. If the match is not working after one semester, you ask the directors for a re-pairing. There is no penalty.

**How does LEP fit with the standard CUA externship program?**
The CUA Law externship office processes all externships. LEP does not run a parallel externship program. The LEP contribution is access to practitioner contacts at specific organizations (federal regulatory agencies, DC transactional boutiques, fintech regulatory shops, Catholic-affiliated business institutions) that the standard externship office does not maintain relationships with. You apply through the standard process; LEP adds names and warm introductions.

**What is the conference funding actually for?**
The working target is up to one thousand dollars per cohort student per year for registration and travel for one conference relevant to the program (transactional, regulatory, entrepreneurial, or in the program's intellectual register). The final funding cap and conference-list approval process will be set for AY 2026-27.

## For current CUA students

**I am a current 1L in Spring 2026. Can I engage with the program now?**
Yes. Email the directors at hoffarth@cua.edu and ask to be added to the cohort mailing list and notified about the first cohort coffee in September 2026. Look at the readings page for one entry-point essay you can engage with before 2L registration. Register for the Business Boot Camp when it appears in the Fall 2026 1L menu. See the [For Current Students page]({{ '/for-students/current/' | relative_url }}) for the full action list.

**I am a 2L now. Am I too late to join?**
No. Email the directors and request a mentorship pairing for AY 2026-27. Apply for the LEP Clinic through CUA Law's clinical-program application for the spring or fall semester. Attend the cohort monthly meetings.

**I am a 3L. Is there anything for me?**
Yes. Apply for an externship at a federal agency, regulatory shop, or DC firm office through the CUA Law externship office, with LEP support on practitioner contacts. Continue the clinic if you began it as a 2L. Consider an independent-study writing project with a faculty member on a question that connects the program's intellectual frame to your career direction. Use the cohort and the alumni network as job-search resources.

## Practical questions

***Societas* (and *shutafut*): is this a course, a student organization, a fellowship, or a slogan?**
It is the conceptual frame the program teaches. It is not a separate course or a student organization. The doctrinal content of *societas* and *shutafut* shows up in the substantive courses where the partnership-versus-loan question matters (Business Associations, Securities Regulation, M&A, Banking Law), and in the clinic when you advise clients on capital structure. A *Societas* fellowship with stipend support is in development as part of the LEP Fellowship.

**Where do I read *Magnifica Humanitas*?**
At [vatican.va](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html). Reading the encyclical is not required for any course.

**Where do I read the rest of the social encyclicals the program cites?**
The [Readings page]({{ '/readings/' | relative_url }}) collects the primary texts (Leo XIII through Leo XIV) with links to the Vatican texts and to the standard scholarly editions of the Talmudic and Maimonidean sources. None of these readings is required for any course. They are the conceptual reading list for the student who wants to engage with the tradition on its own terms.

**Who do I email?**
For program-specific questions: Alexander Hoffarth at hoffarth@cua.edu. For admissions questions: CUA Columbus School of Law admissions office. For clinic application questions: the CUA Law clinical-program office. For externship questions: the CUA Law career and externship office.
